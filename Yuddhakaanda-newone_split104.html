Yuddha Kaanda - Sarga 104
In this Sarga, Rāvaṇa abuses his charioteer In this Sarga, Rāvaṇa abuses his charioteer, saying that he has insulted his valor and prowess by driving the chariot away while the enemy watched. Rāvaṇa even accuses him of being bribed by the enemy. The charioteer responds, explaining in all sincerity, why he did what he did, considering the circumstances and to provide respite for Rāvaṇa and the horses, both of whom were worn out heavily. Rāvaṇa, convinced and pleased with his explanation, instructs him to drive the chariot back to the battlefront and the charioteer does accordingly.
6.104.1
स तु मोहात् सुसङ्क्रुद्धः
कृतान्तबलचोदितः ।
क्रोधसंरक्तनयनो
रावणः सूतमब्रवीत् ॥
Rāvaṇa, gone out of his mind, his eyes turned blood-red in extreme rage, said to the charioteer, driven by his fate:
6.104.2
हीनवीर्यमिवाशक्तम्
पौरुषेण विवर्जितम् ।
भीरुं लघुमिवासत्त्वम्
विहीनमिव तेजसा ।
विमुक्तमिव मायाभिः
अस्त्रैरिव बहिष्कृतम् ।
मामवज्ञाय दुर्बुद्धे
स्वया बुद्ध्या विचेष्टसे ॥
You insult me by doing what you like, you rogue, as if I have lost my valor and power, given up my self-respect, became incapacitated, am scared like a weakling with no strength, am stripped of of all my magic arts and am deserted by all Astras!
6.104.4
किमर्थं मामवज्ञाय
मच्छन्दमनवेक्ष्य च ।
त्वया शत्रोः समक्षं मे
रथोऽयमपवाहितः ॥
Why have you, insulting me and not caring about my intent, driven away the chariot from the presence of the enemy?
6.104.5
त्वयाऽद्य हि ममानार्य
चिरकालसमार्जितम् ।
यशो वीर्यं च तेजश्च
प्रत्ययश्च विनाशितः ॥
You have destroyed in a trice, O ignoble creature, the sterling reputation and fame that I had earned over many years for my valor and power.
6.104.6
शत्रोः प्रख्यातवीर्यस्य
रञ्जनीयस्य विक्रमैः ।
पश्यतो युद्धलुब्धोऽहम्
कृतः कापुरुषस्त्वया ॥
I was made to appear like a coward by you, in the eyes of the enemy who is famous for his valor and who deserves to be entertained with my prowess!
6.104.7
यस्त्वं रथमिमं मोहात्
न चेद्वहसि दुर्मते ।
सत्योऽयं प्रतितर्को मे
परेण त्वमुपस्कृतः ॥
You must have brought this chariot here either because you are stupid or because, which I conclude to be the case, you have been bribed by the enemy.
6.104.8
न हि तद्विद्यते कर्म
सुहृदो हितकाङ्क्षिणः ।
रिपूणां सदृशं त्वेतत्
यत्त्वयैतदनुष्ठितम् ॥
What you did seems like something that an enemy would do; it does not at all seem like what a friend who wishes well would do.
6.104.9
निवर्तय रथं शीघ्रम्
यावन्नोपैति मे रिपुः ।
यदि वाऽध्युषितोऽसि त्वम्
स्मर्यते यदि मे गुणः ॥
Take the chariot back quickly (to the battlefield) before my enemy leaves, if you have truly known me for long and if you can remind yourself of what I prefer.
6.104.10
एवं परुषमुक्तस्तु
हितबुद्धिरबुद्धिना ।
अब्रवीद्रावणं सूतो
हितं सानुनयं वचः ॥
Thus spoken to harshly by Rāvaṇa who had lost his mind, the charioteer who wished him well said these words, intent on his welfare, in a very gentle manner:
6.104.11
न भीतोऽस्मि न मूढोऽस्मि
नोपजप्तोऽस्मि शत्रुभिः ।
न प्रमत्तो न निस्नेहो
विस्मृता न च सत्क्रिया ॥
I am neither afraid, nor dull-witted, nor have been bought by the enemies. I am being neither flippant, nor any less devoted (to you than before). I have not forgotten all the good things you have done to me.
6.104.12
मया तु हितकामेन
यशश्च परिरक्षता ।
स्नेहप्रस्कन्नमनसा
प्रियमित्यप्रियं कृतम् ॥
It is only because of my interest in your well being and to protect your reputation, and my heart filled completely with love, that I tried to please, but ended up displeasing you.
6.104.13
नास्मिन्नर्थे महाराज
त्वं मां प्रियहिते रतम् ।
कश्चिल्लघुरिवानार्यो
दोषतो गन्तुमर्हसि ॥
It does not behoove you, O great king, to find fault with me in this matter, as if I were some lowly ignoble creature.
6.104.14
श्रूयतां प्रतिदास्यामि
यन्निमित्तं मया रथः ।
नदीवेग इवाभोगे
संयुगे विनिवर्तितः ॥
Please listen, and let me give my response as to why I have driven the chariot away from the battlefield, with the speed of the waters of a river rushing down from a higher elevation.
6.104.15
श्रमं तवावगच्छामि
महता रणकर्मणा ।
न हि ते वीर्यसौमुख्यम्
प्रकर्षं वोपधारये ॥
I found you quite exhausted with the extraordinary efforts you have already put in, in the fight. I saw you neither asserting your valor nor winning over the enemy.
6.104.16
रथोद्वहनखिन्नाश्च
भग्ना मे रथवाजिनः ।
दीना धर्मपरिश्रान्ता
गावो वर्षहता इव ॥
The horses too were extremely worn out with their long toil. They looked weak and exhausted, pouring with sweat, like cows buffeted by a hailstorm.
6.104.17
निमित्तानि च भूयिष्ठम्
यानि प्रादुर्भवन्ति नः ।
तेषु तेष्वभिपन्नेषु
लक्षयाम्यप्रदक्षिणम् ॥
The more I analyzed the omens that showed up the more I found them boding ill for us.
6.104.18
देशकालौ च विज्ञेयौ
लक्षणानीङ्गीतानि च ।
दैन्यं खेदश्च हर्षश्च
रथिनश्च महाबल ।
स्थलनिम्नानि भूमेश्च
समानि विषमाणि च ।
युद्धकालश्च विज्ञेयः
परस्यान्तरदर्शनम् ।
उपयानापयाने च
स्थानं प्रत्यपसर्पणम् ।
सर्वमेतद्रथस्थेन
ज्ञेयं रथकुटुम्बिना ॥
O you of extraordinary strength! A charioteer who is as much part of the chariot itself, should be cognizant of everything, the circumstances, the disposition and intent of the charioter and also whether he is downcast, worried or cheerful, the elevations and depressions on the ground, and where it is level and where it is not, the right time for offense and the weak points of the enemy, and when the chariot should be stationed in one place, when it should be driven towards the enemy and when away from the enemy, and when it should be withdrawn completely.
6.104.21
तव विश्रमहेतोश्च
तथैषां रथवाजिनाम् ।
रौद्रं वर्जयता खेदम्
क्षमं कृतमिदं मया ॥
It is for the sake of providing a respite for you as well as for these horses from the hard and trying demands (of the fight) that I did what I thought was the right thing to do.
6.104.22
स्वेच्छया न मया वीर
रथोऽयमपवाहितः ।
भर्तृस्नेहपरीतेन
मयेदं यत्कृतं विभो ॥
I did not drive away the chariot willy-nilly, O valiant one! It is all done, my lord, with utmost love for you!
6.104.23
आज्ञापय यथातत्त्वम्
वक्ष्यस्यरिनिषूदन ।
तत्करिष्याम्यहं वीर
गतानृण्येन चेतसा ॥
Command me as to what I should do, O destroyer of the foe! I shall do it, O valiant one, with the spirit of freeing myself from the debt I owe you!
6.104.24
सन्तुष्टस्तेन वाक्येन
रावणस्तस्य सारथेः ।
प्रशस्यैनं बहुविधम्
युद्धलुब्धोऽब्रवीदिदम् ॥
Pleased by those words of his charioteer, Rāvaṇa, who lusted for battle, heaped praises upon him and said:
6.104.25
रथं शीघ्रमिमं सूत
राघवाभिमुखं कुरु ।
नाहत्वा समरे शत्रून्
निवर्तिष्यति रावणः ॥
Take the chariot quick, O charioteer, and place it in front of Rāghava. Rāvaṇa never turns back from the battlefield without slaying the foe.
6.104.26
एवमुक्त्वा ततस्तुष्टो
रावणो राक्षसेश्वरः ।
ददौ तस्मै शुभं ह्येकम्
हस्ताभरणमुत्तमम् ।
श्रुत्वा रावणवाक्यं तु
सारथिः सन्न्यवर्तत ॥
Having said this and pleased with him, Rāvaṇa, the King of the Rākshasas, gave him a splendid ornament for the hand. And the charioteer drove the chariot back following the instructions of Rāvaṇa.
6.104.27
ततो द्रुतं रावणवाक्यचोदितः
प्रचोदयामास हयान् स सारथिः ।
स राक्षसेन्द्रस्य ततो महारथः
क्षणेन रामस्य रथाग्रतो ऽभवत् ॥
The charioteer immediately drove the horses, following the instructions of Rāvaṇa and the great chariot of the king of Rākshasas was in front of Rāma's chariot in no time.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
युद्धकाण्डे चतुरुत्तरशततमः सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the one hundred and fourth Sarga in Yuddha Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
