Baala Kaanda - Sarga 24
The previous Sarga ended with "Viṡwāmitra entertained Rāma and Lakshmaṇa by telling fascinating stories." indeed, this Sarga starts on fulfilling that promise.
You will read the stories of river Sarayu merging into the river Gaṅgā, the story of the lands of Malada and Karūṡa, and the story of Tāṭaka in this Sarga.
If you have young children, please do make an effort to read these stories to them every week. It will have unbelievable impact on their imagination. It stimulates parts of their brains that are not touched by video games and cartoon shows.
Verbal stimulation evokes more imagination than visual stimulation, because verbal stimulation is suggestive whereas visual stimulation is limited by the concretized graphics. This is the reason why children often tell that 'the book is better than the movie', as in the case of, say, 'Harry Potter'.
1.24.1
ततः प्रभाते विमले
कृत्वाह्निकमरिन्दमौ ।
विश्वामित्रं पुरस्कृत्य
नद्यास्तीरमुपागतौ ॥
Then, on that clear morning, the subjugators of enemies (Rāma and Lakshmaṇa) went to the banks of the river led by Viṡwāmitra after completing the daily ablutions.
1.24.2
ते च सर्वे महात्मानो
मुनयः संशितव्रताः ।
उपस्थाप्य शुभां नावम्
विश्वामित्रमथाब्रुवन् ॥
The Mahātma Munis, who followed rigorous discipline, brought a fine boat for them, and said to Viṡwāmitra:
1.24.3
आरोहतु भवान्नावम्
राजपुत्रपुरस्कृतः ।
अरिष्टं गच्छ पन्थानम्
माभूत्कालविपर्ययः ॥
May you board the boat, with the princes ahead of you. Have a safe journey and may you continue on schedule!
1.24.4
विश्वामित्रस्तथेत्युक्त्वा
तानृषीनभिपूज्य च ।
ततार सहितस्ताभ्याम्
सरितं सागरङ्गमाम् ॥
Viṡwāmitra said "Surely", and took leave of them after paying due respects. Then he crossed the river that would meet the ocean, along with the duo.
1.24.5
ततः शुश्राव वै शब्दं
अतिसंरम्भवर्धितम् ।
मध्यमागम्य तोयस्य
सह रामः कनीयसा ॥
While they were crossing the river, Rāma and Lakshmaṇa heard a deafening sound fast approaching them.
1.24.6
अथ रामः सरिन्मध्ये
पप्रच्छ मुनिपुङ्गवम् ।
वारिणो भिद्यमानस्य
किमयं तुमुलो ध्वनिः ॥
Then, in the middle of the river, Rāma asked the eminent Muni: "What is this loud sound that seems to rip apart the waters!"
1.24.7
राघवस्य वचः श्रुत्वा
कौतूहलसमन्वितः ।
कथयामास धर्मात्मा
तस्य शब्दस्य निश्चयम् ॥
Hearing those curious words of Rāma, (Viṡwāmitra, ) the Dharmātma, told him about the sound.
1.24.8
कैलासपर्वते राम
मनसा निर्मितं सरः ।
ब्रह्मणा नरशार्दूल
तेनेदं मानसं सरः ॥
O Rāma! Tiger among Men! In the mountain range of Kailāsa, Brahma created a lake. It is known as Mānasa, because he created it by the will of his mind.
Mānasa means mind. The lake referred to here is popularly known as Mānasa Sarōvara (The lake from the mind).
1.24.9
तस्मात्सुस्राव सरसः
साऽयोध्यामुपगूहते ।
सरःप्रवृत्ता सरयूः
पुण्या ब्रह्मसरश्च्युता ॥
From that lake flows the river that surrounds Ayōdhyā. The river is known as Sarayu because it emerged from the Sarah (lake). It is considered sacred because it emerged out of the lake created by Brahma.
Coincidentally, I am translating this Sarga, as I am watching an episode of NASA TV's Nova program, on how the climate system of the entire planet is inter-connected and how the monsoons of India and the warm temperatures in US, during the same months, are related to each other.
Viṡwāmitra here, is performing the role of NASA TV for the young and curious brothers, Rāma and Lakshmaṇa.
1.24.10
तस्यायमतुलः शब्दो
जाह्नवीमभिवर्तते ।
वारिसङ्क्षोभजो राम
प्रणामं नियतः कुरु ॥
The loud sound that you hear is a result of the waters of that river rushing and merging with the waters of river Gaṅgā. Please pay obeisance with due reverence!
1.24.11
ताभ्यां तु तावुभौ कृत्वा
प्रणाममतिधार्मिकौ ।
तीरं दक्षिणमासाद्य
जग्मतुर्लघुविक्रमौ ॥
The duo of Dhārmikas paid due obeisance to the duo of rivers. And then they reached the southern side of the river and moved onwards with long strides.
1.24.12
स वनं घोरसङ्काशम्
दृष्ट्वा नृपवरात्मजः ।
अविप्रहतमैक्ष्वाकः
पप्रच्छ मुनिपुङ्गवम् ॥
Then they came across a virgin, creepy and dreadful forest and the princes of Ikshwāku dynasty asked the eminent Muni:
1.24.13
अहो वनमिदं दुर्गम्
झिल्लिकागणनादितम् ।
भैरवैः श्वापदैः पूर्णम्
शकुन्तैर्दारुणारुतैः ॥
O my! This forest is impenetrable! It is full of strident sounds of crickets, frightening sounds of Ṡakunta birds and shrill howls of the Ṡwāpadas.
Ṡwāpadas are a type of wild animals, which are also known as Ghatuka Mṛgas.
1.24.14
नानाप्रकारैः शकुनैः
वाश्यद्भिर्भैरवैः स्वनैः ।
सिंहव्याघ्रवराहैश्च
वारणैश्चोपशोभितम् ॥
It resounds with the scary and creepy sounds of different types of birds, lions, tigers, boars and elephants.
1.24.15
धवाश्वकर्णककुभैः
बिल्वतिन्दुकपाटलैः ।
सङ्कीर्णं बदरीभिश्च
किं न्वेतद्दारुणं वनम् ॥
It is full of Dhava, Aṡvakarṇa, Kakubha, Bilva, Tinduka, Pāṭala, and Badari trees, all entwined into an impenetrable mesh.
1.24.16
तमुवाच महातेजा
विश्वामित्रो महामुनिः ।
श्रूयतां वत्स काकुत्स्थ
यस्यैतद्दारुणं वनम् ॥
Then that radiant and great Muni told him: "My dear prince of Kākutstha! let me tell you about this dreadful forest!"
1.24.17
एतौ जनपदौ स्फीतौ
पूर्वमास्तां नरोत्तम ।
मलदाश्च करूशाश्च
देवनिर्माणनिर्मितौ ॥
O best among men! Once upon a time, there used to be two big residential lands (villages) called Malada and Karūṡa that were built by celestial architects.
A place of past glory is described here in the next few Ṡlōkas.
Stories like this evoke the imagination of children. It gives them a sense, and a way to relate to the glories of bygone times. A story like this conveys more than a complicated explanation or theory does.
Hope you are reading Rāmāyaṇa to your children every week!
1.24.18
पुरा वृत्रवधे राम
मलेन समभिप्लुतम् ।
क्षुधा चैव सहस्राक्षम्
ब्रह्महत्या समाविशत् ॥
O Rāma! When the Thousand-eyed killed Vṛtra, he was inflicted with the Pāpa for killing a Brāhmaṇa. Because of that, his body became full of contaminant toxins and he suffered from excessive and insatiable hunger.
It is very common to see many stories in the Purānas mentioning Indra getting into some sort of trouble all the time.
These are symbolic of the troubles that every administrator gets dragged into or slips into. As an administrator, one would be required to take timely action, knowing fully well that there will be fallouts that needs to be taken care of later. If the administrator has people on his side, they would certainly come to his aid, to rectify the fallouts.
In this story, Indra was inflicted with something like nuclear radiation. It was removed by the Munis and stored in Malada and Karūṡa, which were later compensated for.
1.24.19
तमिन्द्रं स्नापयन् देवा
ऋषयश्च तपोधनाः ।
कलशैः स्नापयामासुः
मलं चास्य प्रमोचयन् ॥
Then Dēvas and Ṛshis, enriched with Tapa, gave him a purifying bath with water from the Kalaṡas, ridding him of afflictions and toxins.
1.24.20
इह भूम्यां मलं दत्त्वा
दत्त्वा कारूशमेव च ।
शरीरजं महेन्द्रस्य
ततो हर्षं प्रपेदिरे ॥
After the toxins and hunger were removed from the body of Mahēndra and deposited in this land, they felt happy and relieved.
1.24.21
निर्मलो निष्करूशश्च
शुचिरिन्द्रो यदाऽभवत् ।
ददौ देशस्य सुप्रीतो
वरं प्रभुरनुत्तमम् ॥
Relieved of the hunger and toxins, and becoming clean, the pleased lord granted an unparalleled boon for this land.
1.24.22
इमौ जनपदौ स्फीतौ
ख्यातिं लोके गमिष्यतः ।
मलदाश्च करूशाश्च
ममाङ्गमलधारिणौ ॥
"Having taken my toxins and hunger, these lands will be known as Malada and Karūṡa. They will become rich, prosperous and popular."
Mala (part of Malada) means toxins. Karūṡa means hunger.
It takes a child's imagination to enjoy this Ṡlōka, and a child psychologist's insights to understand its significance.
1.24.23
साधु साध्विति तं देवाः
पाकशासनमब्रुवन् ।
देशस्य पूजां तां दृष्ट्वा
कृतां शक्रेण धीमता ॥
All the Dēvas commended the grand gesture of the discerning Ṡakra.
Ṡakra is another name for Indra.
1.24.24
एतौ जनपदौ स्फीतौ
दीर्घकालमरिन्दम ।
मलदाश्च करूशाश्च
मुदितौ धनधान्यतः ॥
O subjugator of foes! For a long time the vast lands of Malada and Karūṡa were prosperous and happy with granaries and treasuries full.
1.24.25
कस्यचित्त्वथ कालस्य
यक्षी वै कामरूपिणी ।
बलं नागसहस्रस्य
धारयन्ती तदा ह्यभूत् ।
ताटका नाम भद्रं ते
भार्या सुन्दस्य धीमतः ।
मारीचो राक्षसः पुत्रो
यस्याः शक्रपराक्रमः ॥
After a while there happened to be a Yakshiṇi by name Tāṭaka, who could change her form at will, possessing strength equivalent to a thousand elephants. She was the wife of the clever Sunda and she had a son by name Māreeca who was comparable to Ṡakra in prowess.
1.24.27
वृत्तबाहुर्महावीर्यो
विपुलास्यतनुर्महान् ।
राक्षसो भैरवाकारो
नित्यं त्रासयते प्रजाः ॥
He (Māreeca) had round arms, a big face and large body. He was mighty strong. His appearance was fearsome. He was scaring and frightening people.
1.24.28
इमौ जनपदौ नित्यम्
विनाशयति राघव ।
मलदांश्च करूशांश्च
ताटका दुष्टचारिणी ॥
O prince of Raghu Dynasty! Tāṭaka, the menace, used to destroy these two lands of Malada and Karūṡa.
1.24.29
सेयं पन्थानमावार्य
वसत्यत्यर्धयोजने ।
अत एव च गन्तव्यम्
ताटकाया वनं यतः ॥
She is blocking the way one and half Yōjanas from here. Hence people cannot enter this forest.
1.24.30
स्वबाहुबलमाश्रित्य
जहीमां दुष्टचारिणीम् ।
मन्नियोगादिमं देशम्
कुरु निष्कण्टकं पुनः ॥
I call upon you to kill this menacing Tāṭaka using all your strength, and free this land from troubles.
1.24.31
न हि कश्चिदिमं देशम्
शक्नोत्यागन्तुमीदृशम् ।
यक्षिण्या घोरया राम
उत्सादितमसह्यया ॥
O Rāma! Because of this dreaded Yakshiṇi, no one is able to go to this obliterated land.
1.24.32
एतत्ते सर्वमाख्यातम्
यथैतद्दारुणं वनम् ।
यक्ष्या चोत्सादितं सर्वम्
अद्यापि न निवर्तते ॥
Now you have heard the complete background of how the Yakshiṇi has destroyed this place and made it dreadful, with no signs of leaving it alone.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
बालकाण्डे चतुर्विंशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the twenty fourth Sarga in Bāla Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
