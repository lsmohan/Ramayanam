Yuddha Kaanda - Sarga 116
In this Sarga, Seetā feels terrible and miserable hearing the harsh words of Rāma. She swears that she always had Rāma in her heart over which she had control, even though her body might have been touched by the wicked one, over which she had no control.
She feels terrible that Rāma did not consider her character that he knew all too well. She asks Lakshmaṇa to arrange for a pyre for her, not wanting to live any more, after thus being abandoned by her husband in the midst of all the assembled crowds. Lakshmaṇa arranges the pyre, after realizing, from his gestures, that Rāma is neither going to change his mind nor is going to stop Seetā from entering the pyre.
Offering salutations to Agni, and praying to him to save her if her character is pure and is unlike what Rāghava suspects, she jumps into the pyre. All the women, Vānaras and Rākshasas cry.
6.116.1
एवमुक्ता तु वैदेही
परुषं रोमहर्षणम् ।
राघवेण सरोषेण
भृशं प्रव्यथिताऽभवत् ॥
Thus told by Rāghava in his wrath, words that were harsh and hair-raising, Vaidēhee was in extreme agony.
6.116.2
सा तदश्रुतपूर्वं हि
जने महति मैथिली । ।
श्रुत्वा भर्तृवचो घोरम्
लज्जयावनता भवत् ॥ ॥
Hearing those terrible words of her husband, the likes of which she had never heard before, spoken in the midst of a huge gathering of people, Maithili hung her head down in shame.
6.116.3
प्रविशन्तीव गात्राणि
स्वानि सा जनकात्मजा ।
वाक्शरैस्तैः सशल्येव
भृशमश्रूण्यवर्तयत् ॥
Seeming to implode into her own limbs, she, the daughter of Janaka, shed a flood of tears, as if she was filled with the shrapnels of those verbal darts.
6.116.4
ततो बाष्पपरिक्लिष्टम्
प्रमार्जन्ती स्वमाननम् ।
शनैर्गद्गदया वाचा
भर्तारमिदमब्रवीत् ॥
Wiping her face which was bedaubed with tears, she said these words slowly to her husband, her voice choking:
6.116.5
किं मामसदृशं वाक्यम्
ईदृशं श्रोत्रदारुणम् ।
रूक्षं श्रावयसे वीर
प्राकृतः प्राकृतामिव ॥
Why do you, O valiant one, say such cruel words that are so terrible to hear that should have never been spoken, like a common man to his womenfolk?
6.116.6
न तथाऽस्मि महाबाहो
यथा त्वमवगच्छसि ।
प्रत्ययं गच्छ मे स्येन
चारित्रेणैव ते शपे ॥
I am not like the one you think I am, O you of mighty arm! Trust me, I swear solemnly by my character!
6.116.7
पृथक्स्त्रीणां प्रचारेण
जातिं तां परिशङ्कसे ।
परित्यजेमां शङ्कां तु
यदि तेऽहं परीक्षिता ॥
You suspect all women, judging by the conduct of some ordinary women. Give up your suspicion, for you know me too well!
6.116.8
यद्यहं गात्रसंस्पर्शम्
गताऽस्मि विवशा प्रभो ।
कामकारो न मे तत्र
दैवं तत्रापराध्यति ॥
If I had touched some one else's body, it is not because I wanted to, but because I was helpless; it is the fault of Providence, not mine.
6.116.9
मदधीनं तु यत्तन्मे
हृदयं त्वयि वर्तते ।
पराधीनेषु गात्रेषु
किं करिष्याम्यनीश्वरी ॥
My heart, which is the only thing in my control, was firmly lodged in you. What can I, the powerless one, do about my body, which was in someone else's control?
6.116.10
सहसंवृद्धभावेन
संसर्गेण च मानद ।
यद्यहं ते न विज्ञाता
हता तेनास्मि शाश्वतम् ॥
O you, who uphold everyone's dignity! If you do not know me, in spite of living together for so long and interacting with each other, alas, I am completely undone, forever!
6.116.11
प्रेषितस्ते यदा वीरो
हनुमानवलोककः ।
लङ्कास्थाऽहं त्वया राजन्
किं तदा न विसर्जिता ॥
Why was I not abandoned right then, O King, when you sent Hanumān to search for me in Laṅkā?
6.116.12
प्रत्यक्षं वानरस्यास्य
तद्वाक्यसमनन्तरम् ।
त्वया सन्त्यक्तया वीर
त्यक्तं स्याज्जीवितं मया ॥
Having been abjured by you, O valiant one, I would have given up my life right then, if only I had heard that word directly from that Vānara!
6.116.13
न वृथा ते श्रमोऽयं स्यात्
संशये न्यस्य जीवितम् ।
सुहृज्जनपरिक्लेशो
न चायं विपुलस्तव ॥
You would not, then, have had this wasted effort, that put even your own life at risk; nor would there have been this extensive and exhausting efforts by your friends.
6.116.14
त्वया तु नरशार्दूल
क्रोधमेवानुवर्तता ।
लघुनेव मनुष्येण
स्त्रीत्वमेव पुरस्कृतम् ॥
O tiger among men, succumbing to anger like an ordinary man, you see me just as a woman.
6.116.15
अपदेशेन जनकात्
उत्पत्तिर्वसुधातलात् ।
मम वृत्तं च वृत्तज्ञ
बहु ते न पुरस्कृतम् ॥
You did not take into consideration my character in its entirety, which you know all too well. I may be known as Janaka's daughter to the world, but actually emerged directly from the earth itself.
Rāma takes pride in his descent and his abilities. Seetā, equally takes pride in her descent and her character.
Rāma is a symbol of the people who are powerful by nature. Seetā is a symbol of people who are weak by nature. In contemporary times, with the idea of 'equality' ruling the airwaves, we have lost appreciation for the natural differences among people, in other words, for diversity. Not everyone is created the same by Nature. Some are naturally weak, some are naturally strong.
Strong, or weak, we all seek self-respect, we take pride in what we stand for. We would rather give up our life, than give up what we stand for. This is the essence of 'Ijjat', a concept that runs throughout the Indian subcontinent, cutting across the lines of castes and religions, a phenomena studied by sociologists widely. Ijjat is what distinguishes the 'noble' from the 'ordinary' who are swayed by emotion. Lakshmaṇa, who gets easily swayed by emotion, is a symbol of 'ordinary', even though he is powerful. His devotion is to his leader Rāma, rather than to Dharma, much like that of the core vote banks of the politicians in modern times.
Rāma puts his life at risk to prove his abilities, which were challenged, i.e., to save his Ijjat. Seetā, in the same way, puts her life at risk to prove her character, i.e., to do the same, save her Ijjat. Others, the ordinary men, are aghast by the visual of a woman giving up her life by jumping into the fire. But she knows what she is doing, and Rāma knows what she is doing, too. For both of them, what they stand for is more important than their lives. Living even one more day, if it is not going to be glorious, is not an option for either of them. It is the same spirit with which a soldier fights on the battlefield, even today. By doing what she does here, Seetā reaches the heights that Rāma did risking his life to slay Rāvaṇa. And thus, they make a pair that we all admire.
6.116.16
न प्रमाणीकृतः पाणिः
बाल्ये मम निपीडितः ।
मम भक्तिश्च शीलं च
सर्वं ते पृष्ठतः कृतम् ॥
The hand of mine that you clasped when I was a young bride, seems to be of no account to you. My devotion, my character and everything else as well are relegated to the background by you.
6.116.17
एवं ब्रुवाणा रुदती
बाष्पगद्गदभाषिणी ।
अब्रवील्लक्ष्मणं सीता
दीनं ध्यानपरं स्थितम् ॥
Speaking thus and crying, her voice choked by tears, Seetā told Lakshmaṇa who stood there miserable and deep in thought:
6.116.18
चितां मे कुरु सौमित्रे
व्यसनस्यास्य भेषजम् ।
मिथ्योपघातोपहता
नाहं जीवितुमुत्सहे ॥
Raise a pyre, a remedy for this misery, for me, O Sowmitri! I do not want to live any more, hit by these false allegations!
6.116.19
अप्रीतेन गुणैर्भर्त्रा
त्यक्ता या जनसंसदि ।
या क्षमा मे गतिर्गन्तुम्
प्रवेक्ष्ये हव्यवाहनम् ॥
For one who is abandoned in the midst of the assembled crowds by a husband who is not happy with her virtues, this is the right thing to do; I will throw myself into the fire who carries the offerings to the Dēvas.
6.116.20
एवमुक्तस्तु वैदेह्या
लक्ष्मणः परवीरहा ।
अमर्षवशमापन्नो
राघवाननमैक्षत ॥
Thus told by Vaidēhee, Lakshmaṇa, destroyer of enemy warriors, was outraged and looked at Rāghava.
Why did Rāma not respond to the pleading of Seetā? Back in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa, when Seetā pleads with Rāma to take her along with him to the Vana, he tries to dissuade her, but ultimately agrees. In other words, there is a back and forth negotiation between the two then. Why not now? Because Seetā is in agreement that she had been touched by Rāvaṇa, though not through her volition. And Rāma has already said that, as the scion of a famed dynasty, he cannot take back a wife who has been touched by him. Seetā understands that and hence is not making a case against Rāma's objection. Seetā is making a case for consideration, given that Rāma knows her character too well. Well, Rāma trusts her too, but trust is not proof enough in the court of public and in the court of history.
Would not even the threat of her self-immolation be a sufficient reason for reconsideration? No, threatening to harm oneself to coerce others out of their will is not acceptable. Putting a gun to your head to coerce others is as bad as putting a gun to the other's head, if the objective is to coerce others to your will. This is why many Indians could not agree in principle with Gandhi's approach of 'fasting-unto-death'. In any case, Seetā is not threatening Rāma here with self-immolation. She just does not want to live for even one more day with an allegation, for which she has no tools to disprove, hanging over her head, and because of which she cannot go back to her husband, which is the only thing she wants. Anything short of that is not acceptable to her. What she wants is not acceptable to Rāma. Rāma made his decision. Seetā made her decision. Seetā dies, at least, gaining sympathy and freed from the misery. Rāma, on the other hand, has to live, known as a stone-hearted king, till time calls upon him. This much must have been evident to Rāma, Seetā and the crowd. Hence everyone could do nothing but go through those moments in agony, helplessly.
6.116.21
न हि रामं तदा कश्चित्
कालान्तकयमोपमम् ।
अनुनेतुमथो वक्तुं
द्रष्टुं वा प्यशकत्सुहृत् ॥
No one, even a good friend, dared try to persuade, or say a word or even look at Rāma, who was like Yama at the end of the Time.
6.116.22
स विज्ञाय मनश्छन्दम्
रामस्याकारसूचितम् ।
चितां चकार सौमित्रिः
मते रामस्य वीर्यवान् ॥
Understanding Rāma's mind based on his body language, Sowmitri prepared the pyre, with the consent of Rāma, the valiant one.
6.116.23
अधोमुखं तदा रामम्
शनैः कृत्वा प्रदक्षिणम् ।
उपासर्पत वैदेही
दीप्यमानं हुताशनम् ॥
Going slowly and with reverence around Rāma, who stood with his face down, Vaidēhee approached the blazing fire.
6.116.24
प्रणम्य देवताभ्यश्च
ब्राह्मणेभ्यश्च मैथिली ।
बद्धाञ्जलिपुटा चेदम्
उवाचाग्निसमीपतः ॥
Offering her salutations to the Dēvas and the Brāhmaṇas and with her palms joined in praying gesture, Maithili said, standing near the pyre:
6.116.25
यथा मे हृदयं नित्यम्
नापसर्पति राघवात् ।
तथा लोकस्य साक्षी माम्
सर्वतः पातु पावकः ॥
If my heart had never swayed away from Rāghava, then, may Agni, a witness to the entire world, protect me in every manner!
6.116.26
यथा मां शुद्धचारित्राम्
दुष्टां जानाति राघवः ।
तथा लोकस्य साक्षी माम्
सर्वतः पातु पावकः ॥
If my character is pure and is unlike what Rāghava suspects, then, may Agni, a witness to the entire world, protect me in every manner!
6.116.27
कर्मणा मनसा वाचा
यथा नातिचराम्यहम् ।
राघवं सर्वधर्मज्ञम्
तथा मां पातु पावकः ॥
If I have never been guilty of transgression in thought, word or deed against Rāghava, who knows all Dharma, then, may Agni protect me in every manner!
6.116.28
आदित्यो भगवान् वायुः
दिशश्चन्द्रस्तथैव च ।
अहश्चापि तथा सन्ध्ये
रात्रिश्च पृथिवी तथा ।
यथान्येऽपि विजानन्ति
तथा चारित्रसंयुताम् ॥
If the exalted Sun, the Vāyu, the Cardinals, the Day and the Night, and the Sandhyas, the Earth and Others know me to be of good character, (then, may Agni protect me in every manner)!
6.116.29
एवमुक्त्वा तु वैदेही
परिक्रम्य हुताशनम् ।
विवेश ज्वलनं दीप्तम्
निःशङ्केनान्तरात्मना ॥
Saying this and going around the fire in reverence, Vaidēhee entered the blazing fire with nothing bothering her any more.
6.116.30
जनः स सुमहांस्तत्र
बालवृद्धसमाकुलः ।
ददर्श मैथिलीं दीप्ताम्
प्रविशन्तीं हुताशनम् ॥
The huge crowd there, which also included a great number of young people and old people, witnessed Maithili entering the fire, radiant.
6.116.31
सा तप्तनवहेमाभा
तप्तकाञ्चनभूषणा ।
पपात ज्वलनं दीप्तम्
सर्वलोकस्य सन्निधौ ॥
She, glowing like gold purified in fire and adorned with ornaments made of gold purified in fire, jumped into that blazing fire, in the presence of all the people.
6.116.32
ददृशुस्तां विशालाक्षीम्
पतन्तीं हव्यवाहनम् ।
सीतां सर्वाणि रूपाणि
रुक्मवेदिनिभां तदा ॥
All the beings witnessed the wide-eyed Seetā, who had the glow of a golden Vēdi, fall into the pyre.
6.116.33
ददृशुस्तां महाभागाम्
प्रविशन्तीं हुताशनम् ।
सीतां कृत्स्नास्त्रयो लोकाः
पुण्यामाज्याहुतीमिव ॥
The entire three worlds watched Seetā, the highly blessed one, entering the fire as if she were like the sacred ghee offering.
6.116.34
प्रचुक्रुशुः स्त्रियः सर्वान्
तां दृष्ट्वा हव्यवाहने ।
पतन्तीं संस्कृतां मन्त्रैः
वसोर्धारामिवाध्वरे ॥
All the womenfolk wept aloud seeing her fall in the fire, like a stream of ghee sanctified with Mantras poured into the fire in a Yajña.
6.116.35
ददृशुस्तां त्रयो लोका
देवगन्धर्वदानवाः ।
शप्तां पतन्तीं निरये
त्रिदिवाद्देवतामिव ॥
All the inhabitants of the three worlds, Dēvas, Gandharvas and Dānavas, saw her falling, like a goddess under a curse falling from heaven into hell.
6.116.36
तस्यामग्निं विशन्त्यां तु
हाहेति विपुलस्वनः ।
रक्षसां वानराणां च
सम्बभूवाद्भुतोपमः ॥
As she thus fell into the flames, there broke out a great cry, ‘Alas, alas!’ from the Vānaras and the Rākshasas creating a spectacle, albeit an eerie one.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
युद्धकाण्डे षोडशोत्तरशततमः सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the one hundred and sixteenth Sarga in Yuddha Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
