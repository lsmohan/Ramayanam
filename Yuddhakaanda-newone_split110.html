Yuddha Kaanda - Sarga 110
In this Sarga, the Rākshasis wail over the death of Rāvaṇa, their lord. They lament resenting him abducting Seetā, not returning her to Rāma heeding the advice of Vibheeshaṇa, and thereby bringing destruction upon all the Rākshasas. In the end, they resign themselves to the fact that it is Providence that has had the ultimate say.
6.110.1
रावणं निहतं श्रुत्वा
राघवेण महात्मना ।
अन्तःपुराद्विनिष्पेतू
राक्षस्यः शोककर्शिताः ॥
Hearing that Rāvaṇa was slain by Mahātma Rāma, the grief-stricken Rākshasis rushed out of the inner apartments.
6.110.2
वार्यमाणाः सुबहुशो
वेष्टन्त्यः क्षितिपांसुषु ।
विमुक्तकेश्यो दुःखार्ता
गावो वत्सहता इव ॥
Despite the many attempts to hold them, they rolled in the dust on the grounds, their hair disheveled, and distraught with grief like cows that had lost their young.
6.110.3
उत्तरेण विनिष्क्रम्य
द्वारेण सह राक्षसैः ।
प्रविश्यायोधनं घोरम्
विचिन्वन्त्यो हतं पतिम् ।
राजपुत्रेति वादिन्यो
हा नाथेति च सर्वशः ।
परिपेतुः कबन्धाङ्काम्
महीं शोणितकर्दमाम् ॥
They came out, along with the Rākshasas, through the northern gate, and entered the dreadful battlefield that was mired in gore and had headless trunks for landmarks, searching for their lord who was slain, crying, ‘Ah, my prince, my lord!’
6.110.5
ता बाष्पपरिपूर्णाक्ष्यो
भर्तृशोकपराजिताः ।
करेण्व इव नर्दन्त्यो
विनेदुर्हतयूथपाः ॥
Their eyes filled with tears, completely lost in grief for their lord, they cried like she-elephants that had lost the lord of the herd.
6.110.6
ददृशुस्ता महाकायम्
महावीर्यं महाद्युतिम् ।
रावणं निहतं भूमौ
नीलाञ्जनचयोपमम् ॥
They then saw the huge body of Rāvaṇa of extraordinary valor and extraordinary splendor struck down and fallen on the ground, looking like a huge mound of collyrium.
6.110.7
ताः पतिं सहसा दृष्ट्वा
शयानं रणपांसुषु ।
निपेतुस्तस्य गात्रेषु
च्छिन्ना वनलता इव ॥
As soon as they saw their lord who was lying in the dust of the battlefield, they fell down on every limb of his, like creepers in the forest that were cut down.
6.110.8
बहुमानात् परिष्वज्य
काचिदेनं रुरोद ह ।
चरणौ काचिदालम्ब्य
काचित् कण्ठेऽवलम्ब्य च ॥
One wept, embracing him with deep regard, another holding his feet and yet another clinging to his neck.
6.110.9
उत्क्षिप्य च भुजौ काचित्
भूमौ सुपरिवर्तते ।
हतस्य वदनं दृष्ट्वा
काचिन्मोहमुपागमत् ॥
One had lifted his arms and rolled on the ground. Another fainted seeing the face of him who was slain.
6.110.10
काचिदङ्के शिरः कृत्वा
रुरोद मुखमीक्षती ।
स्नापयन्ती मुखं बाष्पैः
तुषारैरिव पङ्कजम् ॥
One put his head on her lap and cried looking at his face and drenching it with her tears, like dewdrops do a lotus.
6.110.11
एवमार्ताः पतिं दृष्ट्वा
रावणं निहतं भुवि ।
चुक्रुशुर्बहुधा शोकात्
भूयस्ताः पर्यदेवयन् ॥
Distressed seeing Rāvaṇa, their lord, struck down and fallen on the ground, they sobbed a lot in grief, lamenting:
6.110.12
येन वित्रासितः शक्रो
येन वित्रासितो यमः ।
येन वैश्रवणो राजा
पुष्पकेण वियोजितः ।
गन्धर्वाणामृषीणां च
सुराणां च महात्मनाम् ।
भयं येन महद्दत्तम्
सोऽयं शेते रणे हतः ॥
He, by whom Ṡakra and Yama were terrified, by whom Vaiṡravaṇa, the king, was stripped of his Pushpaka, by whom a great fear was instilled in Gandharvas, Ṛshis, and the mighty Suras, is now lying down in the battlefield, slain.
6.110.14
असुरेभ्यः सुरेभ्यो वा
पन्नगेभ्योऽपि वा तथा ।
न भयं यो विजानाति
तस्येदं मानुषाद्भयम् ॥
He, who knew no fear from Asuras, Suras and Pannagas, had to finally reckon with fear from a human!
6.110.15
अवध्यो देवतानां
यस्तथा दानवरक्षसाम् ।
हतः सोऽयं रणे शेते
मानुषेण पदातिना ॥
He who could not be killed by Dēvas, Dānavas or Rākshasas, is now lying on the battlefield, slain by a human who came on foot.
6.110.16
यो न शक्यः सुरैर्हन्तुम्
न यक्षैर्नासुरैस्तथा ।
सोऽयं कश्चिदिवासत्त्वो
मृत्युं मर्त्येन लम्भितः ॥
He, whom neither Suras, nor Yakshas, nor Asuras could slay, was done to death by a mere mortal, that too, one in exile, as if he was a powerless weakling.
6.110.17
एवं वदन्त्यो बहुधा
रुरुदुस्तस्य ताः स्त्रियः ।
भूय एव च दुःखार्ता
विलेपुश्च पुनःपुनः ॥
Saying many such things, the women cried. Distressed by bouts of grief, they lamented again and again.
6.110.18
अशृण्वता च सुहृदाम्
सततं हितवादिनाम् ।
मरणायाहृता सीता
राक्षसाश्च निपातिताः ।
एताः सममिदानीं ते
वयमात्मा च पातिताः ॥
You would not listen to friends who always spoke for your wellbeing. You abducted Seetā inviting your own death, and the Rākshasas were also struck down. Now, all of us too, are destroyed, along with you.
6.110.19
ब्रुवाणोऽपि हितं वाक्यम्
इष्टो भ्राता विबीषणः ।
धृष्टं परुषितो मोहात्
त्वयाऽऽत्मवधकाङ्क्षिणा ॥
Your dear brother Vibheeshaṇa who spoke in your best interest was rebuked with the harshest of words by you, as you seemed to seek your own death out of delusion.
6.110.20
यदि निर्यातिता ते स्यात्
सीता रामाय मैथिली ।
न नः स्याद् व्यसनं घोरम्
इदं मूलहरं महत् ।
वृत्तकामो भवेद्भ्राता
रामो मित्रकुलं भवेत् ।
वयं चाविधवाः सर्वाः
सकामा न च शत्रवः ॥
If only your brother was heard and you had returned Seetā, the princess of Mithilā, to Rāma, this dreaded tragedy, which has struck at the root, would not have befallen us; we would all have not become widows, your enemies would not have had their wish fulfilled, and Rāma would have become one of your friends.
6.110.22
त्वया पुनर्नृशंसेन
सीतां संरुन्धता बलात् ।
राक्षसा वयमात्मा च
त्रयं तुल्यं निपातितम् ॥
In your cruelty and with your force, you kept Seetā in captivity, and now the three, the Rākshasas, us, and your own self, are all equally destroyed.
6.110.23
न कामकारः कामं वा
तव राक्षसपुङ्गव ।
दैवं चेष्टयते सर्वम्
हतं दैवेन हन्यते ॥
O bull among Rākshasas! May be, you did not do it with your own free will, for destiny drives it all and one gets killed by Providence alone.
6.110.24
वानराणां विनाशोऽयम्
रक्षसानां महाहवे ।
तव चैव महाबाहो
दैवयोगादुपागतः ॥
This destruction of Vānaras, Rākshasas and yourself in this great battle, O you of mighty arm, is the act of Providence.
6.110.25
नैवार्थेन न कामेन
विक्रमेण न चाज्ञया ।
शक्या दैवगतिर्लोके
निवर्तयितुमुद्यता ॥
Neither by making a wish, nor by using wealth, neither by showing prowess, nor by decrying it, can the course willed by Providence be changed.
6.110.26
विलेपुरेवं दीनास्ताः
राक्षसाधिपयोषितः ।
कुरर्य इव दुःखार्ता
बाष्पपर्याकुलेक्षणाः ॥
Thus they mourned, those poor women of the lord of Rākshasas, like grief-stricken ospreys, their eyes filled with tears.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
युद्धकाण्डे दशोत्तरशततमः सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the one hundred and tenth Sarga in Yuddha Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
