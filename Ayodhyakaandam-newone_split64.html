Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 64
In this Sarga, Daṡaratha proceeds to the Āṡrama of the aged parents of the Muni whom he killed accidentally. The blind parents think that it is their son who has come and asks him for the reason for the delay.
Daṡaratha tells him about the horrible mistake he has done. The parents cry and ask him to take them to their son. They cry pitifully, falling on the body of their son and give up their lives on their son's funeral pyre, after cursing Daṡaratha also to die, crying for his own son.
After narrating to Kousalyā that recollection of killing of the son of Muni, the king laments about what he has done to Rāma, and breathes his last, past midnight.
2.64.1
वधमप्रतिरूपं तु
माहर्षेस्तस्य राघवः ।
विलपन्नेव धर्मात्मा
कौसल्यां पुनरब्रवीत् ॥
Lamenting on that unprecedented kind of killing of the Maharshi, Rāghava, the Dharmātma said again to Kousalyā:
2.64.2
तदज्ञानान्महत्पापम्
कृत्वाहं सङ्कुलेन्द्रियः ।
एकस्त्वचिन्तयं बुध्या
कथं नु सुकृतं भवेत् ॥
With that sinful act committed out of ignorance, all my senses were shaken. I, alone by myself, wondered what the right course of action, thereafter, would be!
It should be everyone's experience to have committed a few mistakes in childhood/youth, out of sheer ignorance, that could have caused considerable damage.
One would, then, feeling embarrassed to disclose the mistake to anyone, try to think of the next step, all by oneself. Vālmeeki captures that juvenile behavior here.
2.64.3
ततस्तं घटमादाय
पूर्णं परमवारिणा ।
आश्रमं तमहं प्राप्य
यथाऽख्यातपथं गतः ॥
Then I filled the pot with the best of the waters, and took the foot track, as I was told, and reached the Āṡrama.
2.64.4
तत्राहं दुर्बलावन्धौ
वृद्धावपरिणायकौ ।
अपश्यं तस्य पितरौ
लूनपक्षाविव द्विजौ ।
तन्निमित्ताभिरासीनौ
कथाभिरपरिश्रमौ ।
तामाशां मत्कृते हीनै
उदासीनावनाथवत् ॥
There I saw his weak, blind and aged parents, who looked like birds with their wings severed, who seemed forlorn and indifferent, who were talking about their son, who had no one to lead them now and whose hopes were lost because of me!
2.64.6
शोकोपहतचित्तश्च
भयसन्त्रस्तचेतनः ।
तच्चाऽश्रमपदं गत्वा
भूयश्शोकमहं गतः ॥
With my mind distressed with grief, and my heart palpitating with fear, I went to that Āṡrama. And once again, I plunged into grief.
2.64.7
पदशब्दं तु मे श्रुत्वा
मुनिर्वाक्यमभाषत ॥
Hearing the sound of my footsteps, the Muni said:
2.64.7
किं चिरायसि मे पुत्र
पानीयं क्षिप्रमानय ।
यन्निमित्तमिदं तात
सलिले क्रीडितं त्वया ।
उत्कण्ठिता ते मातेयम्
प्रविश क्षिप्रमाश्रमम् ॥
O son! why have you delayed so long? Please give me the water quick. My dear! Were you playing in the water? Your mother is agitated! Come inside the Āṡrama quick!
2.64.9
यद्व्यलीकं कृतं पुत्र
मात्रा ते यदि वा मया ।
न तन्मनसि कर्तव्यम्
त्वया तात तपस्विना ॥
Did your mother or I do anything wrong? May you, the Tapasvi, not take it to your heart!
2.64.10
त्वं गतिस्त्वगतीनां च
चक्षुस्त्वं हीनचक्षुषाम् ।
समासक्तास्त्वयि प्राणाः
किं त्वं नो नाभिभाषसे ॥
You are the recourse for us who are without any other recourse. You are the eyes for us, who have no eyes. Our lives are anchored in yours. Why are you not speaking?
2.64.11
मुनिमव्यक्तया वाचा
तमहं सज्जमानया ।
हीनव्यञ्जनया प्रेक्ष्य
भीतचित्त इवाब्रुवम् ॥
I took a look at that Muni. My mind was taken over by fear. I told him in blurred and unclear words, with all the consonants dropped from them:
2.64.12
मनसः कर्म चेष्टाभिः
अभिसंस्तभ्य वाग्बलम् ।
आचचक्षे त्वहं तस्मै
पुत्रव्यसनजं भयम् ॥
Managing my mind, faculties, and the strength in my voice by an effort of will, I acquainted him with the frightful incident that happened to his son.
2.64.13
क्षत्रियोऽहं दशरथो
नाहं पुत्रो महात्मनः ।
सज्जनावमतं दुःखम्
इदं प्राप्तं स्वकर्मजम् ॥
I am a Kshatriya. My name is Daṡaratha. O Mahātma! I am not your son. This sorrow befell entirely out of my own deeds that would be condemnable by any sane person.
2.64.14
भगवंश्चापहस्तोऽहम्
सरयूतीरमागतः ।
जिघांसुश्श्वापदं कञ्चिन्
निपाने चाऽगतं गजम् ॥
O Bhagawan! I came to the banks of river Sarayu with bow in my hands, intending to kill any beast of prey or elephant that might come there to drink water.
2.64.15
ततश्श्रुतो मया शब्दो
जले कुम्भस्य पूर्यतः ।
द्विपोऽयमिति मत्वाऽयम्
बाणेनाभिहतो मया ॥
Then I heard the sound of a pot being filled with water. I mistook it to be the sound of an elephant and shot it with my arrow.
2.64.16
गत्वा नद्यास्तत स्तीरम्
अपश्यमिषुणा हृदि ।
विनिर्भिन्नं गतप्राणम्
शयानं भुवि तापसम् ॥
Then I went to the bank of the river and saw a Muni, his heart ripped by the arrow, lying all but dead on the ground.
2.64.17
भगवंच्छब्दमालक्ष्य
मया गजजिघांसुना ।
विसृप्टोऽम्भसि नाराचः
तेन ते निहतस्सुतः ॥
O Bhagawan! I wanted to aim at the sound to kill the elephant, and shot the arrow towards the waters. But your son was killed, instead!
2.64.18
ततस्तस्यैव वचनात्
उपेत्य परितप्यतः ।
स मया सहसा बाण
उधृतो मर्मतस्तदा ॥
I then approached him and pulled the arrow from his vitals, as he, suffering severely, requested me to!
2.64.19
स चोधृतेन बाणेन
तत्रैव स्वर्गमास्थितः ।
भवन्तौ पितरौ शोचन्
अन्धाविति विलप्य च ॥
He left instantaneously to Heaven when the arrow was pulled out, grieving and lamenting for you, his parents, who are blind!
2.64.20
अज्ञानाद्भवतः पुत्रः
सहसाऽभिहतो मया ।
शेषमेवं गते यत्स्यात्
तत्प्रसीदतु मे मुनिः ॥
Your son was killed by me instantaneously, by mistake. For whatever has happened and whatever is left to be done henceforth, may the Muni have pity on me!
2.64.21
स तच्च्रुत्वा वचः क्रूरम्
मयोक्तमघशंसिना ।
नाशकत्तीव्रमायासम्
अकर्तुं भगवानृषिः ॥
Hearing me speak those wretched and sad words, that Ṛshi, the Bhagawān, could not help but give in to grief.
2.64.22
स बाष्पपूर्णवदनो
निश्श्वसन्शोककर्शितः ।
मामुवाच महातेजाः
कृताञ्जलिमुपस्थितम् ॥
That supremely radiant Muni, with his face filled with tears, heaving sighs and tormented by grief, said to me as I stood by with palms joined:
2.64.23
यद्येतदशुभं कर्म
न त्वं मे कथयेस्स्वयम् ।
फलेन्मूर्धास्म ते राजन्
सद्यश्शतसहस्रधा ॥
O King! Had you not told me about this terrible act on your own, your head would have shattered into hundreds of thousands of pieces immediately.
2.64.24
क्षत्रियेण वधो राजन्
वानप्रस्थे विशेषतः ।
ज्ञानपूर्वं कृतस्स्थानात्
च्यावयेदपि वज्रिणम् ॥
Wanton killing by a Kshatriya, of a Vana-dwelling anchorite especially, would topple even the wielder of the thunderbolt (Indra) from his place.
the wielder of the thunderbolt (Indra)
2.64.25
सप्तधा तु फलेन्मूर्धा
मुनौ तपसि तिष्ठति ।
ज्ञानाद्विसृजतश्शस्त्रम्
तादृशे ब्रह्मचारिणि ॥
Whoever shoots an arrow knowingly on a Muni, who is sitting in Tapa, or anyone similarly involved in matters of Brahman, will find his head split into seven pieces.
2.64.26
अज्ञानाद्धिकृतं यस्मात्
इदं तेनैव जीवसि ।
अपि ह्यद्य कुलं न स्यात्
इक्ष्वाकूणां कुतो भवान् ॥
You are still alive only because you have done it out of ignorance. If not, the entire race of Ikshwākus would have been wiped out; where would you be then!
2.64.27
नय नौ नृप तं देशम्
इति मां चाभ्यभाषत ।
अद्य तं द्रष्टुमिच्छावः
पुत्रं पश्चिमदर्शनम् ।
रुधिरेणावसिक्ताङ्गम्
प्रकीर्णाजिनवाससम् ।
शयानं भुवि निस्संज्ञम्
धर्म राजवशं गतम् ॥
Then he said to me: Lead both of us, O King, to that place. We would like to have a last look at him, our son, whose body is covered in blood, whose deer skin garb is in disarray, who lies on the ground senseless, and who has passed into the keeping of Yama!
2.64.29
आथाहमेकस्तं देशम्
नीत्वा तौ भृशदुःखितौ ।
अस्पर्शयमहं पुत्रम्
तं मुनिं सह भार्यया ॥
Then, all by myself, I took both of them including his wife, who were overwhelmed by grief, to that place and made them touch their son.
2.64.30
तौ पुत्रमात्मनस्स्पृष्ट्वा
तमासाद्य तपस्विनौ ।
निपेततुश्शरीरेऽस्य
पिता चास्येदमब्रवीत् ॥
Then those two Tapasvis approached their son, touched him and fell on his body. Then the father said these words:
2.64.31
नाभिवादयसे माद्य
न च माऽमभिभाषसे ।
किं नु शेषे तु भूमौ त्वम्
वत्स किं कुपितो ह्यसि ॥
My dear! You do not greet me today! You are not speaking lovingly! Are you angry with us? Why are you sleeping here on the ground?
2.64.32
न त्वहं ते प्रियं पुत्र
मातरं पश्य धार्मिक ।
किं नु नालिङ्गसे पुत्र
सुकुमार वचो वद ॥
My son, do you not like me? Then, look at your mother, O Dhārmika! Why won't you give me a hug! O gentle one, say something!
2.64.33
कस्य वाऽपररात्रेऽहम्
श्रोष्यामि हृदयङ्गमम् ।
अधीयानस्य मधुरम्
शास्त्रं वान्यद्विशेषतः ॥
Whose sweet and lovely voice will I hear in the early hours, reciting Ṡastras and other exquisite texts?
2.64.34
को मां सन्द्यामुपास्यैव
स्नात्वा हुतहुताशनः ।
श्लाघयिष्यत्युपासीनः
पुत्र शोकभयार्दितम् ॥
Who would speak to me as I take bath, pay salutations to Sandhya, complete fire rituals and sit nervously, distressed by grief?
2.64.35
कन्दमूलफलं हृत्वा
को मां प्रियमिवातिथिम् ।
भोजयिष्यत्यकर्मण्यम्
अप्रग्रहमनायकम् ॥
Who will gather roots and fruits and feed me, the one who cannot work, who cannot earn and who has no guide, like the most welcome guest?
2.64.36
इमामन्धां च वृद्धां च
मातरं ते तपस्विनीम् ।
कथं वत्स भरिष्यामि
कृपणां पुत्रगर्धिनीम् ॥
My dear! How can I offer succor to your blind and aged mother, the pitiful Tapasvini, who yearns for her son?
2.64.37
तिष्ठ मा मा गमः पुत्र
यमस्य सदनं प्रति ।
श्वो मया सह गन्तासि
जनन्या च समेधितः ॥
My son! Hold on! Do not, please do not leave for the abode of Yama! You could go tomorrow, along with me and your mother!
2.64.38
उभावपि च शोकार्तौ
अनाथौ कृपणौ वने ।
क्षिप्रमेव गमिष्यावः
त्वया हीनौ यमक्षयम् ॥
Both of us also, tormented by grief, and pitiful in this Vana without you, shall go very soon to the abode of Yama!
2.64.39
ततो वैवस्वतं दृष्ट्वा
तं प्रवक्ष्यामि भारतीम् ।
क्षमतां धर्मराजो मे
बिभृयात्पितरावयम् ॥
When I see Yama, the Dharmarāja, the son of Vivaswan, I will plead: "May I be condoned! May he be allowed to support his parents!"
2.64.40
दातुमर्हति धर्मात्मा
लोकपालो महायशाः ।
ईदृशस्य ममाक्षय्या
मेकामभयदक्षिणाम् ॥
It befits the highly renowned Dharmātma, the lord of the worlds, to grant me this one comforting boon, that means everything to me.
2.64.41
अपापोऽसि यदा पुत्र
निहतः पापकर्मणा ।
तेन सत्येन गच्छाशु
ये लोकाश्शस्त्रयोधिनाम् ॥
My son! You are a sinless one, killed by this sinful soul! Hence, you shall soon attain the worlds that are the privilege of weapon-wielding warriors!
2.64.42
यान्ति शूरा गतिं यां च
सङ्ग्रामेष्वनिवर्तिनः ।
हतास्त्वभिमुखाः पुत्र
गतिं तां परमां व्रज ॥
My son! You shall attain the highest of the worlds, that would only be attained by heroes who face their enemies in a war without ever backing off!
2.64.43
यां गतिं सगरश्शैब्यो
दिलीपो जनमेजयः ।
नहुषो दुन्धुमारश्च
प्राप्तास्तां गच्छ पुत्रक ॥
My son! You shall attain the same high worlds that Sagara, Ṡaibya, Dileepa, Janamējaya, Nahusha and Dundhumāra attained.
2.64.44
या गति स्सर्वसाधूनाम्
स्वाध्यायात्तपसाच या ।
या भूमिदस्याहिऽताग्नेः
एकपत्नी व्रतस्य च ।
गोसहस्रप्रदातऽणाम्
या या गुरुभृतामपि ।
देहन्यासकृतां या च
तां गतिं गच्छ पुत्रक ॥
You shall go where all good men go and where the study of the Vedas and Tapa lead. You shall attain the same high worlds that await him who has given gifts of land, who has regularly performed fire rituals or who has stayed monogamous all his life. You shall be with those who have given away thousands of cows, taken good care of the elders or who have given up their bodies willingly.
2.64.46
न हि त्वस्मिन्कुले जातो
गच्छत्यकुशलां गतिम् ।
स तु यास्यति येन त्वम्
निहतो मम बान्धवः ॥
No one born in our lineage would ever attain an unworthy world. But the one who killed you, my relation, certainly will.
2.64.47
एवं स कृपणं तत्र
पर्यदेवयतासकृत् ।
ततोऽस्मै कर्तुमुदकम्
प्रवृत्तस्सहभार्यया ॥
Thus he lamented pitiably, repeatedly. And then, along with his wife, he set about offering water-libations.
2.64.48
स तु दिव्येन रूपेण
मुनिपुत्रस्स्वकर्मभिः ।
स्वर्गमध्यारुहत्क्षिप्रम्
शक्रेण सह धर्मवित् ॥
Soon that son of the Muni, who knew Dharma, by virtue of his own good deeds, ascended to Heaven taking a divine body, led by Ṡakra.
2.64.49
आबभाषे च वृद्धौ तौ
सह शक्रेण तापसः ।
आश्वास्यच मुहूर्तं तु
पितरौ वाक्यमब्रवीत् ॥
He and Ṡakra spoke words of solace to those old people. Thereafter he told his parents:
2.64.50
स्थानमस्मि महत्प्राप्तो
भवतोः परिचारणात् ।
भवन्तावपि च क्षिप्रम्
मम मूलमुपैष्यतः ॥
By virtue of serving you two, I have attained an exalted state. You too shall join me before long.
2.64.51
एवमुक्त्वा तु दिव्येन
विमानेन वपुष्मता ।
आरुरोह दिवं क्षिप्रम्
मुनिपुत्रो जितेन्द्रियः ॥
Having said this, the Muni's son, who had mastered his senses, ascended to Heaven immediately in a splendid celestial car.
2.64.52
स कृत्वा तूदकं तूर्णम्
तापसस्सह भार्यया ।
मामुवाच महातेजाः
कृताञ्जलिमुपस्थितम् ॥
Soon after completing the offering of water-libations along with his wife, that supremely radiant Muni said to me, as I was waiting upon him with palms joined:
2.64.53
अद्यैव जहिं मां राजन्
मरणे नास्ति मे व्यथा ।
यच्छरेणैकपुत्रं माम्
त्वमकर्षीरपुत्रकम् ॥
I had only one son. With your arrow, you made me sonless. Kill me also right away, O King; I do not worry about dying.
2.64.54
त्वया तु यदविज्ञानात्
निहतो मे सुतश्शुचिः ।
तेन त्वामभिशप्स्यामि
सुदुःखमतिदारुणम् ॥
For killing my son, who was blameless, albeit by ignorance, I shall curse you to endure a terrible grief.
2.64.55
पुत्रव्यसनजं दुःखम्
यदेतन्मम साम्प्रतम् ।
एवं त्वं पुत्रशोकेन
राजन्कालं करिष्यसि ॥
Just as I suffer now, on account of my son, you shall also suffer, O King, on account of your son, and die.
2.64.56
अज्ञानात्तु हतो यस्मात्
क्षत्रियेण त्वया मुनिः ।
तस्मात्त्वां नाविशत्याशु
ब्रह्महत्या नराधिप ॥
O lord of the people! However, you will not suffer the sin of killing a Brāhmaṇa, although you killed a Muni, being a Kshatriya, since you did so unknowingly.
2.64.57
त्वामप्येतादृशो भावः
क्षिप्रमेव गमिष्यति ।
जीवितान्तकरो घोरो
दातारमिव दक्षिणा ॥
But a similar dreadful misery (as we experience) which will put an end to your life shall as quickly find you, as the fruit of the honorarium given by a donor shall accrue to him.
2.64.58
एवं शापं मयि न्यस्य
विलप्य करुणं बहु ।
चितामारोप्य देहं तत्
मिथुनं स्वर्गमभ्ययात् ॥
Having thus cursed me and lamented long, that couple ascended the funeral pyre and went to Heaven.
Not only wives, but anyone who loses someone whom they dearly love, without whom they find life to be meaningless, seem to give up their lives on the funeral pyre of their loved ones, in times gone by.
2.64.59
तदेतच्छिन्तयानेन
स्मृतं पापं मया स्वयम् ।
तदा बाल्यात्कृतं देवि
शब्दवेध्यनुशिक्षिणा ॥
O Queen! As I brood on my plight, I now recall the sin that I committed in youthful folly, while practicing the art of Ṡabdavēdhi.
2.64.60
तस्यायं कर्मणो देवि
विपाकस्समुपस्थितः ।
अपथ्यैस्सहसंभुक्ते
व्याधिरन्नरसे यथा ॥
The inevitable consequence of that act has now come upon me, like illness that comes as a consequence of eating bad foods.
2.64.61
तस्मान्मामागतं भद्रे
तस्योदारस्य तद्वचः ।
यदहं पुत्रशोकेन
सन्त्यक्ष्याम्यद्य जीवितम् ।
चक्षुभ्यां त्वां न पश्यामि
कौसल्ये साधु मां स्फृश ॥
Those words of doom uttered by that man of power have found me out, O auspicious one, and I shall die today, grieving for my son. My eyes are failing; I am not able to see you! O Kousalyā! Please lay your hand assuringly on me!
2.64.62
इत्युक्त्वा स रुदंस्त्रस्तो
भार्यामाह च भूमिपः ॥
Saying thus, the lord of the land cried with fear and then again said to his wife:
2.64.63
एतन्मे सदृशं देवि
यन्मया राघवे कृतम् ।
सदृशं तत्तु तस्यैव
यदनेन कृतं मयि ॥
Whatever I did to Rāghava only befits (the wretched) me. Whatever he did in response only befits him.
2.64.64
दुर्वृत्तमपि कः पुत्रम्
त्यजेद्भुवि विचक्षणः ।
कश्च प्रव्राज्यमानो वा
नासूयेत्पितरं सुतः ॥
Would there be any sensible man in this world who abandons his own son, even if he were given to evil ways? And would there be a son who does not get outraged about his father, when ordered to go in exile?
Daṡaratha was, indeed, pitiably caught between a rock and hard place. He either had to forgo his dearest son and exile him for no fault of his, or have to renege on the boons he had given to Kaikēyee and default on his word.
2.64.65
यदि मां संस्पृशेद्रामः
सकृदद्य लभेत वा ।
यमक्षयमनुप्राप्ता
द्रक्ष्यन्ति न हि मानवाः ॥
If only Rāma could just touch me once now, or at least come near me! For, no one, after going to the abode of Yama, can see (their loved ones)!
2.64.66
चक्षुषा त्वां न पश्यामि
स्मृतिर्मम विलुप्यते ।
दूता वैवस्वतस्यैते
कौसल्ये त्वरयन्ति माम् ॥
I am unable to see you with my eyes. My memory is failing. The envoys of the son of Vivaswan, O Kousalyā, are hastening me up!
2.64.67
अतस्तु किं दुःखतरम्
यदहं जीवितक्षये ।
न हि पश्यामि धर्मज्ञम्
रामं सत्यपराक्रमम् ॥
In these last moments, what else could be of greater grief, than not being able to see Rāma, the one who knows Dharma and one who is of true valor!
2.64.68
तस्यादर्शनजश्शोकः
सुतस्याप्रतिकर्मणः ।
उच्छोषयति मे प्राणान्
वारिस्तोकमिवातपः ॥
This grief, born out of not seeing my son who has done the incredible, is drying up my life, like the sun would, the shallow waters.
2.64.69
न ते मनुष्या देवास्ते
ये चारुशुभकुण्डलम् ।
मुखं द्रक्ष्यन्ति रामस्य
वर्षे पञ्चदशे पुनः ॥
Those who will see again, fifteen years from now, that lovely face of Rāma with his charming ear-pendants, are indeed not ordinary human beings, but Dēvas.
2.64.70
पद्मपत्रेक्षणं सुभ्रु
सुदंष्ट्रं चारुनासिकम् ।
धन्या द्रक्ष्यन्ति रामस्य
ताराधिपनिभं मुखम् ॥
Blessed are they who will see Rāma's moon-like face with the lotus-petal eyes, the beautiful eyebrows, fine teeth and winsome nose.
2.64.71
सदृशं शारदस्येन्दोः
फुल्लस्य कमलस्य च ।
सुगन्धि मम नाथस्य
धन्या द्रक्ष्यन्ति तन्मुखम् ॥
Blessed are those who will behold the fragrant face of my beloved lord Rāma, that resembles a post-monsoon moon and a fully bloomed lotus.
2.64.72
निवृत्तवनवासं तम्
अयोध्यां पुनरागतम् ।
द्रक्ष्यन्ति सुखिनो रामम्
शुक्रं मार्गगतं यथा ॥
Happy will they be who see him return to Ayōdhyā from the life in the forest, like Venus rising in the sky.
2.64.73
कौसल्ये चित्तमोहेन
हृदयं सीदतीव मे ।
वेदये न च संयुक्तान्
शब्दस्पर्शरसानहम् ॥
Kousalyā, with my mind disoriented, my heart is sinking. I am unable to feel either sound or touch or flavor, even from very near.
2.64.74
चित्तनाशाद्विपद्यन्ते
सर्वाण्येवेन्द्रियाणि मे ।
क्षीणस्नेहस्य दीपस्य
संसक्ता रश्मयो यथा ॥
All my senses are dying down, with the failing of mind, like the light of a lamp does when the oil is used up.
2.64.75
अयमात्मभवश्शोको
मामनाथमचेतनम् ।
संसादयति वेगेन
यथा कूलं नदीरयः ॥
I have become forlorn and my senses are numbed. This grief that springs from within me is fast destroying me like the swift waters of a river does, its banks. 
2.64.76
हा राघव महाबाहो
हा ममाऽयासनाशन ।
हा पितृप्रिय मे नाथ
हाऽद्य क्वासि गतस्सुत ॥
O Rāghava! O mighty-armed! Only you could dispel my grief! O beloved of your father! O my lord! O my son! Where have you gone today!
2.64.77
हा कौसल्ये नशिष्यामि
हा सुमित्रे तपस्विनि ।
हा नृशंसे ममामित्रे
कैकेयि कुलपांसनि ॥
Ah Kousalyā! Ah Sumitrā, Tapasvini! Ah Kaikēyee, cruel wretch, my foe, who have dragged the name of the family into dust! I am finished!
2.64.78
इति रामस्य मातुश्च
सुमित्रायाश्च सन्निधौ ।
राजा दशरथश्शोचन्
जीवितान्तमुपागमत् ॥
Lamenting thus in the presence of the mother of Rāma and Sumitrā, king Daṡaratha met with his end of life.
2.64.79
यदा तु दीनं कथयन्नराधिपः
प्रियस्य पुत्त्रस्य विवासनातुरः ।
गतेऽर्धरात्रे भृशदुःखपीडित-
स्तदा जहौ प्राणमुदारदर्शनः ॥
Thus, the magnanimous King, saying these words pitifully, distressed at the banishment of his beloved son, gave up his life tormented by extreme grief when it was past midnight.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे चतुष्षष्टितमस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the sixty fourth Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
