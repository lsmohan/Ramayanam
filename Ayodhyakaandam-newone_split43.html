Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 43
Heartbroken Kousalyā blames Daṡaratha for sending her son away. She wishes he somehow comes back miraculously the very next day, making her and the entire people of Ayōdhyā happy.
2.43.1
ततः समीक्ष्य शयने
सन्नं शोकेन पार्थिवम् ।
कौसल्या पुत्रशोकार्ता
तमुवाच महीपतिम् ॥
Kousalyā, distraught with grief for her son, looked at the King as he also lay on the bed sunk in misery and said to that lord of the earth:
2.43.2
राघवे नरशार्दूले
विषमुप्त्वाहिजिह्मगा ।
विचरिष्यति कैकेयी
निर्मुक्तेव हि पन्नगी ॥
Kaikēyee, crooked like a serpent, Having deposited her venom on Rāghava, the tiger among men, will now move like a cobra that has shed its slough!
2.43.3
विवास्य रामं सुभगा
लब्धकामा समाहिता ।
त्रासयिष्यति मां भूयो
दुष्टाहिरिव वेश्मनि ॥
That pretty woman, having exiled Rāma and got her wish, will now, by all means, make my life miserable like a serpent lurking in the house.
2.43.4
अथ स्म नगरे रामः
चरन् भैक्षं गृहे वसेत् ।
कामकारो वरं दातुम्
अपि दासं ममात्मजम् ॥
It would have been better if I had given away my son as a slave. At least, he would have been here in the house, doing the work that is assigned and going around the city for alms.
2.43.5
पातयित्वा तु कैकेय्या
रामं स्थानाद्यथेष्टतः ।
प्रदिष्टो रक्षसां भागः
पर्वणीवाहिताग्निना ॥
Rāma has been dislodged by Kaikēyee from his place as she willed. It is like giving the offerings of the fire ritual on an auspicious day to the Rākshasas!
This terse Sloka demands the knowledge of the practices of that day. The practice is to make offerings to Dēvas in the Hōma Kuṇḍa.
Imagine performing a fire ritual on a very important day and blowing it all off finally, by giving the offerings to Rākshasas!
One interpretation: Rāma is the offering. Consecration is the fire ritual. Ayōdhyā's people are the Dēvas. And the Vana is the Rākshasas.
Another possible interpretation: Kingdom is the offering. Consecration day is the auspicious day. Rāma is the Dēva. Kaikēyee is the Rākshasa, who snatched the offering from Rāma.
2.43.6
गजराजगतिर्वीरो
महाबाहुर्धनुर्धरः ।
वनमाविशते नूनम्
सभार्य स्सह लक्ष्मणः ॥
The mighty-armed, holding the bow, must have entered the Vana with the gait of a majestic elephant, along with wife and Lakshmaṇa.
2.43.7
वने त्वदृष्टदुःखानाम्
केकय्यानुमते त्वया ।
त्यक्तानां वनवासाय
कान्ववस्था भविष्यति ॥
They are strangers to hardships. Who knows in what plight they will find themselves, consigned by you, in obedience to Kaikēyee's wish, to that life in the Vana!
2.43.8
ते रत्नहीनास्तरुणाः
फलकाले विवासिताः ।
कथं वत्स्यन्ति कृपणाः
फलमूलैः कृताशनाः ॥
How could they, deprived of the good things of life in the prime of their youth, and sent into banishment when it is time for enjoying themselves, live dejected subsisting on roots and fruits ?
2.43.9
अपीदानीं स कालस्स्यान्
मम शोकक्षय श्शिवः ।
सभार्यं यत्सह भ्रात्रा
पश्येयमिह राघवम् ॥
How I wish that that auspicious hour could come for me this very minute, when I see Rāghava back here with his wife and brother, putting an end to my sorrow!
2.43.10
श्रृत्वैवोपस्थितौ वीरौ
कदायोध्या भविष्यति ।
यशस्विनी हृष्टजना
सूच्छ्रितध्वजमालिनी ॥
When will this renowned Ayōdhyā be seen with festoons and flags floating high and with people filled with joy, hearing the news of the return of those Veeras?
2.43.11
कदा प्रेक्ष्य नरव्याघ्रौ
अरण्यात्पुनरागतौ ।
नन्दिष्यति पुरी हृष्टा
समुद्र इव पर्वणि ॥
When will this city see the return of those tigers among men from the forest and swell with joy, like the ocean on a full-moon day?
2.43.12
कदाऽयोध्यां महाबाहुः
पुरीं वीरः प्रवेक्ष्यति ।
पुरस्कृत्य रथे सीताम्
वृषभो गोवधूमिव ॥
When will the mighty-armed hero enter the city of Ayōdhyā, having Seetā in front of him in the chariot, like a bull preceded by cow?
2.43.13
कदा प्राणिसहस्राणि
राजमार्गे ममात्मजौ ।
लाजैरवकिरिष्यन्ति
प्रविशन्तावरिन्दमौ ॥
When will the people by thousands shower puffed grain on the royal pathway seeing my sons, the subduers of foes, enter the city?
2.43.14
प्रविशन्तौ कदाऽयोध्याम्
द्रक्ष्यामि शुभकुण्डलौ ।
उदग्रायुधनिस्त्रिंशौ
सश्रृङ्गाविव पर्वतौ ॥
When shall I see both of them, with the lovely ear-pendants and bow and sword held high, enter Ayōdhyā like crested mountains ?
2.43.15
कदासुमनसः कन्यात्
विजातीनां फलानि च ।
प्रदिशन्तः पुरीं हृष्टाः
करिष्यन्ति प्रदक्षिणम् ॥
When again shall your girls go around the city in delight offering fruits and flowers to Brāhmaṇas?
2.43.16
कदा परिणतो बुद्ध्या
वयसा चामरप्रभः ।
अभ्युपैष्यति धर्मज्ञः
त्रिवर्ष इव मां ललन् ॥
When will Rāma, as matured with age and wisdom as he is, knowing Dharma, come to play with me like a three year old child?
2.43.17
निस्संशयं मया मन्ये
पुरा वीर कदर्यया ।
पातुकामेषु वत्सेषु
मातऽणां शातितास्स्तनाः ॥
Undoubtedly, O Veera, I must have been cruel enough in the past to cut off the teats of mother cows when their calves craved to suckle.
It is a common experience in India since the days of Rāma and before, to see women with pinched hearts and a sense of guilt, when they separate the sucking calf from its mother, in order to milk the cow.
2.43.18
साहं गौरिव सिंहेन
विवत्सा वत्सला कृता ।
कैकेय्या पुरुषव्याघ्र
बालवत्सेव गौर्बलात् ॥
That is probably why, o tiger among men, I, full of affection for my child, have been separated forcefully from my youngling by Kaikēyee, like a cow from its calf by a lioness.
2.43.19
न हि तावद्गुणैर्जुष्टम्
सर्वशास्त्रविशारदम् ।
एकपुत्रा विना पुत्रम्
अहं जीवितुमुत्सहे ॥
Deprived of my only son, who has so many virtues and is learned in all Ṡastras, I shall be utterly unable to cling to my life.
2.43.20
न हि मे जीविते किञ्चित्
सामर्थ्यमिह कल्प्यते ।
अपश्यन्त्याः प्रियं पुत्रम्
महाबाहुं महाबलम् ॥
I do not have even an iota of capability to live on without seeing my dear son of mighty arm and immense strength.
2.43.21
अयं हि मां दीपयते समुत्थितः
तनूजशोकप्रभवो हुताशनः ।
महीमिमां रश्मिभिरुद्धतप्रभः
यथा निदाघे भगवान् दिवाकरः ॥
The raging fire of grief for my son burns me away as the mighty lord sun burns the earth in summer with his blazing rays!
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे त्रिचत्वारिंशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the forty-third Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
