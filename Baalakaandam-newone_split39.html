Baala Kaanda - Sarga 39
In this Sarga, Viṡwāmitra narrates the story of the sacrificial horse being stolen by Indra and the sixty thousand sons of Sagara digging the earth everywhere in search of the horse.
1.39.1
विश्वामित्रवचः श्रुत्वा
कथान्ते रघुनन्दनः ।
उवाच परमप्रीतो
मुनिं दीप्तमिवानलम् ॥
Rāma heard the story being narrated by Viṡwāmitra, who was radiant like a fire. Rāma found it fascinating and wanted the stories to continue, and asked:
1.39.2
श्रोतुमिच्छामि भद्रं ते
विस्तरेण कथामिमाम् ।
पूर्वको मे कथं ब्रह्मन्
यज्ञं वै समुपाहरत् ॥
O Brahman! May all bode well for you! I would like to hear it in detail. Tell me how my ancestor performed the Yajña!
1.39.3
विश्वामित्रस्तु काकुत्स्थम्
उवाच प्रहसन्निव ।
श्रूयतां विस्तरो राम
सगरस्य महात्मनः ॥
Viṡwāmitra smiled and said to Rāma, the prince of Kākutstha lineage: “Let me tell you the full story in detail!”
Viṡwāmitra must have been smiling at the curiosity of the young lad Rāma about his ancestor.
1.39.4
शङ्करश्वशुरो नाम
हिमवानचलोत्तमः ।
विन्ध्यपर्वतमासाद्य
निरीक्षेते परस्परम् ॥
Himavān, the father-in-law of Ṡaṅkara, the best among the mountains, and the mountain range of Vindhya are in the direct line of sight of each other.
Ṡaṅkara is another name for lord Ṡiva.
It is amazing that people in those bygone eras were not only conversant with the geography of the land, but were also conscious of the details on an everyday basis.
1.39.5
तयोर्मध्ये प्रवृत्तोऽभूत्
यज्ञः स पुरुषोत्तम ।
स हि देशो नरव्याघ्र
प्रशस्तो यज्ञकर्मणि ॥
O foremost of spirited beings, the land in between those two mountain ranges is known to be the best for performing Yajñas. That is where the Yajña was performed.
1.39.6
तस्याश्वचर्यां काकुत्स्थ
दृढधन्वा महारथः ।
अंशुमानकरोत्तात
सगरस्य मते स्थितः ॥
O dear prince of Kākutstha dynasty! Amṡuman, the great charioteer and archer guarded the horse on its path fully entrusted by Sagara.
Aṡwamēdha Yajña was a simple political instrument that was used to bring weak kingdoms under the rule of one large empire.
Any king, who was convinced of his own strength, set a horse free to roam. All the areas that the horse wandered around were claimed by the king, unless the king of that land put up a challenge.
In that process, the weak kingdoms became parts and provinces of a larger kingdom, with minimal military or civilian bloodshed in the process.
The analogy for this, in modern times, is the mergers and acquisitions process among businesses. Small and weak businesses are 'eaten' by large corporations all the time.
The old customers of the old business are served by the new corporation after the merger, in a seamless manner, most of the time.
Governments and kings are the servants of their subjects. Just as businesses provide 'services' to people, the kings and warriors used to provide governance and administrative 'services' to the people.
As such, kings and administrators were interchangeable in those days and the identity of land or people was not tied to the rulers.



The motivation for Indra (Ṡlōka 1.39.7) to take away the horse is not given here, leaving it open for various conjunctures. There are quite a few loose ends like this in the Rāmāyaṇa. It could be because portions of the original text were lost over time, or stories like these were too well known at that time, to be elaborated.
1.39.7
तस्य पर्वणि तं यज्ञम्
यजमानस्य वासवः ।
राक्षसीं तनुमास्थाय
यज्ञीयाश्वमपाहरत् ॥
Indra took the form of a Rākshasa and took away the horse of that Yajña that was being performed by Sagara.
1.39.8
ह्रियमाणे तु काकुत्स्थ
तस्मिन्नश्वे महात्मनः ।
उपाध्यायगणास्सर्वे
यजमानमथाब्रुवन् ॥
O prince of Raghu dynasty! As the horse of that Mahātma was being snatched, all the priests said to the performer of the Yajña:
1.39.9
अयं पर्वणि वेगेन
यज्ञीयाश्वोऽपनीयते ।
हर्तारं जहि काकुत्स्थ
हयश्चैवोपनीयताम् ॥
O prince of Kākutstha lineage (Sagara)! The horse is being taken away quickly on this auspicious day. You have to strike that abductor and bring the horse back.
1.39.10
यज्ञच्छिद्रं भवत्येतत्
सर्वेषामशिवाय नः ।
तत्तथा क्रियतां राजन्
यथाऽच्छिद्रः क्रतुर्भवेत् ॥
It would be inauspicious if the Yajña is disrupted now. Do what is needed to continue the Yajña without disruption.
1.39.11
उपाध्यायवचः श्रुत्वा
तस्मिन् सदसि पार्थिवः ।
षष्टिं पुत्रसहस्राणि
वाक्यमेतदुवाच ह ॥
Hearing those words of the priests, The king said to his sixty thousand sons in the presence of every one:
1.39.12
गतिं पुत्रा न पश्यामि
रक्षसां पुरुषर्षभाः ।
मन्त्रपूतैर्महाभागैः
आस्थितो हि महाक्रतुः ॥
O my sons, best among men! I do not believe Rākshasas can enter this area of the Yajña, that is made sacred by the Mantras of all the great priests.
1.39.13
तद्गच्छत विचिन्वध्वम्
पुत्रका भद्रमस्तु वः ।
समुद्रमालिनीं सर्वाम्
पृथिवीमनुगच्छत ॥
My dear sons! Go and search the entire earth to its edges, that is surrounded by the waters of the oceans for the horse that was taken away. May all bode well for you!
1.39.14
एकैकं योजनं पुत्रा
विस्तारमभिगच्छत ।
यावत्तुरगसन्दर्शः
तावत् खनत मेदिनीम् ।
तं चैव हयहर्तारम्
मार्गमाणा ममाज्ञया ॥
Each one of you should dig one Yōjana of the earth in search of the abductor. Continue your search till you find the horse.
1.39.15
दीक्षितः पौत्रसहितः
सोपाध्यायगणो ह्यहम् ।
इह स्थास्यामि भद्रं वो
यावत्तुरगदर्शनम् ॥
I will wait here in Deeksha along with my grandson (Amṡuman) and the priests till the horse is recovered. May all bode well for you!
1.39.16
ते सर्वे हृष्टमनसो
राज पुत्रा महाबलाः ।
जग्मुर्महीतलं राम
पितुर्वचनयन्त्रिताः ॥
O Rāma! All of those mighty strong princes went on to dig the earth with enthusiasm, as dictated by their father.
1.39.17
योजनायामविस्तारम्
एकैको धरणीतलम् ।
बिभिदुः पुरुषव्याघ्र
वज्रस्पर्शसमैर्नखैः ॥
Each one of those tigers among men, broke apart one Yōjana area of the ground with their nails that were as hard as diamonds.
1.39.18
शूलैरशनिकल्पैश्च
हलैश्चापि सुदारुणैः ।
भिद्यमाना वसुमती
ननाद रघुनन्दन ॥
O prince of Raghu dynasty! The earth reverberated with loud sounds as it was broken everywhere with spears comparable to Vajrāyudha and ploughs that were horrendous.
Vajrāyudha is the celebrated weapon of Indra.
1.39.19
नागानां वध्यमानानां
असुराणां च राघव ।
राक्षसानां च दुर्द्धर्षः
सत्त्वानां निनदोऽभवत् ॥
O prince of Raghu dynasty! The terrible cries made by Rākshasas, Asuras, serpents and other beings as they were being crushed, filled the earth.
1.39.20
योजनानां सहस्राणि
षष्टिं तु रघुनन्दन ।
बिभिदुर्धरणीं वीरा
रसातलमनुत्तमम् ॥
O prince of Raghu dynasty! Those Veeras broke the earth over an area of sixty thousand Yōjanas turning it into the nether world of Rasātala.
1.39.21
एवं पर्वतसम्बाधम्
जम्बूद्वीपं नृपात्मजाः ।
खनन्तो नृपशार्दूल
सर्वतः परिचक्रमुः ॥
O tiger among princes! They kept digging all over Jamboo Dweepa, which was surrounded by mountains.
1.39.22
ततो देवाः सगन्धर्वाः
सासुरास्सहपन्नगाः ।
सम्भ्रान्तमनसः सर्वे
पितामहमुपागमन् ॥
Then the Dēvas, Gandharvas, Asuras and Serpents were agitated and concerned and went to lord Brahma.
1.39.23
ते प्रसाद्य महात्मानम्
विषण्णवदनास्तदा ।
ऊचुः परमसन्त्रस्ताः
पितामहमिदं वचः ॥
With frightened minds and saddened faces they pleaded to him, received his attention and said:
1.39.24
भगवन् पृथिवी सर्वा
खन्यते सगरात्मजैः ।
बहवश्च महात्मानो
हन्यन्ते तलवासिनः ॥
The entire earth is being dug by the sons of Sagara. They are killing many Mahātmas who live underneath the earth.
1.39.25
अयं यज्ञहरोऽस्माकं
अनेनाश्वोऽपनीयते ।
इति ते सर्वभूतानि
निघ्नन्ति सगरात्मजाः ॥
The sons of Sagara suspect everyone of having taken their horse. They are killing everyone who is not able to show their horse to them.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
बालकाण्डे एकोनचत्वारिंशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the thirty ninth Sarga in Bāla Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
