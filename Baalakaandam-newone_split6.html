Baala Kaanda - Sarga 6
The previous Sarga described the city of Ayōdhyā. This Sarga describes the virtuous people of the city and the riches, abundance and order they were enjoying.
What leads to riches and abundance? Rāmāyaṇa's answer is very simple. People committed to a just and equitable society automatically enjoy riches and abundance. When the environment is just and stable and offers a level playing field, the society automatically flourishes.
Rāmāyaṇa is the story of people and leaders whose primary commitment is to maintain Dharma, a just and stable environment. It is the story of Rāma who set an example, by making enormous personal sacrifices when called for, in the course of protecting Dharma and adhering to truth.
To honor a word given years earlier by someone to someone in the family, he sacrificed all the power, riches and comfort.
In deference to an undercurrent of public sentiment against having a queen who was under the protection of a sex-thirsty enemy for almost a year, he sacrificed his marriage.
1.6.1
तस्यां पुर्यामयोध्यायाम्
वेदवित्सर्वसङ्ग्रहः ।
दीर्घदर्शी महातेजाः
पौरजानपदप्रियः ।
इक्ष्वाकूणामतिरथो
यज्वा धर्मरतो वशी ।
महर्षिकल्पो राजर्षिः
त्रिषु लोकेषु विश्रुतः ।
बलवान्निहतामित्रो
मित्रवान् विजितेन्द्रियः ।
धनैश्च सङ्ग्रहैश्चान्यैः
शक्रवैश्रवणोपमः ।
यथा मनुर्महातेजा
लोकस्य परिरक्षिता ।
तथा दशरथो राजा
वसन् जगदपालयत् ॥
King Daṡaratha, the celebrated warrior of Ikshwāku dynasty, who was well reputed in all the three worlds, ably ruled the earth from that great city of Ayōdhyā following the tradition of the brilliant and great Manu, who protected the entire world.
Daṡaratha was brilliant and knowledgeable in all Vēdas. He was completely in control of all sections of people. He always had his eye on the long term aspects. He was liked by the denizens of both urban and rural areas. He had performed many Yajñas. He had keen interest in the nuances of Dharma. He had complete control of his senses. He had many friends who were aligned with him. He had the strength to destroy his foes. He had money, possessions and other riches comparable to those of Ṡakra and Vaiṡravaṇa He was renowned in all the three worlds as a Rājarshi comparable to Maharshis.
Ṡakra is another name for Indra. Vaiṡravaṇa is another name for Kubēra.
Ṛshi is a visionary and an architect. In this Ṡlōka Daṡaratha is referred to as Rājarshi, a composite word of Rāja (King) and Ṛshi (Architect). He is a visionary king.
1.6.5
तेन सत्याभिसन्धेन
त्रिवर्गमनुतिष्ठता ।
पालिता सा पुरी श्रेष्ठा
इन्द्रेणेवामरावती ॥
That great city was governed by Daṡaratha, who had always stood firmly by his word and invested equally in realizing all the three Vargas, as well as Amarāvati was governed by Indra.
Three Vargas: Dharma, Artha, Kāma.
Rāmāyaṇa's focus is on Dharma, Artha and Kama (the fourth being Mōksha). This is asserted many a time, as you have already seen in the previous Sargas.
1.6.6
तस्मिन् पुरवरे हृष्टा
धर्मात्मानो बहुश्रुताः ।
नरास्तुष्टा धनैः स्वैः स्वैः
अलुब्धाः सत्यवादिनः ॥
The denizens of that great city were well read, happy and contented with their respective riches. They firmly stood by the side of Satya and Dharma.
Satya is a well known Sanskrit word. Hence we would use it as is.
1.6.7
नाल्पसन्निचयः कश्चित्
आसीत्तस्मिन् पुरोत्तमे ।
कुटुम्बी यो ह्यसिद्धार्थोऽ -
गवाश्वधनधान्यवान् ॥
Not even a single household could be found in that city that was not contented or not having enough of cows, horses, food grains or money.
1.6.8
कामी वा न कदर्यो वा
नृशंसः पुरुषः क्वचित् ।
द्रष्टुं शक्यमयोध्यायाम्
नाविद्वान्न च नास्तिकः ॥
It was difficult to find any one in Ayōdhyā who was rude or Nāstika or sexually perverted or menacing or exploiting or causing distress to own kith and kin.
The word Nāstika has multiple connotations. It could mean someone not accepting the authority of Vēdas or not accepting the invisible universal forces.
1.6.9
सर्वे नराश्च नार्यश्च
धर्मशीलाः सुसंयताः ।
उदिताः शीलवृत्ताभ्याम्
महर्षय इवामलाः ॥
Every man and woman prospered. Everyone conducted himself fully in tune with Dharma Their minds were fully in their control and were as clean as that of Maharshis.
Having complete control of mind is extolled all through Rāmāyaṇa and is a common theme in all Indic literature.
This simply means exercising good judgment and being discreet at all times and avoiding knee-jerk and ill-considered reactions.
1.6.10
नाकुण्डली नामकुटी
नास्रग्वी नाल्पभोगवान् ।
नामृष्टो नानुलिप्ताङ्गो
नासुगन्धश्च विद्यते ॥
None could be found in Ayōdhyā who fell short in enjoying fanciful luxuries. Everyone had oil bath regularly and decorated themselves with perfumes, creams, lotions, and ornaments on their ears and head.
1.6.11
नामृष्टभोजी नादाता
नाप्यनङ्गदनिष्कधृक् ।
नाहस्ताभरणो वापि
दृश्यते नाप्यनात्मवान् ॥
None could be found over there, who did not have enough food to eat, who was not fully aware of oneself, who did not have enough decorations on hands, arms and neck and who was not generous.
The many double negatives as used in the Ṡlōka Does not flow well in English prose. But they are very poetic in Sanskrit.
1.6.12
नानाहिताग्निर्नायज्वा
न क्षुद्रो वा न तस्करः ।
कश्चिदासीदयोध्यायाम्
न च निर्वृत्तसङ्करः ॥
No one there was wretched and mean or a thief, nor trespassed the line of probity nor had not done Yajñas and daily fire rituals.
1.6.13
स्वकर्मनिरता नित्यम्
ब्राह्मणा विजितेन्द्रियाः ।
दानाध्ययनशीलाश्च
संयताश्च प्रतिग्रहे ॥
The Brāhmaṇas had full mastery over their senses. They pursued their activities with full zeal. Being generous and being studious was in their very nature. They were very restrained in accepting gifts.
1.6.14
न नास्तिको नानृतको
न कश्चिदबहुश्रुतः ।
नासूयको न वाशक्तो
नाविद्वान् विद्यते तदा ॥
There was no one who was dishonest or found fault with everything for no reason or did not believe in Providence or inherently incapable, or not well read in multiple texts.
1.6.15
नाषडङ्गविदत्रासीत्
नाव्रतो नासहस्रदः ।
न दीनः क्षिप्तचित्तो वा
व्यथितो वापि कश्चन ॥
There was no one who did not know the six Vēdāngas or did not perform the customary Vratas or had not been charitable. There was no one there who felt helpless or had undergone hardships or with disturbed mind.
Vratas are rituals performed over many parts of the year based on celestial events, cycles of nature and for many other purposes.
1.6.16
कश्चिन्नरो वा नारी वा
नाश्रीमान्नाप्यरूपवान् ।
द्रष्टुं शक्यमयोध्यायाम्
नापि राजन्यभक्तिमान् ॥
No man or woman could be found in Ayōdhyā who did not trust the king or who was not handsome or beautiful or who was not endowed with riches and possessions.
1.6.17
वर्णेष्वग्र्यचतुर्थेषु
देवतातिथिपूजकाः ।
कृतज्ञाश्च वदान्याश्च
शूरा विक्रमसंयुताः ।
दीर्घायुषो नराः सर्वे
धर्मं सत्यं च संश्रिताः ।
सहिताः पुत्रपौत्रैश्च
नित्यं स्त्रीभिः पुरोत्तमे ॥
Members of all the four Varṇas worshiped the gods and honored their guests. They were grateful, generous, accomplished and valiant. They were steadfast in their adherence to Dharma and in upholding the truth. All of them were blessed with long lives along with children, grandchildren and chaste women.
1.6.19
क्षत्रं ब्रह्ममुखं चासीत्
वैश्याः क्षत्रमनुव्रताः ।
शूद्राः स्वधर्मनिरताः
त्रीन्वर्णानुपचारिणः ॥
Kshatriyas were following Brāhmaṇas. Vaiṡyas were following Kshatriyas. Sūdras were pursuing their own activities and also helping the other three.
The system of Varṇas is foundational in Indic civilization. It is a social system, with no parallel anywhere in the world. It gives the simple formula for success of a society and well being of everyone.
Rulers (Kshatriyas) should follow thinkers, scientists and philosophers (Brāhmaṇas) but not subdue them. (Think of politicians telling scientists what to do!) Businesses (Vaiṡyas) should operate within the governance framework set by Rulers, but not subvert it. (Think of business people dictating law makers on what laws to make!) Workers (Sūdras) should enable all those three functions.
The Varṇa system is a more intricate topic than it appears. The four Varṇas are not unique to Indic Civilization. They are there in every society and in every part of the world without exception. Scientists, Politicians, Business People and common folk, are not specific to India. Indic Civilization formally recognized the four types of people and provided the governance structure for their interactions.
In the absence of such a system, no one is responsible for anything and everyone is driven by selfish ends, without concern for the overall welfare of the society and the result is 'dog eat dog' world, which we see today.
Modern India is a communal society, which is far different from the Varṇa system described here. Every community today is set to fight for its own good, without regard to the good of overall society.
1.6.20
सा तेनेक्ष्वाकुनाथेन
पुरी सुपरिरक्षिता ।
यथा पुरस्तान्मनुना
मानवेन्द्रेण धीमता ॥
Thus that city was governed and protected well by the descendant of Ikshwāku, just as it was done by the highly intelligent and capable Manu, the lord of people.
1.6.21
योधानामग्निकल्पानाम्
पेशलानाममर्षिणाम् ।
सम्पूर्णा कृतविद्यानाम्
गुहा केसरिणामिव ॥
Ayōdhyā resembled a cave full of lions, replete with well trained, highly skilled and proud warriors that were as powerful and fierce as fire.
1.6.22
काम्भोजविषये जातैः
बाह्लीकैश्च हयोत्तमैः ।
वनायुजैर्नदीजैश्च
पूर्णा हरिहयोत्तमैः ॥
Ayōdhyā was resplendent with horses comparable to the legendary horse of Indra bred in various countries like Kāmbhoja, Bāhlika, Vanāyu and Sindhu.
1.6.23
विन्ध्यपर्वतजैर्मत्तैः
पूर्णा हैमवतैरपि ।
मदान्वितैरतिबलैः
मातङ्गैः पर्वतोपमैः ।
ऐरावतकुलीनैश्च
महापद्मकुलैस्तथा ।
अञ्जनादपि निष्पन्नैः
वामनादपि च द्विपैः ॥
It was resplendent with muscular and brawny elephants of enormous strength with rut flowing from their temples and looking like mountains bred in the mountainous ranges of Himalayas and Vindhyas from the blood of celestial elephants like Airāvata, Mahāpadma, Anjana and Vāmana.
The word 'blood' here is used figuratively to mean 'lineage'.
1.6.25
भद्रैर्मन्द्रैर्मृगैश्चैव
भद्रमन्द्रमृगैस्तथा ।
भद्रमन्द्रैर्भद्रमृगैः
मृगमन्द्रैश्च सा पुरी ।
नित्यमत्तैः सदा पूर्णा
नागैरचलसन्निभैः ॥
Ayōdhyā’s inebriated elephants that resembled mountains, were a mix of three types: Bhadra, Mandra and Mṛga. Some were Bhadra type, some were Mandra type and others were Mṛga type. Some were a mix of Bhadra and Mandra types. Some were a mix of Bhadra and Mṛga types. Some were a mix of Mṛga and Mandra types. And others were a mix of all three types.
1.6.26
सा योजने च द्वे भूयः
सत्यनामा प्रकाशते ।
यस्यां दशरथो राजा
वसन् जगदपालयत् ॥
That city of Ayōdhyā, the abode of the ruler of the kingdom, Daṡaratha, true to its name, was impregnable even within two Yōjanas of its perimeter.
The literal meaning of Ayōdhyā is ' impregnable'.
1.6.27
तां पुरीं स महातेजा
राजा दशरथो महान् ।
शशास शमितामित्रो
नक्षत्राणीव चन्द्रमाः ॥
Having subdued all his foes and shining like a moon among stars, the brilliant King Daṡaratha ruled over that city.
1.6.28
तां सत्यनामां दृढतोरणार्गलां
गृहैर्विचित्रैरुपशोभितां शिवाम् ।
पुरीमयोध्यां नृसहस्रसङ्कुलां
शशास वै शक्रसमो महीपतिः ॥
The king, comparable to Ṡakra, ruled over the auspicious city of Ayōdhyā resplendent with an assortment of dwellings housing thousands of people fortified by hefty gates with strong locks, true to its name.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
बालकाण्डे षष्ठस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the sixth Sarga in Bāla Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
