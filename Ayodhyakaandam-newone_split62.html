Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 62
In this Sarga, Daṡaratha gets distressed by the harsh words of Kousalyā, seen in the previous Sarga, and becomes absorbed in sorrowful thought. He suddenly recollects a mistake he committed in the past. The grief born out of that memory gets added to his grief for Rāma.
He then begs Kousalyā to understand his predicament. Kousalyā repents being harsh with him and begs for his forgiveness. Feeling a little better, the kings slips into sleep, with the weight of grief upon him.
2.62.1
एवं तु क्रुद्धया राजा
राममात्रा सशोकया ।
श्रावितः परुषं वाक्यम्
चिन्तयामस दुःखितः ॥
The king, receiving a torrent of such harsh words from the mother of Rāma, was taken over by grief and anger and felt greatly distressed and became absorbed in sorrowful thought.
2.62.2
चिन्तयित्वा स च नृपो
मुमोह व्याकुलेन्द्रियः ।
अथ दीर्घेण कालेन
संज्ञामाप परन्तपः ॥
Those thoughts led his senses into in disarray and made his mind disoriented. It took a while for that harrier of foe to regain sanity.
2.62.3
स संज्ञामुपलभ्यैव
दीर्घमुष्णं च निश्श्वसन् ।
कौसल्यां पार्श्वतो दृष्ट्वा
पुन श्चिन्तामुपागमत् ॥
As soon as he regained his sanity, he heaved long hot sighs. He looked at Kousalyā by his side and was absorbed in thoughts again.
2.62.4
तस्य चिन्तयमानस्य
प्रत्याभात्कर्म दुष्कृतम् ।
यदनेन कृतं पूर्वम्
अज्ञानाच्छब्दवेधिना ॥
Thus brooding, he suddenly recollected a terrible thing he has done out of ignorance in the past, while aiming a target by ear.
2.62.5
अमनास्तेन शोकेन
रामशोकेन च प्रभुः ।
द्वाभ्यामपि महाराजः
शोकाभ्यामन्वतप्यत ॥
The two sources of grief, the grief on account of Rāma and the grief from that recollection made the powerful king extremely distressed.
2.62.6
दह्यामानस्सशोकाभ्याम्
कौसल्यामाह भूपतिः ।
वेपमानोऽञ्जलिं कृत्वा
प्रसादार्थमवाङ्मुखः ॥
The lord of lands was shaken, fried by those two sources of grief. He then, joining his palms together and with his head bowed down, said to Kousalyā:
2.62.7
प्रसादये त्वां कौसल्ये
रचितोऽयं मयाऽञ्जलिः ।
वत्सला चानृशंसा च
त्वं हि नित्यं परेष्वपि ॥
O Kousalyā! Please be sympathetic to me! I beseech you with my joined palms. You show affection even to those who are unrelated. You are, by no means, a cruel person.
2.62.8
भर्ता तु खलु नारीणाम्
गुणवान्निर्गुणोऽपि वा ।
धर्मं विमृशमानानाम्
प्रत्यक्षं देवि दैवतम् ॥
O queen! Virtuous or not, is not a husband a living god for a woman who regards Dharma?
2.62.9
सा त्वं धर्मपरा नित्यम्
दृष्टलोक परावरा ।
नार्हसे विप्रियं वक्तुम्
दुखिःताऽपि सुदुःखितम् ॥
You have always been a strict adherent of Dharma. You have seen the best and worst of this world. Even though you are sad, it does not befit you to say unkind words to someone who is extremely sad.
2.62.10
तद्वाक्यं करुणं राज्ञः
श्रुत्वा दीनस्य भाषितम् ।
कौसल्या व्यसृजद्बाष्पम्
प्रणालीव नवोदकम् ॥
Hearing those pitiable words of the king, uttered in the most helpless manner, Kousalyā shed a stream of tears, like fresh rain water gushing out of a spout.
2.62.11
सा मूर्ध्निबध्वा रुदती
राज्ञः पद्ममिवाञ्जलिम् ।
सम्भ्रमादब्रवीत् त्रस्ता
त्वरमाणाक्षरं वचः ॥
Weeping, she held the King's palms that were joined together in the shape of a lotus bud, and placed them on her head. Moved by a great fear, she said, in words that came tumbling in her extreme agitation:
Vālmeeki pours out the emotions in every detail and this emphasis on emotions is what makes Rāmāyaṇa a Kavya.
The image of the Krounca bird shot by a hunter, writhing in a pool of blood, must have still been vivid in his mind.
2.62.12
प्रसीद शिरसा याचे
भूमौ निपतितास्मि ते ।
याचितास्मि हता देव
क्षन्तव्याहं न हि त्वया ॥
Be kind to me, my lord, I beg you with bowed head, and falling on the ground (at your feet). I feel terrible by seeing you beseech me thus. I do not even deserve to be forgiven!
2.62.13
नैषा हि सा स्त्री भवति
श्लाघनीयेन धीमता ।
उभयोर्लोकयोर्वीर
पत्या या संप्रसाद्यते ॥
O Veera! A woman who drags her husband, who is sagacious and worthy of being extolled, to the state of beseeching her, is neither fit for this life, nor for the one after.
2.62.14
जानामि धर्मं धर्मज्ञ
त्वां जाने सत्यवादिनम् ।
पुत्रशोकार्तया तत्तु
मया किमपि भाषितम् ॥
O knower of Dharma! I know Dharma. I also know that you are a man your word. Whatever I uttered, was only born out of the grief, on account of my son.
2.62.15
शोको नाशयते धैर्यम्
शोको नाशयते श्रुतम् ।
शोको नाशयते सर्वम्
नास्ति शोकसमो रिपुः ॥
Grief destroys strength. Grief annuls learning. Grief destroys everything. There is no enemy equal to grief.
Everyone, at one occasion or other, would say things that they later wish that they had not.
But, instead of being defensive about them, one is better off acknowledging them and deflect the blame to the very source, which caused one to say such things.
In this instance, Kousalyā blames her harsh words on the power of grief that afflicted her.
2.62.16
शक्य आपतितस्सोढुम्
प्रहारो रिपुहस्ततः ।
सोढुंमापतितश्शोकः
सुसूक्ष्मोऽपि न शक्यते ॥
One can bear a blow received from the hand of an enemy, but not even the slightest shock of grief.
2.62.17
धर्मज्ञाश्श्रुतिमन्तोऽपि
छिन्नधर्मार्थसंशयाः ।
यतयो वीर मुह्यन्ति
शोकसम्मूढचेतसः ॥
O Veera! even Munis who know Dharma, who are good at Ṡastras and who have all their questions regarding (conflicting) Dharma and Artha cleared, get confused when their minds are numbed by grief.
2.62.18
वनवासाय रामस्य
पञ्चरात्रोऽद्य गण्यते ।
यश्शोकहतहर्षायाः
पञ्चवर्षोपमो मम ॥
This is counted as the fifth night of Rāma's life in Vana. But the joy that was erased by this grief make it feel like five years, to me.
2.62.19
तं हि चिन्तयमानायाः
शोकोऽयं हृदि वर्धते ।
नदीनामिव वेगेन
समुद्रसलिलं महत् ॥
The grief in my heart swells, as I think of him, like a mighty ocean does from the rapid discharge of waters by the rivers.
2.62.20
एवं हि कथयन्त्यास्तु
कौसल्यायाश्शुभं वचः ।
मन्दरश्मिरभूत्सूर्यो
रजनी चाभ्यवर्तत ॥
As Kousalyā was saying these heartening words, the rays of the sun began to fade as the night set in.
2.62.21
तथा प्रसादितो वाक्यैः
देव्या कौसल्यया नृपः ।
शोकेन च समाक्रान्तो
निद्राया वशमेयिवान् ॥
Thus, the king, who felt better by those words of lady Kousalyā, fell asleep, overtaken by grief.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे द्विषष्टितमस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the sixty second Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
