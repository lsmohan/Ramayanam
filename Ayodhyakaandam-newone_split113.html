Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 113
In this Sarga, Bharata takes the sandals and leaves for Ayōdhyā. On his way back he pays obeisance to Ṛshi Bharadwāja and tells him about Rāma's decision to stay back in the Vana and that he has been ordered to go back to Ayōdhyā and that the sandals of Rāma that he is taking with him would rule the kingdom. Bharadwāja lauds his sense of Dharma and his devotion to it.
Bharata is scorched by grief seeing Ayōdhyā, which, bereft of his father and brother, had lost its entire luster and is drowned in silence.
2.113.1
ततश्शिरसि कृत्वा तु
पादुके भरतस्तदा ।
आरुरोह रथं हृष्टः
शत्रुघ्नेन समन्वितः ॥
Then Bharata, placing the sandals on his head, got onto his chariot joyously, along with Ṡatrughna.
2.113.2
वसिष्ठो वामदेवश्च
जाबालिश्च दृढव्रतः ।
अग्रतः प्रययु स्सर्वे
मन्त्रिणो मन्त्रपूजिताः ॥
All the ministers, Vasishṭha, Vāmadēva and Jābāli of rigorous discipline, who were honored for their wise counsel, proceeded ahead of them.
2.113.3
मन्दाकिनीं नदीं रम्यां
प्राङ्मुखास्ते ययुस्तदा ।
प्रदक्षिणं च कुर्वाणाः
चित्रकूटं महागिरिम् ॥
Then they circumambulated Citrakūṭa, the great mountain in reverence and headed east towards the beautiful river Mandākini.
The life energy of ancient civilizations comes from this reverence and connection with Nature. Everything magnanimous like a mountain, river and ocean, magical like the emergence of a bird from the egg or the birth of an animal or the first rain drops of the monsoon season, help us connect with the mystery of the universe.
India, with its unbroken civilization, was able to carry customs like circumambulating a mountain, giving Hārati to a river, worshipping trees and plants like Banyan, Tulasi and animals like cows, snakes, monkeys, rats and dogs, that represent our connection with the nature, into the modern times.
Unfortunately, in other parts of the world, where the ancient civilizations did not survive and where synthetic and dry religions (i.e. religions based on a book or a person and not on nature and mystery) dominated or casted their shadow, this enigmatic relationship with nature got dampened, damaged, erased and even derided. Everything that is not said by the person or not written in the book became dried up.
Even in India, the worship of money and technology is doing the same harm, i.e., disconnect with the nature and mystery, to some extent.
2.113.4
पश्यन्धातुसहस्राणि
रम्याणि विविधानि च ।
प्रययौ तस्य पार्श्वेन
ससैन्यो भरतस्तदा ॥
Bharata, along with the army proceeded along the flanks of that mountain seeing thousands of varieties of beautiful mineral deposits.
2.113.5
अदूराच्चित्रकूटस्य
ददर्श भरतस्तदा ।
आश्रमं यत्र स मुनिः
भरद्वाजः कृतालयः ॥
Not very far from that Citrakūṭa, Bharata saw the Āṡrama which the Muni Bharadwāja had made his abode.
2.113.6
स तमाश्रममागम्य
भरद्वाजस्य बुद्धिमान् ।
अवतीर्य रथात्पादौ
ववन्दे भरतस्तदा ॥
Upon reaching that Āṡrama of sagacious Bharadwāja, Bharata got down from the chariot and offered Vandana at his feet.
2.113.7
ततो हृष्टो भरद्वाजो
भरतं वाक्यमब्रवीत् ।
अपि कृत्यं कृतं तात
रामेण च समागतम् ॥
Then, the pleased Bharadwāja said these words to Bharata: "My dear child! Could you meet Rāma? Did you accomplish what you wanted to?"
2.113.8
एवमुक्तस्स तु ततो
भरद्वाजेन धीमता ।
प्रत्युवाच भरद्वाजं
भरतो धर्मवत्सलः ॥
Asked thus by the sagacious Bharadwāja, Bharata, who loved Dharma, responded to him saying:
2.113.9
स याच्यमानो गुरुणा
मया च दृढविक्रमः ।
राघवः परमप्रीतो
वसिष्ठं वाक्यमब्रवीत् ॥
Pleaded by the guru as well as by me, the pleased Rāghava, of steadfast prowess, said these words to Vasishṭha:
2.113.10
पितुः प्रतिज्ञां तामेव
पालयिष्यामि तत्त्वतः ।
चतुर्दश हि वर्षाणि
या प्रतिज्ञा पितुर्मम ॥
I will abide by the word given by our father in its every sense; the fourteen years is the core aspect of my father's word.
Bharata is faced with the hard task of explaining himself convincingly to someone who has not witnessed his meeting with Rāma.
2.113.11
एवमुक्तो महाप्राज्ञो
वसिष्ठः प्रत्युवाच ह ।
वाक्यज्ञो वाक्यकुशलं
राघवं वचनं महत् ॥
Vasishṭha, the sage extraordinaire, who is adept with the right words, responded in these pregnant words to Rāghava, who is skilled at words.
In the previous Sarga Vālmeeki described Bharata's conversation about the sandals. But here, Vālmeeki describes the same scene as if Vasishṭha had spoken. Why the contradiction?
It is not a contradiction. It is just a style of narration, of adding more detail when an incident or scene is described again. It is like showing the scene from a different angle or from someone else's perspective.
2.113.12
एते प्रयच्छ संहृष्टः
पादुके हेमभूषिते ।
अयोध्यायां महाप्राज्ञ
योगक्षेमकरे तव ॥
O extraordinarily sagacious one! Give us with a glad heart your sandals that are decorated in gold, which shall look after the Yōga and Kshēma of Ayōdhyā!
2.113.13
एवमुक्तो वसिष्ठेन
राघवः प्राङ्मुखः स्थितः ।
पादुके ह्यधिरुह्यैते
मम राज्याय वै ददौ ॥
Thus told by Vasishṭha, Rāghava turned to the east, stepped on these sandals, and then gave them to me for the governance of the kingdom.
2.113.14
निवृत्तोऽहमनुज्ञातो
रामेण सुमहात्मना ।
अयोध्यामेव गच्छामि
गृहीत्वा पादुके शुभे ॥
Having been ordered to return (to Ayōdhyā) by Rāma, the great Mahātma, I took these auspicious sandals and am heading towards Ayōdhyā.
2.113.15
एतच्छ्रुत्वा शुभं वाक्यं
भरतस्य महात्मनः ।
भरद्वाजश्शुभतरं
मुनिर्वाक्यमुवाच तम् ॥
Hearing those becoming words of Mahātma Bharata, Bharadwāja, the Muni, spoke words that were even more gratifying to the ear:
2.113.16
नैतच्चित्रं नरव्याघ्र
शीलवृत्तवतां वर ।
यदार्यं त्वयि तिष्ठेत्तु
निम्ने सृष्टमिवोदकम् ॥
O tiger among men! O foremost among the ones counted for their steady character! It is no surprise that such nobility is lodged in you, like water that is poured down settles at the lowest spot!
2.113.17
अमृतस्समहाबाहुः
पिता दशरथस्तव ।
यस्य त्वमीदृशः पुत्रो
धर्मज्ञो धर्मवत्सलः ॥
Daṡaratha, your father, of mighty arm is truly immortal by having a son like you, who knows Dharma and loves it.
2.113.18
तमृषिं तु महात्मानम्
उक्तवाक्यं कृताञ्जलिः ।
आमन्त्रयितुमारेभे
चरणावुपगृह्य च ॥
When that Ṛshi, the Mahātma, spoke thus, he saluted him with joined palms and touched his feet and requested his permission to leave.
2.113.19
ततः प्रदक्षिणं कृत्वा
भरद्वाजं पुनः पुनः ।
भरतस्तु ययौ श्रीमान्
अयोध्यां सह मन्त्रिभिः ॥
After circumambulating Bharadwāja in reverence many a time, Bharata, the blessed one, started for Ayōdhyā along with the ministers.
2.113.20
यानैश्च शकटैश्चैव
हयैर्नागैश्च सा चमूः ।
पुनर्निवृत्ता विस्तीर्णा
भरतस्यानुयायिनी ॥
Bharata's extensive army commenced its return march with its carriages and carts, its horses and elephants.
2.113.21
ततस्ते यमुनां दिव्यां
नदीं तीर्त्वोर्मिमालिनीम् ।
ददृशुस्तां पुन स्सर्वे
गङ्गां शुभजलां नदीम् ॥
Then, after crossing the exquisite river Yamuna, of undulating waves, all of them saw river Gaṅgā, of auspicious waters, again.
2.113.22
तां रम्यजलसंपूर्णां
सन्तीर्य सहबान्धवः ।
शृङ्गिबेरपुरं रम्यं
प्रविवेश ससैनिकः ।
शृङ्गिबेरपुराद्भूयः
त्वयोध्यां सन्ददर्श ह ॥
Crossing that beautiful river which was abundant with water, along with his kin, he entered Ṡṛṅgibērapura, along with his army. Then, proceeding from Ṡṛṅgibērapura, he reached Ayōdhyā.
2.113.23
अयोध्यां च ततो दृष्ट्वा
पित्रा भ्रात्रा विवर्जिताम् ।
भरतो दुःख सन्तप्तः
सारथिं चेदमब्रवीत् ॥
Then, seeing Ayōdhyā that was bereft of his father and brother, Bharata, scorched by grief said to his charioteer:
2.113.24
सारथे पश्य विध्वस्ता
साऽयोध्या न प्रकाशते ।
निराकारा निरानन्दा
दीना प्रतिहतस्वरा ॥
O charioteer! See Ayōdhyā! It has became a widow, with no trace of its luster! It is out of shape and its joy vanished! It is pitiful and its voice is muffled!
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे त्रयोदशोरशततमस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the one hundred and thirteenth Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
