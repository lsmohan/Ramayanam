Aranya Kaanda - Sarga 60
In this Sarga, Rāma searches the wide and deep jungle for Seetā. Each and every tree reminds him the figure and limbs of his beloved. He asks each tree whether it has seen Seetā. Like a madman, he goes from wood to wood, mountain to mountain, river to river looking for Seetā. He hallucinates that she is hiding behind the bushes with her yellow silk garment seen here and there. But then again, he convinces himself that Seetā would not play such cruel jokes on him. He imagines the terrible act of her being devoured by the Rākshasas piece by piece.
3.60.1
भृशमाव्रजमानस्य
तस्याधो वामलोचनम् ।
प्रास्फुरच्चास्खलद्रामो
वेपथुश्चाप्य जायत ॥
As he hastened towards the Āṡrama, Rāma floundered, his frame trembled and the lower lid of his left eye quivered intensely.
Vālmeeki saw the male of a pair of birds struck down by a hunter writhing in a pool of blood for no fault of his and the bird’s beloved watching over him helplessly.
It must have moved him so much that he does not seem to have enough occasion to express it through the story of Rāma.
In Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa, he expressed it through the characters of Daṡaratha, Kousalyā, the people of Ayōdhyā, and later Bharata.
From now on, he expresses it through Rāma, to his heart’s content.
How will a sensitive soul ever find peace, after witnessing someone wrongly accused or wrongly punished or wrongly thrown into misery?
3.60.2
उपालक्ष्य निमित्तानि
सोऽशुभानि मुहुर्मुहुः ।
अपि क्षेमं नु सीताया
इति वै व्याजहार च ॥
Seeing evil omens again and again, he exclaimed, ‘I hope Seetā is safe!’
3.60.3
त्वरमाणो जगामाथ
सीतादर्शनलालसः ।
शून्यमावसथं दृष्ट्वा
बभूवोद्विग्नमानसः ॥
Eagar to see Seetā, he went in all haste. Seeing the Āṡrama empty, his mind was distraught.
3.60.4
उद्भ्रमन्निव वेगेन
विक्षिपन्रघुनन्दनः ।
तत्र तत्रोटजस्थानम्
अभिवीक्ष्य समन्ततः ।
ददर्श पर्णशालां च
रहितां सीतया तदा ।
श्रिया विरहितां ध्वस्ताम्
हेमन्ते पद्मिनीमिव ॥
Like one reeling, flinging his arms, the delight of Raghus searched every spot all over that cottage, only to find that leafy cottage devoid of its life, like a lotus pond in winter.
3.60.6
रुदन्तमिव वृक्षैश्च
म्लानपुष्पमृगद्विजम् ।
श्रिया विहीनं विध्वस्तम्
सन्त्यक्तवनदेवतम् ।
विप्रकीर्णाजिनकुशम्
विप्रविद्धब्रुसीकटम् ।
दृष्ट्वा शून्यं निजस्थानम्
विललाप पुनः पुनः ॥
Seeing his very own dwelling empty, its trees appearing to weep, its flowers wilting, its birds and beasts dispirited, its charm robbed and thrown into disorder, with the sylvan deities having deserted it, deerskin and Kuṡa grass scattered all over the place, and the reed-mats thrown around, he lamented repeatedly.
3.60.8
हृता मृता वा नष्टा वा
भक्षिता वा भविष्यति ।
निलीनाप्यथवा भीरुः
अथवा वनमाश्रिता ॥
Has she been abducted, devoured, or is she lost or dead? Has the timid one hid herself? Or did she go into the Vana?
We have seen how strong Seetā is, in the previous Sargas 47 through 56.
Yet, Rāma, out of love, refers to her as ‘timid Seetā’. It is similar to the usage of saying ‘poor fellow’ about someone who faces unwarranted and undeserving adversity.
3.60.9
गता विचेतुं पुष्पाणि
फलान्यपि च वा पुनः ।
अथवा पद्मिनीं याता
जलार्थं वा नदीं गता ॥
Has she gone to pluck flowers or fruits? Or has she gone to the lotus pond? Or has she gone to the river to fetch water?
3.60.10
यत्नान्मृगयमाणस्तु
नाससाद वने प्रियाम् ।
शोकरक्तेक्षणश्शोकात्
उन्मत्त इव लक्ष्यते ॥
Search as much as he could, he did not find his beloved in the Vana. With his eyes bloodshot with grief, he looked like a mad man.
3.60.11
वृक्षाद्वृक्षं प्रधावन्स
गिरेश्चाद्रिं नदान्नदीम् ।
बभूव विलपन्रामः
शोकार्णवपरिप्लुतः ॥
Sunk in an ocean of grief he ran lamenting from one tree to the other, from hill to mountain and stream to river.
3.60.12
अपि काचित्त्वया दृष्टा
सा कदम्बप्रिया प्रिया ।
कदम्ब यदि जानीषे
शंस सीतां शुभाननाम् ॥
O Kadamba tree! Have you seen my beloved who loves the Kadamba flowers? Please tell me if you know about Seetā of auspicious face!
3.60.13
स्निग्धपल्लवसङ्काशा
पीतकौशेयवासिनी ।
शंसस्व यदि वा दृष्टा
बिल्व बिल्वोपमस्तनी ॥
O Bilva tree! Tell me if you have seen the one who has the sheen of tender shoots, who wears yellow garments of silk and whose breasts are like Bilva fruits!
3.60.14
अथवाऽर्जुन शंस त्वम्
प्रियां तामर्जुनप्रियाम् ।
जनकस्य सुता भीरुः
यदि जीवति वा न वा ॥
O Arjuna tree! You, at least, tell me about her, my beloved who loves the Arjuna trees; is she, the daughter of Janaka, the timid one, alive and not?
3.60.15
ककुभः ककुभोरूं ताम्
व्यक्तं जानाति मैथिलीम् ।
यथा पल्लवपुष्पाढ्यो
भाति ह्येष वनस्पतिः ॥
This Kakubha tree, which is resplendent with the tender shoots and flowers must surely know about Maithili whose thighs resemble the Kakubha (branches)!
3.60.16
भ्रमरैरुपगीतश्च
यथा द्रुमवरो ह्ययम् ।
एष व्यक्तं विजानाति
तिलकस्तिलकप्रियाम् ॥
This Tilaka tree, the best among trees, whose glory is sung by the humming bees hovering around it must very well and surely know about her who loves the Tilaka!
3.60.17
अशोक शोकापनुद
शोकोपहतचेतसम् ।
त्वन्नामानं कुरु क्षिप्रम्
प्रियासन्दर्शनेन माम् ॥
O Aṡōka tree, O dispeller of sadness! My heart is stricken with grief. Please show my beloved to me and make me your namesake fast!
The literal meaning of Aṡōka is ‘without sadness’.
3.60.18
यदि ताल त्वया दृष्टा
पक्वतालफलस्तनी ।
कथयस्व वरारोहाम्
कारुण्यं यदि ते मयि ॥
O Tāla tree! If you have seen her of excellent Kaṭee and ripened Tāla fruit like breasts, would you be kind enough to tell me about her?
3.60.19
यदि दृष्टा त्वया सीता
जम्बु जम्बूफलोपमा ।
प्रियां यदि विजानीषे
निःशङ्कं कथयस्व मे ॥
O Jambū tree! If you have seen or know about Seetā, my beloved, who resembles the Jambū fruit please tell me, without holding back anything!
3.60.20
अहो त्वं कर्णिकाराद्य
सुपुष्पैश्शोभसे भृशम् ।
कर्णिकारप्रिया साध्वी
शंस दृष्टा प्रिया यदि ॥
O Karṇikāra tree! You are quite splendid today in excellent flower! Please tell me whether you have seen my beloved, a good dame, who loves the Karṇikāra trees!
3.60.21
चूतनीपमहासालान्
पनसान्कुरवान्धवान् ।
दाडिमाननसान्गत्वा
दृष्ट्वा रामो महायशाः ।
मल्लिका माधवीश्चैव
चम्पकान्केतकीस्तथा ।
पृच्छन्रामो वने भ्रान्तः
उन्मत्त इव लक्ष्यते ॥
Rāma, a man of great renown, looking like a madman, ran around seeing, approaching and asking thus, the variety of trees of the Vana such as Cūta, Neepa, giant Sāla, Panasa, Kurava, Dhava, Dāḍima, Anasā, Mallika, Mādhavi, Campaka and Kētakee.
It is such a sad situation! Your beloved suddenly disappears without a trace and there is no one even to talk to, except the trees!
3.60.23
अथवा मृगशाबाक्षीम्
मृग जानासि मैथिलीम् ।
मृगविप्रेक्षणी कान्ता
मृगीभिस्सहिता भवेत् ॥
You at least, O deer, should know where Maithili with the fawn eyes is! Ha, my beloved with flitting eyes of the deer should be with the does!
3.60.24
गज सा गजनासोरूः
यदि दृष्टा त्वया भवेत् ।
तां मन्ये विदितां तुभ्यम्
आख्याहि वरवारण ॥
O best of elephants, you might have seen Maithili whose thighs are like the elephant’s trunk; I think you know about her, please tell me!
3.60.25
शार्दूल यदि सा दृष्टा
प्रिया चन्द्रनिभानना ।
मैथिली मम विस्रब्धम्
कथयस्व न ते भयम् ॥
O tiger, please tell me if my darling Maithili with the moon-bright face was seen by you! Please have no apprehension, you have nothing to fear from me.
3.60.26
किं धावसि प्रिये दूरे
दृष्टाऽसि कमलेक्षणे ।
वृक्षैराच्छाद्य चात्मानम्
किं मां न प्रतिभाषसे ॥
I see you, O you of lotus eyes! My dear, why do you run away! You hide yourself behind the trees; Why do you not respond to me?
3.60.27
तिष्ठ तिष्ठ वरारोहे
न तेऽस्ति करुणा मयि ।
नात्यर्थं हास्यशीलाऽसि
किमर्थं मामुपेक्षसे ॥
Hold on, O you of excellent Kaṭee, have you no pity for me? You, by any means, are not the one to play practical jokes; why do you ignore me?
3.60.28
पीतकौशेयकेनासि
सूचिता वरवर्णिनि ।
धावन्त्यपि मया दृष्टा
तिष्ठ यद्यस्ति सौहृदम् ॥
Your yellow silks betray you, O you of lovely complexion; I see you even as you run away; stop if you have any affection for me!
3.60.29
नैव सा नूनमथवा
हिंसिता चारुहासिनी ।
कृच्छ्रप्राप्तं न मां नूनम्
यथोपेक्षितुमर्हति ॥
She of lovely laughter must have been maimed; she would otherwise certainly not ignore me, on whom such a suffering has befallen.
3.60.30
व्यक्तं सा भक्षिता बाला
राक्षसैः पिशिताशनैः ।
विभज्याङ्गानि सर्वाणि
मया विरहिता प्रिया ॥
Clearly my darling, young and separated from me, has been devoured by the flesh-eating Rākshasas, with all her limbs divided by them among themselves.
3.60.31
नूनं तच्छुभदन्तोष्ठम्
सुनासं चारुकुण्डलम् ।
पूर्णचन्द्रनिभं ग्रस्तम्
मुखंनिष्प्रभतां गतम् ॥
Surely, that face with its fine teeth, beautiful lips, shapely nose and lovely ear-pendants, rivalling the full moon, must have lost its luster, grabbed (by them)!
3.60.32
सा हि चम्पकवर्णाभा
ग्रीवा ग्रैवेयशोभिता ।
कोमला विलपन्त्यास्तु
कान्ताया भक्षिता शुभा ॥
The lovely neck of my beloved, adorned with necklaces, glossy like the Campaka must have been devoured as she wept.
3.60.33
नूनं विक्षिप्यमाणौ तौ
बाहू पल्लवकोमलौ ।
भक्षितौ वेपमानाग्रौ
सहस्ताभरणाङ्गदौ ॥
Those arms, soft as tender shoots and adorned with armlets and bracelets, should have flung about desperately with the fingertips quivering, as they were eaten up.
3.60.34
मया विरहिता बाला
रक्षसां भक्षणाय वै ।
सार्धेनेव परित्यक्ता
भक्षिता बहुबान्धवा ॥
She, so young, was separated from me, destined to fall a victim to the Rākshasas. She, of countless kith and kin, was eaten up, like one abandoned by a caravan.
3.60.35
हा लक्ष्मण महाबाहो
पश्यसि त्वं प्रियां क्वचित् ।
हा प्रिये क्व गता भद्रे
हा सीतेति पुनः पुनः ॥
O Lakshmaṇa of mighty arm! Do you see Seetā anywhere? O my beloved! O my dear! O Seetā! Where did you go?
3.60.36
इत्येवं विलपन्रामः
परिधावन्वनाद्वनम् ।
क्वचिदुद्भ्रमते वेगात्
क्वचिद्विभ्रमते बलात् ।
क्वचिन्मत्त इवाभाति
कान्तान्वेषणतत्परः ॥
Thus lamenting, Rāma ran from one Vana to the other spinning round and round at times, in great speed. Madly in search of his wife, he looked like a madman, his strength yielding way to imbalance.
3.60.37
स वनानि नदीश्शैलान्
गिरिप्रस्रवणानि च ।
काननानि च वेगेन
भ्रमत्यपरिसंस्थितः ॥
Unable to remain still in one place, he ran helter-skelter to woods and streams, to hills and cascades and dense thickets.
3.60.38
तथा स गत्वा विपुलम् महद्वनं
परीत्य सर्वं त्वथ मैथिलीं प्रति ।
अनिष्ठिताशस्सचकार मार्गणे
पुनः प्रियायाः परमं परिश्रमम् ॥
Having gone over that wide and big jungle in his search and as his hopes regarding Maithili were faltering he doubled his efforts in search of his beloved.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अरण्यकाण्डे षष्टितमस्सर्गः.॥
Thus concludes the sixtieth Sarga in Araṇya Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
