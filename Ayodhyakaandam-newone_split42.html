Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 42
Daṡaratha is unable to take his eyes of off his departing son. As the sight of Rāma's chariot is lost on the horizon, he sinks into grief and falls on the ground. Kousalyā and Kaikēyee hold him on both sides. He tells Kaikēyee not to touch him and asks the attendants to take him to Kousalyā's house.
Lamenting about the fate of his two sons and daughter-in-law, the king sinks into grief. Kousalyā sits near him through the night, herself bitterly crying.
2.42.1
यावत्तु गच्छतस्तस्य
राजा रूपमपश्यत ।
नैवेक्ष्वाकुवरस्तावत्
संजहारात्मचक्षुषी ॥
That prominent king of Ikshwāku dynasty could not take his eyes off as long as the silhouette (of Rāma) was visible.
2.42.2
यावद्राजा प्रियं पुत्रम्
पश्यति स्म स चक्षुषा ।
उत्ससर्ज मही तावत्
तदा दूरमिवान्तरम् ॥
The horizon seemed to have stretched itself for the king to see his beloved son for longer.
2.42.3
न पश्यति रजोऽप्यस्य
यदा रामस्य भूमिपः ।
तदाऽऽर्तश्च विषण्णश्च
पपात धरणीतले ॥
Finally, when even the dust raised by (the chariot of) Rāma was visible no more, the lord of the earth fell to the ground, feeling desolate and struck by grief.
2.42.4
तस्य दक्षिणमन्वागात्
कौसल्या बाहुमङ्गना ।
वामं चास्यान्वगात्पार्श्वम्
कैकेयी भरतप्रिया ॥
Then, his wife Kousalyā held him by his right arm. And Kaikēyee, who loved Bharata, held him on the left side.
2.42.5
तां नयेन च सम्पन्नो
धर्मेण विनयेन च ।
उवाच राजा कैकेयीम्
समीक्ष्य व्यथितेन्द्रियः ॥
The king, an embodiment of justice, Dharma and humility looked at that Kaikēyee with agonized senses and said:
2.42.6
कैकेयि मा ममाङ्गानि
स्प्राक्षीस्त्वं दुष्टचारिणी ।
न हि त्वां द्रष्टुमिच्छामि
न भार्या न च बान्धवी ॥
Do not touch me, Kaikēyee, you have behaved abominably; nor do I wish to see you, you are neither wife nor related to me in any way.
2.42.7
ये च त्वामनुजीवन्ति
नाहं तेषां न ते मम ।
केवलार्थपरां हि त्वाम्
त्यक्तधर्मां त्यजाम्यहम् ॥
They who depend on you for a living are not mine; nor am I theirs. I abjure you for being obsessed with selfish gains alone, completely abandoning Dharma!
2.42.8
अगृह्णां यच्च ते पाणिम्
अग्निं पर्यणयं च यत् ।
अनुजानामि तत्सर्वम्
अस्मिन् लोके परत्र च ॥
I renounce, in this world and the worlds to come, that hand of yours which I took in mine as I led you around the fire!
2.42.9
भरतश्चेत्प्रतीतः स्यात्
राज्यं प्राप्येदमव्ययम् ।
यन्मे स दद्यात्पित्रर्थम्
मां मा तद्दत्तमागमत् ॥
If Bharata is happy over having obtained this kingdom whole and undivided, may no offering that he might make for the propitiation of my soul ever reach me!
2.42.10
अथ रेणुसमुध्वस्तम्
समुत्थाप्य नराधिपम् ।
न्यवर्तत तदा देवी
कौसल्या शोककर्शिता ॥
The lady Kousalyā, worn out with grief, raised the King up, who was all covered in dust, and turned back (towards her palace).
2.42.11
हत्वेव ब्राह्मणं कामात्
स्पृष्ट्वाग्निमिव पाणिना ।
अन्वतप्यत धर्मात्मा
पुत्रं सञ्चिन्त्य तापसम् ॥
That Dharmātma was burned by regret, like one who intentionally killed a Brāhmaṇa or touched fire by hand, as he thought of his son being in the robes of a Muni.
2.42.12
निवृत्त्यैव निवृत्त्यैव
सीदतो रथवर्त्मसु ।
राज्ञो नातिबभौ रूपम्
ग्रस्तस्यांशुमतो यथा ॥
The King's face was overcast like the sun in eclipse, as he turned back again and again looking at the tracks left by the chariot.
2.42.13
विललाप च दुःखार्तः
प्रियं पुत्रमनुस्मरन् ।
नगरान्तमनुप्राप्तम्
बुध्वा पुत्रमथाब्रवीत् ॥
Thinking of his beloved son, he lamented, overtaken by sorrow. Concluding that his beloved son should have passed beyond the city, he said:
2.42.14
वाहननां च मुख्यानाम्
वहतां तं ममात्मजम् ।
पदानि पथि दृश्यन्ते
स महात्मा न दृश्यते ॥
The tracks left on the road by those exquisite horses that carried my son can be seen, but that Mahātma himself, cannot be seen.
It is indeed pathetic to see this old king having to bear the entire responsibility, all by himself, for exiling his own son, who is at no fault and whom everyone loves dearly.
2.42.15
यः सुखेषूपधानेषु
शेते चन्दनरूषितः ।
वीज्यमानो महार्हाभिः
स्त्रीभिर्मम सुतोत्तमः ।
स नूनं क्वचिदेवाद्य
वृक्षमूलमुपाश्रितः ।
काष्ठं वा यदि वाश्मानम्
उपधाय शयिष्यते ॥
That best of my sons, who is used to reclining on luxurious cushions, fanned by beautiful women, his body smeared with sandal, will today lie down somewhere by the base of a tree, using a log of wood or a stone as the headrest.
2.42.17
उत्थास्यति च मेदिन्याः
कृपणः पांसुकुण्ठितः ।
विनिश्श्वसन् प्रस्रवणात्
करेणूनामिवर्षभः ॥
And he will rise from the ground, his body all covered with dust, dejected and heaving great sighs, like the bull elephant that had lorded over its many cows emerges from the Prasravaṇa mountain.
2.42.18
द्रक्ष्यन्ति नूनं पुरुषा
दीर्घबाहुं वनेचराः ।
राममुत्थाय गच्छन्तम्
लोकनाथमनाथवत् ॥
The forest folk will see Rāma of mighty arms, the master of the world, rise up and go like any forlorn creature.
2.42.19
सा नूनं जनकस्येष्टा
सुता सुखसदोचिता ।
कण्टकाक्रमणाक्लान्ता
वनमद्य गमिष्यति ॥
She, the beloved daughter of Janaka, accustomed to living in all comfort, will certainly walk in the forest today, sorely afflicted by treading on thorns.
2.42.20
अनभिज्ञा वनानां सा
नूनं भयमुपैष्यति ।
श्वापदानर्दितं श्रुत्वा
गम्भीरं रोमहर्षणम् ॥
Being a stranger to the Vanas, she will be terrified by the deep roar of savage beasts that would make one's hair stand on end.
2.42.21
सकामा भव कैकेयि
विधवा राज्यमावस ।
न हि तं पुरुषव्याघ्रम्
विना जीवितुमुत्सहे ॥
Ah! Kaikēyee, you will have your wish, and rule the kingdom as a widow. For, I would certainly not like to live without that tiger among men.
2.42.22
इत्येवं विलपन् राजा
जनौघेनाभिसंवृतः ।
अपस्नात इवारिष्टम्
प्रविवेश पुरोत्तमम् ॥
The King, thus lamenting, entered the great city, surrounded by large numbers of people, like a mourner who had bathed for his dead.
2.42.23
शून्यचत्वरवेश्मान्ताम्
संवृतापणदेवताम् ।
क्लान्तदुर्बलदुःखार्ताम्
नात्याकीर्णमहापथाम् ।
तामवेक्ष्य पुरीं सर्वाम्
राममेवानुचिन्तयन् ।
विलपन् प्राविशद्राजा
गृहं सूर्य इवाम्बुदम् ॥
Seeing that best of cities, which lay under a pall of misery, with its deserted squares and house fronts, its closed shops and temples, its inhabitants overwhelmed by grief, its main roads sparsely peopled, the King, thinking of Rāma and lamenting, entered his own home, as the sun enters a cloud.
2.42.25
महाह्रदमिवाक्षोभ्यम्
सुपर्णेन हृतोरगम् ।
रामेण रहितं वेश्म
वैदेह्या लक्ष्मणेन च ॥
Bereft of Rāma, Lakshmaṇa and the princess of Vidēha, that home, which had been like a lake that could hardly be approached, was now without its protective serpent, that had been snatched away by Garuḍa of majestic wings.
This Ṡlōka is terse and hence a little explanation may be helpful.
There is a beautiful lake (the palace).
It is protected by a powerful serpent (Rāma), and hence nobody could even get close to it.
But the serpent is snatched away by Garuḍa (Rāma has been taken away by destiny).
2.42.26
अथ गद्गदशब्दस्तु
विलपन्मनुजाधिपः ।
उवाच मृदुमन्दार्थम्
वचनं दीनमस्वरम् ॥
The lamenting King, his voice choked, then said in a dejected and flat melancholic tone that could hardly be deciphered:
2.42.27
कौसल्यायां गृहं शीघ्रम्
राममातुर्नयन्तु माम् ।
न ह्यन्यत्र ममाश्वासो
हृदयस्य भविष्यति ॥
Take me quickly to the house of Kousalyā, Rāma's mother. Nowhere else will my heart find relief.
2.42.28
इति ब्रुवन्तं राजानम्
अनयन् द्वारदर्शिनः ।
कौसल्याया गृहं तत्र
न्यवेश्यत विनीतवत् ॥
When he said this, the attendants deferentially took the king to the house of Kousalyā and put him on a couch.
2.42.29
ततस्तस्य प्रविष्टस्य
कौसल्याया निवेशनम् ।
अधिरुह्यापि शयनम्
बभूव लुलितं मनः ॥
Though he entered Kousalyā's house and lay comfortably on a couch, his mind was still in turmoil.
2.42.30
पुत्रद्वयविहीनं च
स्नुषयापि विवर्जितम् ।
अपश्यद्भवनं राजा
नष्टचन्द्रमिवाम्बरम् ॥
The house, bereft of his two sons and his daughter-in-law, seemed to the King like the sky that lost had the moon.
2.42.31
तच्च दृष्ट्वा महाराजो
भुजमुद्यम्य वीर्यवान् ।
उच्चैस्स्वरेण चुक्रोश
हा राघव जहासि माम् ॥
The great King looked around that house, lifted up his arms and screamed aloud, strong man as he was, 'Alas, Rāghava, you have forsaken me!'
2.42.32
सुखिता बत तं कालम्
जीविष्यन्ति नरोत्तमाः ।
परिष्वजन्तो ये रामम्
द्रक्ष्यन्ति पुनरागतम् ॥
Happy will those most fortunate men be, who live till Rāma comes back and embrace each other on seeing him!'
2.42.33
अथ रात्र्यां प्रपन्नायाम्
कालरात्र्यामिवात्मनः ।
अर्धरात्रे दशरथः
कौसल्यामिदमब्रवीत् ॥
After the fall of night, which seemed like the night of doom, Daṡaratha said to Kousalyā, at midnight:
2.42.34
रामं मेऽनुगता दृष्टिः
अद्यापि न निवर्तते ।
न त्वा पश्यामि कौसल्ये
साधु मां पाणिना स्पृश ॥
My sight that followed Rāma does not seem to have returned yet; I am not able to see you well, Kousalyā, please touch me with your hand.
2.42.35
तं राममेवानुविचिन्तयन्तं
समीक्ष्य देवी शयने नरेन्द्रम् ।
उपोपविश्याधिकमार्तरूपा
विनिश्वसन्ती विललाप कृच्छ्रम् ॥
Seeing him, who lay on the bed lost completely in the thoughts of Rāma, she, who looked like the embodiment of extreme suffering sat nearby, heaving painful sighs and lamenting bitterly.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे द्विचत्वारिंशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the forty second Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
