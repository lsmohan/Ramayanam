Kishkindha Kaanda - Sarga 27
In this Sarga, Rāma and Lakshmaṇa make a big and spacious cave on the Prasravaṇa mountain as their residence. Rāma reflects on the beauty of the mountain and the nearby river with Lakshmaṇa.
But even though the mountain is comfortable and everything is available in plenty, Rāma was unable to enjoy any of them, harrowing himself about his abducted wife who is more than life to him.
Lakshmaṇa, who is equally sad, cheers Rāma up, saying he is very capable, and with the help of Sugreeva who will do everything to meet the wishes of Rāma, he should be able to destroy Rāvaṇa, his kingdom and his clan. He goads Rāma to gain composure and bear with patience the four months of the monsoon season. Rāma accepts and follows the wholesome and gentle advice of Lakshmaṇa with due regard.
4.27.1
अभिषिक्ते तु सुग्रीवे
प्रविष्टे वानरे गुहाम् ।
आजगाम सह भ्रात्रा
रामः प्रस्रवणं गिरिम् ॥
After Sugreeva, the Vānara, was consecrated and settled in the cave, Rāma, along with his brother, went to the Prasravaṇa mountain.
4.27.2
शार्दूलमृगसङ्घुष्टम्
सिंहैर्भीमरवैर्वृतम् ।
नानागुल्मलतागूढम्
बहुपादपसङ्कुलम् ।
ऋक्षवानरगोपुच्छैः
मार्जारैश्च निषेवितम् ।
मेघराशिनिभं शैलम्
नित्यं शुचिकरं शिवम् ॥
Tigers and deer abounded there, and lions whose roar was terrifying. It was overgrown with shrubs and creepers of every kind and covered with multitudes of trees. It was the haunt of bears, monkeys, Gōpuccas and wild cats. That mountain, serene and auspicious ever, resembled a great bank of clouds.
4.27.4
तस्य शैलस्य शिखरे
महतीमायतां गुहाम् ।
प्रत्यगृह्णत वासार्थम्
रामस्सौमित्रिणा सह ॥
Rāma, along with Sowmitri, made a big and spacious cave on the top of that mountain as their residence.
4.27.5
कृत्वा च समयं रामः
सुग्रीवेण सहानघः ।
कालयुक्तं महद्वाक्यम्
उवाच रघुनन्दनः ।
विनीतं भ्रातरं भ्राता
लक्ष्मणं लक्ष्मिवर्धनम् ॥
Having made the pact with Sugreeva, Rāma, the flawless one, the delight of the Raghus, said these poignant words to his obedient brother Lakshmaṇa, augmenter of prosperity.
4.27.6
इयं गिरिगुहा रम्या
विशाला युक्तमारुता ।
अस्यां वत्स्याम सौमित्रे
वर्षरात्रमरिन्दम ॥
O Sowmitri, subduer of the foe! This mountain cave is beautiful. It is spacious and served by moderate breezes. Let us live here through the monsoon season.
4.27.7
गिरिशृङ्गमिदं रम्यम्
उन्नतं पार्थिवात्मज ।
श्वेताभिः कृष्णताम्राभिः
शिलाभिरुपशोभितम् ।
नानाधातुसमाकीर्णम्
दरीनिर्झरशोभितम् ॥
This mountain-crest is lofty and beautiful. It is splendid with black, white and copper boulders, and with the spread of minerals of every kind. It is gorgeous with the caverns and water falls.
4.27.9
विविधैर्वृक्षषण्डैश्च
चारुचित्रलतायुतम् ।
नानाविहगसङ्घुष्टम्
मयूररवनादितम् ।
मालतीकुन्दगुल्मैश्च
सिन्धुवारैश्शिरीषकैः ।
कदम्बार्जुनसर्जैश्च
पुष्पितैरुपशोभितम् ॥
If is full of groves of various kinds of trees, and lovely and amazing creepers. It resounds with the sounds of birds of every kind and reverberates with the cawing of peacocks. Its beauty is enhanced by its flowering trees and shrubs, Mālati, Kunda, Sindhuvāra, Ṡireeshaka, Kadamba, Arjuna and Sarja.
4.27.11
इयं च नलिनी रम्या
पुल्लपङ्कजमण्डिता ।
नातिदूरे गुहायानौ
भविष्यति नृपात्मज ॥
This lotus pool, adorned with lotuses in full blossom, will be within easy reach of our cave, O prince.
4.27.12
प्रागुदक्प्रवणे देशे
गुहा साधु भविष्यति ।
पश्चाच्चैवोन्नता सौम्य
निवातेयं भविष्यति ॥
This mountain cave, on the slopes in the northeast direction, raised at the back, my dear, will be free from high winds.
4.27.13
गुहाद्वारे च सौमित्रे
शिला समतला शुभा ।
श्लक्ष्णा चैवायता चैव
भिन्नाञ्जनचयोपमा ॥
At the entrance to the cave, O Sowmitri, the rocky surface is level; it is smooth and spacious like a mound of well prepared colliriyum.
4.27.14
गिरिशृङ्गमिदं तात
पश्य चोत्तरतः शुभम् ।
भिन्नाञ्जनचयाकारम्
अम्भोधरमिवोत्थितम् ॥
Look at the serene mountain-crest in the north, my dear, which resembles a huge mound of colliriyum and looks like a heavy cloud that stood up (on its side).
4.27.15
दक्षिणस्यामपि दिशि
स्थितं श्वेतमिवाम्बरम् ।
कैलासशिखरप्रख्यम्
नानाधातुविभूषितम् ॥
Even to the south, there is a mountain rivalling the peak of Kailāsa standing like a white cloud, adorned by many kinds of ores.
4.27.16
प्राचीनवाहिनीं चैव
नदीं भृशमकर्दमाम् ।
गुहायाः पूर्वतः पश्य
त्रिकूटे जाह्नवीमिव ॥
And look at the river that flows to the east in front of the cave, and is wholly free from mud like the Jahnavi at Trikūta.
4.27.17
चन्दनैस्तिलकैस्तालैः
तमालैरतिमुक्तकैः ।
पद्मकैः सरलैश्चैव
अशोकैश्चैव शोभिताम् ।
वानीरैस्तिमिशैश्चैव
वकुलैः केतकैर्धवैः ।
हिन्तालैस्तिनिशैर्नीपैः
वेतसैः कृतमालकैः ।
तीरजैश्शोभिता भाति
नानारूपै स्ततस्ततः ।
वसनाभरणोपेता
प्रमदेवाभ्यलङ्कृता ॥
It is like a woman gorgeously bedecked with garments and ornaments, adorned with rows of a variety of trees, Campaka, Tilaka, palm, Tamāla, Atimukta, Padmaka, Sarala and Aṡōka, Vāneera, Timida, Vakula, Kētaka, Dhava, Hintāla, Tiniṡa, Neepa, Vētasa, grown here and there on its banks.
4.27.20
शतशः पक्षिसङ्घैश्च
नानानादैर्विनादिता ।
एकैकमनुरक्तैश्च
चक्रवाकैरलङ्कृता ।
पुलिनैरतिरम्यैश्च
हंससारससेवितैः ।
प्रहसन्तीवभात्येषा
नारी सर्वविभूषिता ॥
Adorned with pairs of Cakravākas in love, its sandflats favored by swans and Sārasas, reverberating with the clamor of hundreds of flocks of birds of different notes, it shines like a woman bedecked all over, laughing.
4.27.22
क्वचिन्नीलोत्पलैश्छन्ना
भाति रक्तोत्पलैः क्वचित् ।
क्वचिदाभाति शुक्लैश्च
दिव्यैः कुमुदकुड्मलैः ॥
At places, it is covered with blue lilies. At others, it shines with red lilies. And at even others, it shines with white ones. And in others, it is splendid with Kumuda buds.
4.27.23
पारिप्लवशतैर्जुष्टा
बर्हिक्रौञ्चविनादिता ।
रमणीया नदी सौम्य
मुनिसङ्घैर्निषेविता ॥
With its hundreds of aquatic birds, reverberating with the cries of peacocks and Krouncas and hosts of Munis visiting it, the river is enchanting, my dear!
We do not quite realize, but even humans are cute like birds, when seen in natural and serene environment involved in their simple and orderly activities.
The beauty of the river is enhanced not only by the birds, but also by the Munis.
4.27.24
पश्य चन्दनवृक्षाणाम्
पङ्क्तीस्सुरचिता इव ।
ककुभानां च दृश्यन्ते
मनसेवोदितास्समम् ॥
Look at the sandal trees, so beautiful, in orderly rows; and those Kakubha trees, so perfectly alike, as if they are born at once, out of imagination.
There is a philosophical and scientific underpinning for the usage of ‘perfectly alike ... coming out of imagination’ in this Ṡlōka.
The philosophical question here is, can any two things be created one hundred percent identical? Both modern scientists and ancient philosophers agree that if the initial conditions are not identical, the resulting products will not be identical.
In a universe that is constantly changing, there can never be two identical ‘initial conditions’. Every condition is different from the other, separated either by space or time. That is why assembly lines in the manufacturing plants have to go to extreme lengths, to make the products one hundred percent identical.
Ancient Indians pondered about things being ‘identical’, and philosophized that even Brahma cannot create two identical things (Bhūtas), unless the mind conceives them at the very same moment and they take shape instantly, with no lag whatsoever.
4.27.25
अहो सुरमणीयोऽयम्
देशश्शत्रुनिषूदन ।
दृढं रंस्याव सौमित्रे
साध्वत्र निवसावहै ॥
Ah! What an enchanting place this is, O slayer of the foe! Let us spend our time here, Sowmitri! We are going to enjoy thoroughly.
4.27.26
इतश्च नातिदूरे सा
किष्किन्धा चित्रकानना ।
सुग्रीवस्य पुरी रम्या
भविष्यति नृपात्मज ॥
And even Kishkindhā of lovely woods, the beautiful city of Sugreeva, is not too far from here, O prince!
4.27.27
गीतवादित्रनिर्घोषः
श्रूयते जयतां वर ।
नर्दतां वानराणां च
मृदङ्गाडम्बरै स्सह ॥
O foremost of the victorious! Even the sounds of song and music from there can be heard (here), along with the shouts of Vānaras and the sounds of drums!
4.27.28
लब्ध्वा भार्यां कपिवरः
प्राप्य राज्यं सुहृद्वृतः ।
ध्रुवं नन्दति सुग्रीवः
सम्प्राप्य महतीं श्रियम् ॥
Sugreeva, regaining his wife, and also obtaining the kingdom and abundance of wealth, is certainly enjoying along with his friends.
4.27.29
इत्युक्त्वा न्यवसत्तत्र
राघवस्सहलक्ष्मणः ।
बहुदृश्यदरीकुञ्जे
तस्मिन्प्रस्रवणे गिरौ ॥
Reflecting thus, Rāghava along with Lakshmaṇa made that Prasravaṇa mountain of many caves and bushes, his place of residence.
NSS and NCC cadets in India and Scouts in USA are taught how to live in wilderness, all by themselves, when needed. It seems all the princes of Rāma’s times were also taught the same. They were able to put up a camp anywhere and live.
4.27.30
सुसुखेऽपि बहुद्रव्ये
तस्मिन्हि धरणीधरे ।
वसतस्तस्य रामस्य
रतिरल्पाऽपि नाभवत् ।
हृतां हि भार्यां स्मरतः
प्राणेभ्योऽपि गरीयसीम् ।
उदयाभ्युदितं दृष्ट्वा
शशाङ्कं च विशेषतः ॥
Rāma lived on that comfortable mountain which offered everything in plenty. But he could not enjoy even a bit of it, not even the moon rising from behind the mountain to the east, as he thought of his abducted wife, who was more than life for him.
4.27.32
आविवेश न तं निद्रा
निशासु शयनं गतम् ।
तत्समुत्थेन शोकेन
बाष्पोपहतचेतसम् ॥
With his mind wrecked by those thoughts and the grief and the tears, he was unable to sleep at night, lying on the bed.
4.27.33
तं शोचमानं काकुत्स्थम्
नित्यं शोकपरायणम् ।
तुल्यदुःखोऽब्रवीद्भ्राता
लक्ष्मणोऽनुनयन्वचः ॥
Lakshmaṇa, his brother, who was equally sad, said these words to cheer up the incessantly grieving Kākutstha:
4.27.34
अलं वीर व्यथां गत्वा
न त्वं शोचितुमर्हसि ।
शोचतह्यवसीदन्ति
सर्वार्था विदितं हि ते ॥
You have been harrowing yourself for too long, O Veera! You shall not cave in to grief, for it will bring everything to a ruin; and you know this very well.
4.27.35
भवान्क्रियापरो लोके
भवान् देवपरायणः ।
आस्तिको धर्मशीलश्च
व्यवसायी च राघव ॥
You have (always) been a man of action in this world, O Rāghava! You are also well aware of the hand of Providence. You believe in the order of things. You cling to Dharma and you (always) take initiative.
4.27.36
न ह्यव्यवसितश्शत्रुम्
राक्षसं तं विशेषतः ।
समर्थस्त्वं रणे हन्तुम्
विक्रमैर्जिह्मकारिणम् ॥
Without concerted effort, you will not be able to kill the enemy, that too a Rākshasa, who resorts to (every sort of) deception in a fight.
4.27.37
समुन्मूलय शोकं त्वम्
व्यवसायं स्थिरं कुरु ।
ततस्सपरिवारं तम्
राक्षसं हन्तुमर्हसि ॥
Tear out your sorrow by the roots and put forth a determined effort. You shall kill that Rākshasa including all his kith and kin.
4.27.38
पृथिवीमपि काकुत्स्थ
ससागरवनाचलाम् ।
परिवर्तयितुं शक्तः
किं पुनस्तं रावणम् ॥
O Kākutstha! You can turn even the earth, with all its Vanas, mountains and oceans upside down. What to speak of (taking on) Rāvaṇa?
4.27.39
शरत्कालं प्रतीक्षस्व
प्रावृट्कालोऽयमागतः ।
ततस्सराष्ट्रं सगणम्
रावणं त्वं वधिष्यसि ॥
This monsoon season has come in between; wait for the arrival of post-monsoon season. You shall, then destroy Rāvaṇa, his realm and his clan.
4.27.40
अहं तु खलु ते वीर्यम्
प्रसुप्तं प्रतिबोधये ।
दीप्तैराहुतिभिः काले
भस्मच्छन्नमिवानलम् ॥
I am merely rousing your dormant might, as one does the fire covered in ash with inflammable oblations now and then.
4.27.41
लक्ष्मणस्य तु तद्वाक्यम्
प्रतिपूज्य हितं शुभम् ।
राघवस्सुहृदं स्निग्धम्
इदं वचनमब्रवीत् ॥
Duly respecting those meaningful and nicely spoken words, Rāghava said these words to the affectionate and good-hearted (Lakshmaṇa):
4.27.42
वाच्यं यदनुरक्तेन
स्निग्धेन च हितेन च ।
सत्यविक्रमयुक्तेन
तदुक्तं लक्ष्मण त्वया ॥
You have spoken, O Lakshmaṇa, what is expected from a man of true valor who is friendly, means well and is sincerely interested (in me).
4.27.43
एष शोकः परित्यक्तः
सर्वकार्यावसादकः ।
विक्रमेष्वप्रतिहतम्
तेजः प्रोत्साहयाम्यहम् ॥
I shall give up this grief that only brings everything to naught. I shall stir the power that makes me prevail against all odds.
4.27.44
शरत्कालं प्रतीक्षिष्ये
स्थितोऽस्मि वचने तव ।
सुग्रीवस्य नदीनां च
प्रसादमनुपालयन् ॥
I shall wait for the post-monsoon season, abiding by the advice you have given. I will wait for the grace of the rivers and that of Sugreeva.
4.27.45
उपकारेण वीरस्तु
प्रतीकारेण युज्यते ।
अकृतज्ञोऽप्रतिकृतो
हन्ति सत्त्ववतां मनः ॥
A Veera always pays back the favor he has received; those who do not remember or reciprocate will certainly hurt the feelings of good men.
4.27.46
अथैवमुक्तः प्रणिधाय लक्ष्मणः
कृताञ्जलिस्तत्प्रतिपूज्य भाषितम् ।
उवाच रामं स्वभिरामदर्शनं
प्रदर्शयन्दर्शनमात्मनश्शुभम् ॥
Lakshmaṇa, thus told, joining his palms in reverence, accepted, with due regard, what had been said. Showing his good feelings in his eyes, he said to Rāma of charming looks:
4.27.47
यथोक्तमेतत्तव सर्वमीप्सितं
नरेन्द्र कर्ता न चिराद्धरीश्वरः ।
शरत्प्रतीक्षः क्षमतामिमं भवान्
जलप्रपातं रिपुनिग्रहे धृतः ॥
O lord of men! The Lord of Vānaras will, not too long from now, do all that you wish and said. May you hold yourself patiently till these torrential rains come to an end. Please wait for the arrival of post-monsoon season, fully resolved to destroy the foe.
4.27.48
नियम्य कोपं प्रतिपाल्यतां शरत्
क्षमस्व मासां श्चतुरो मया सह ।
वसाचलेऽस्मिन्मृगराजसेविते
संवर्तयन् शत्रुवधे समुद्यमम् ॥
Restrain your anger and wait for the post-monsoon season, spending the next four months as best you can, living along with me on this mountain, a lion’s resort, resolved on the destruction of the foe.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
किष्किन्धाकाण्डे सप्तविंशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the twenty seventh Sarga in Kishkindhā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
