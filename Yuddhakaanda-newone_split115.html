Yuddha Kaanda - Sarga 115
In this Sarga, which presents a dramatic turn of events, Rāma puts out what he has kept to himself thus far.
He tells Seetā that he, as the scion of a great lineage and as a self-respecting individual, is clear that he does not want to do anything more with her. He makes it clear that he cannot take back a woman who was obscenely touched by another man, Rāvaṇa, who cannot be expected to have let a woman of divine beauty in the house to herself.
He tells that he did all that he did to restore the renown of himself and his dynasty, which had been challenged by Rāvaṇa. He expresses his resentment over the entire ordeal he had to go through in this process. He says that he feels better for having brought things to a logical closure, so that he can move on with his life.
6.115.1
तां तु पार्श्वस्थितां प्रह्वाम्
रामः सम्प्रेक्ष्य मैथिलीम् ।
हृदयान्तर्गतक्रोधो
व्याहर्तुमुपचक्रमे ॥
Looking at Maithili, who stood by his side humbly, Rāma began to express the resentment that he held deep in his heart:
6.115.2
एषाऽसि निर्जिता भद्रे
शत्रुं जित्वा रणाजिरे ।
पौरुषाद्यदनुष्ठेयम्
तदेतदुपपादितम् ॥
My dear, the foe is vanquished and you are won in the battle. I have done what behooves someone worthy of being called a man.
What did Rāma feel on the day when Seetā was abducted? One, loss and two, shame and outrage.
Rāma cried out loud over the loss of Seetā, like any other ordinary man who loses a loved one, which is depicted extensively in the last part of Araṇya Kāṇḍa, much of Kishkindhā Kāṇḍa and in the beginning of this Yuddha Kāṇḍa.
The incident made Rāma hang his head in shame, as one who could not safeguard his own wife. He was outraged that one like him, the scion of a great dynasty that ruled the earth, was challenged. Rāma kept this shame and rage to himself, and it was not depicted thus far in the narration. It is the sort of shame that he could not speak out openly to even his own brother, Lakshmaṇa.
Sometimes, it may be difficult to get a feel for this deep sense of shame that Rāma feels. An analogy might help to understand it. Imagine the situation of a parent whose child goes missing. The world sympathizes with the parent over the loss outwardly, but it also blames the parent silently for the carelessness, or whatever other reason, that caused the child to have gone missing. The parent, in addition to the loss, suffers the shame also, albeit silently.
6.115.3
गतोऽस्म्यन्तममर्षस्य
धर्षणा सम्प्रमार्जिता ।
अवमानश्च शत्रुश्च
युगपन्निहतै मया ॥
I have taken my wrath to its logical end. All aggression against me is wiped out. The humiliation meted by the foe and the foe himself were obliterated at once by me.
6.115.4
अद्य मे पौरुषं दृष्टम
अद्य मे सफलः श्रमः ।
अद्य तीर्णप्रतिज्ञोऽहम्
प्रभवाम्यद्य चात्मनः ॥
Today, the world saw my mettle. My efforts have paid off, my vows are fulfilled, and I can go about with my life.
6.115.5
या त्वं विरहिता नीता
चलचित्तेन रक्षसा ।
दैवसम्पादितो दोषो
मानुषेण मया जितः ॥
The lapse that occurred as Providence willed, when you were carried off in my absence by the fickle-minded Rākshasa, is now undone by me, a human.
6.115.6
सम्प्राप्तमवमानं यः
तेजसा न प्रमार्जति ।
कस्तस्य पुरुषेणार्थो
महताप्यल्पचेतसः ॥
If one, using all that is in his power, cannot erase the slight that is put on him, of what use is the huge ego of such a weakling?
6.115.7
लङ्घनं च समुद्रस्य
लङ्कायाश्चावमर्दनम् ।
सफलं तस्य च श्लाध्यम्
अद्य कर्म हनूमतः ॥
The successful crossing of the ocean and pummeling of Laṅkā by Hanumān stands out today, worthy of all praise.
Rāma is putting out in the open all the efforts gone into recovering Seetā, this far.
Again, to appreciate why Rāma is saying this, imagine the feelings of a parent after recovering a missing child. On the one hand the parent will be overwhelmed with the joy of recovering the child, but on the other, the parent, at that moment, would recall the entire ordeal of recovering the child that might have lasted days or weeks, and also the help rendered by everyone in that process.
6.115.8
युद्धे विक्रमतश्चैव
हितं मन्त्रयतश्च मे ।
सुग्रीवस्य ससैन्यस्य
सफलोऽद्य परिश्रमः ॥
The prowess shown by Sugreeva in the battle, his strategies that helped my advancement, and the efforts of his forces, all have borne fruit today.
6.115.9
निर्गुणं भ्रातरं त्यक्त्वा
यो मां स्वयमुपस्थितः ।
विभीषणस्य भक्तस्य
सफलोऽद्य परिश्रमः ॥
The efforts of Vibheeshaṇa who left his brother who lacked all virtues, and devoted himself to me of his own accord, have borne fruit today.
6.115.10
इत्येवं ब्रुवतः श्रुत्वा
सीता रामस्य तद्वचः ।
मृगीवोत्फुल्लनयना
बभूवाश्रुपरिप्लुता ॥
On hearing those words of Rāma who was speaking thus, Seetā's eyes became wide like those of a (startled) fawn, and were filled with tears.
6.115.11
पश्यतस्तां तु रामस्य
भूयः क्रोधो व्यवर्धत ।
प्रभूताज्यावसिक्तस्य
पावकस्येव दीप्यतः ॥
The longer Rāma looked at her the more his anger raged, like fire raging when ghee is added.
6.115.12
स बद्ध्वा भ्रुकुटीं वक्त्रे
तिर्यक्प्रेक्षितलोचनः ।
अब्रवीत् परुषं सीताम्
मध्ये वानररक्षसाम् ॥
Then, with a face that had eyebrows knotted and eyes looking with suspicion, he spoke these harsh words to Seetā, in the midst of Vānaras and Rākshasas:
6.115.13
यत् कर्तव्यं मनुष्येण
धर्षणां परिमार्जता ।
तत् कृतं सकलं सीते
शत्रुहस्तादमर्षणात् ॥
I have done, O Seetā, all that one who would want to rout out the aggression of the enraged foe, should do.
6.115.14
पश्यतस्तां तु रामस्य
समीपे हृदयप्रियाम् ।
जनवादभयाद्राज्ञो
बभूव हृदयं द्विधा ॥
As he looked at her, the love of his heart, who stood near him, and as he became apprehensive of public opinion, Rāma, the king, found his heart pulled in two different directions.
These three Ṡlōkas are found in some editions in the place of the above three Ṡlōkas.
6.115.15
सीतामुत्पलपत्राक्षीं
नीलकुञ्चितमूर्धजाम् ।
अवदद्वै वरारोहां
मध्ये वानररक्षसाम् ॥
Then he said, in the midst of Vānaras and Rākshasas, to Seetā of lily petal like eyes, curly black tresses and lovely Kaṭee:
6.115.16
यत्कर्तव्यं मनुष्येण
धर्षणां प्रतिमार्जता ।
तत्कृतं रावणं हत्वा
मयेदं मानकाङ्क्क्षिणा ॥
I have done what is expected of a man who wants to protect his self-respect; I slew Rāvaṇa and wiped out the slight that was laid on me.
6.115.17
निर्जिता जीवलोकस्य
तपसा भावितात्मना ।
अगस्त्येन दुराधर्षा
मुनिना दक्षिणेव दिक् ॥
You are won, like the South, which was inaccessible to the beings of the entire world, was won by the self-realized sage Agastya, with the power of his Tapa.
6.115.18
विदितश्चास्तु ते भद्रे
योऽयं रणपरिश्रमः ।
सुतीर्णः सुहृदां वीर्यात्
न त्वदर्थं मया कृतः ।
रक्षता तु मया वृत्तम्
अपावदं च सर्वतः ।
प्रख्यातस्यात्मवंशस्य
न्यङ्गं च परिरक्षता ॥
Understand, good dame, that this great endeavor of war, which was brought to a successful end, because of the valor of my allies, was not fought because I wanted you back, but to protect my character from aspersions from every quarter and and to save my renowned family from disgrace.
6.115.20
प्राप्तचारित्रसन्देहा
मम प्रतिमुखे स्थिता ।
दीपो नेत्रातुरस्येव
प्रतिकूलाऽसि मे दृढम् ॥
Now that there has arisen a scope for suspecting your record, your presence in front of me is as painful to me as that of a lamp for one who is suffering from an eyesore.
Earlier, we took the analogy of a parent with a missing child to gain an appreciation for Rāma's situation. But that analogy breaks down here for two reasons.
One, Rāma is not just another ordinary person, but the scion of great dynasty, responsible for protecting its name and fame falling into disgrace. Two, it is not a child who went missing, but a wife, a grown up woman, who happened to stay in someone else's house for a long time.
It is interesting that Vālmeeki describes at length how woefully Rāma weeps for her beloved in the forests and how woefully Seetā weeps for her beloved in the Asoka Vana, but does not bring this underlying issue of taking back a woman who was at someone else's house, till this point, perhaps to let the dramatic turn of events play itself out.
6.115.21
तद्गच्छ त्वानुजानेऽद्य
यथेष्टं जनकात्मजे ।
एता दश दिशो भद्रे
कार्यमस्ति न मे त्वया ॥
Hence, O daughter of Janaka, go anywhere as you like and you have my consent. All the ten directions are open to you, and I have nothing to do with you any more.
6.115.22
कः पुमान् हि कुले जातः
स्त्रियं परगृहोषिताम् ।
तेजस्वी पुनरादद्यात्
सुहृल्लेख्येन चेतसा ॥
Which man of power and noble ancestry would clamor for taking back a woman who happened to live in someone else's house?
6.115.23
रावणाङ्कपरिक्लिष्टाम्
दृष्टां दुष्टेन चक्षुषा ।
कथं त्वां पुनरादद्याम्
कुलं व्यपदिशन् महत् ॥
How can I, priding myself on my descent, take you, who have been squeezed on the lap of Rāvaṇa and have been glanced by his wicked eye, back?
6.115.24
तदर्थं निर्जिता मे त्वम्
यशः प्रत्याहृतं मया ।
नास्ति मे त्वय्यभिष्वङ्गो
यथेष्टं गम्यतामितः ॥
The reason for winning you back is to restore the renown. I do not have any interest in you; please go wherever you like from here.
Seetā is already aware how important it is for Rāma to restore his renown. That is the reason why she did not take up on the offer of Hanumān to carry her back from the Aṡōka Vana.
The renown for a king is similar to the 'Approval Ratings' of a Prime Minister or President of a country today. It means a lot to them. Only a king with spotless reputation is respected and his regime lasts long. Others will be laughed at behind their backs.
We tend to think the kingdoms of the bygone eras were feudal fiefdoms and that we have progressed nowadays, by leaps and bounds, into Democracy. But the kingdoms were not fiefdoms. They worked under the pressure of public opinion, at least in the days of Rāma. In fact, they cared for public opinion throughout their regime / reign, not just till they got into power, as the politicians of today do.
6.115.25
तदद्य व्याहृतं भद्रे
मयैतत् कृतबुद्धिना ।
लक्ष्मणे भरते वा त्वम्
कुरु बुद्धिं यथासुखम् ॥
I say this, my dear, with my mind firmly made up. Consider turning to Lakshmaṇa or Bharata for your safety and well being.
6.115.26
शत्रुघ्ने वाथ सुग्रीवे
राक्षसे वा विभीषणे ।
निवेशय मनः सीते
यथा वा सुखमात्मनः ॥
You may likewise consider in your mind, Ṡatrughna or Sugreeva, or Vibheeshaṇa, the Ruler of the Rākshasas, as you feel comfortable.
6.115.27
न हि त्वां रावणो दृष्ट्वा
दिव्यरूपां मनोरमाम् ।
मर्षयेत चिरं सीते
स्वगृहे पर्यवस्थिताम् ॥
Would Rāvaṇa have let you alone for long having set his eyes on you, a divine beauty who is so charming, living right in his own house?
Rāma lays out clearly the suspicion that would rise in anyone's mind.
6.115.28
ततः प्रियार्हश्रवणा तदप्रियं
प्रियादुपश्रुत्य चिरस्य मैथिली ।
मुमोच बाष्पं सुभृशं प्रवेपिता
गजेन्द्रहस्ताभिहतेव वल्लरी ॥
Hearing those unpleasant words from her beloved, Maithili, who deserved to hear nothing but the most pleasing words, shed tears for a long while, shaking violently like a creeper ravaged by an elephant with its trunk.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
युद्धकाण्डे पञ्चदशोत्तरशततमः सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the one hundred and fifteenth Sarga in Yuddha Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
Why does no one plead with Rāma to reconsider his decision at this juncture? Back in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa, when Kaikēyee asked for the two boons, everyone, from Daṡaratha to Sumantra pleaded with her to reconsider her demands. Why did neither Sugreeva, nor Vibheeshaṇa, nor Lakshmaṇa, nor Jāmbavān or any other elder not plead with Rāma to reconsider here?
Because Rāma's decision is not driven by impulse or worry like it was the case with Kaikēyee's demands. Rāma cannot see how he, as the scion of a famed dynasty, can take back a wife who had been touched by the lusty Rāvaṇa and had been captive in his house for so long.
And everyone understands this as clearly as Rāma does. Vibheeshaṇa could have asserted that Rāvaṇa did not touch her after lodging her in the Aṡōka Vana, but he himself cannot vouch for everything that was going in Rāvaṇa's palace. Hanumān too, only had a glimpse of her life for one day, and cannot vouch for the happenings all through this period in Rāvaṇa's palace. Hence they have no case to make against Rāma's decision.
Vālmeeki says a lot without saying.
