Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 24
In this Sarga, Rāma convinces the crying Kousalyā, who wants to follow him to the Vana, that the right thing for her is to stay back and serve her husband. He assures her that Bharata would take good care of her and it would all end well, when he returns from the Vana.
Finding the resolve of Rāma to go to the Vana unshakable, Kousalyā wishes him well, saying that she would wait, with heavy and grief stricken heart, for the day when he comes back.
2.24.1
तं समीक्ष्य त्ववहितम्
पितुर्निर्देश पालने ।
कौसल्या बाष्पसंरुद्धा
वचो धर्मिष्ठमब्रवीत् ॥
Finding that he was firmly bent upon carrying out his father's behest, Kousalyā, with tears interjecting her voice, uttered these very righteous words:
2.24.2
अदृष्टदुःखो धर्मात्मा
सर्वभूतप्रियंवदः ।
मयि जातो दशरथात्
कथमुञ्छेन वर्तयेत् ॥
How could he, the Dharmātma, whom I bore to Daṡaratha, who has never known sorrow, who speaks sweetly to all that lives, live by picking up scattered grain?
2.24.3
यस्य भृत्याश्च दासाश्च
मृष्टान्यन्नानि भुञ्जते ।
कथं स भोक्ष्यतेऽनाथो
वने मूलफलान्ययम् ॥
How could the master, even whose attendants and staff enjoy sumptuous food, survive in the Vana eating fruits and roots, like an orphan?
2.24.4
कः एतच्छ्रद्दधेच्छ्रुत्वा
कस्य वा न भवेद्भयम् ।
गुणवान्दयितो राज्ञा
राघवो यद्विवास्यते ॥
How can anyone believe that Rāghava, such a noble person, especially a dear one, is being sent in exile by the king? How can anyone not be seized by fear?
2.24.5
नूनं तु बलवान् लोके
कृतान्तस्सर्वमादिशन् ।
लोके रामाभिरामस्त्वम्
वनं यत्र गमिष्यसि ॥
Indeed, the Providence that dictates everything, is all powerful! How else could you, O Rāma, the darling of all people, be destined for the Vanas?
2.24.6
अयं तु मामात्मभव
स्तवादर्शनमारुतः ।
विलापदुःखसमिधो
रुदिताश्रुहुताहुतिः ।
चिन्ताबाष्पमहाधूमः
तवागमनचित्तजः ।
कर्शयित्वा भृशं पुत्र
निश्वासायाससम्भवः ।
त्वया विहीनामिह माम्
शोकाग्निरतुलो महान् ।
प्रधक्ष्यति यथा कक्षम्
चित्रभानुर्हिमात्यये ॥
From the sighs of yearning for your return a mighty and unparalleled fire of grief will rage in me and it will emaciate me.
It will burn me out like the fire in high summer, would the thicket. The denial of the sight of your face will fan it. My lamentations and sorrow will be its firewood. The tears out of my crying will be the oblations. My pitiful choking will be its smoke.
2.24.9
कथं हि धेनुः स्वं वत्सम्
गच्छन्तं नानुगच्छति ।
अहं त्वानुगमिष्यामि
यत्र पुत्र गमिष्यसि ॥
How can a cow not follow its calf, wherever it goes? I will also follow you, my son, wherever you go!
Why is the cow held as a symbol of motherhood? The reason is simple. The calves of other animals follow the mother. Whereas, it is the other way around, in the case of cows. The mother follows the calf, wherever the calf wanders off.
2.24.10
तथा निगदितं मात्रा
तद्वाक्यं पुरुषर्षभः ।
श्रुत्वा रामोऽब्रवीद्वाक्यम्
मातरं भृशदुःखिताम् ॥
Hearing those words that flowed from his mother, Rāma, a bull among men, felt very sad and said:
2.24.11
कैकेय्या वञ्चितो राजा
मयि चारण्यमाश्रिते ।
भवत्या च परित्यक्तो
न नूनं वर्तयिष्यति ॥
The king, deceived by Kaikēyee, and with me gone to the forest cannot survive, for sure, if you also leave him.
2.24.12
भर्तुः किल परित्यागो
नृशंसः केवलं स्त्रियाः ।
स भवत्या न कर्तव्यो
मनसाऽपि विगर्हितः ॥
It will be nothing but cruel for a woman to abandon her husband! May that abominable act not even cross your mind!
2.24.13
यावज्जीवति काकुत्स्थः
पिता मे जगतीपतिः ।
शुश्रूषा क्रियतां तावत्
सहि धर्मस्सनातनः ॥
May my father, the descendant of Kākutstha, the ruler of the worlds, be served well as long as he is alive; for that is the time honored Dharma!
2.24.14
एवमुक्ता तु रामेण
कौसल्या शुभदर्शना ।
तथेत्युवाच सुप्रीता
राममक्लिष्टकारिणम् ॥
With those words of Rāma, Kousalyā cheered up. That auspicious lady, then said, 'May it be so!' to Rāma, who knew how to ease up things.
2.24.15
एवमुक्तस्तु वचनम्
रामो धर्मभृतां वरः ।
भूयस्तामब्रवीद्वाक्यम्
मातरं भृशदुःखिताम् ॥
Having heard her assent, Rāma, the patron of Dharma, again said to his mother, who was very sad:
2.24.16
मया चैव भवत्या च
कर्तव्यं वचनं पितुः ।
राजा भर्ता गुरु श्श्रेष्ठः
सर्वेषामीश्वरः प्रभुः ॥
It behooves me as well as you to abide by the words of father. For he is a venerable elder, our provider and the lord of all peoples.
2.24.17
इमानि तु महारण्ये
विहृत्य नव पञ्च च ।
वर्षाणि परमप्रीतः
स्थास्यामि वचने तव ॥
After spending nine and five years in the great forest, I will do whatever you wish, happily.
2.24.18
एवमुक्ता प्रियं पुत्रम्
बाष्पपूर्णानना तदा ।
उवाच परमार्ता तु
कौसल्या पुत्रवत्सला ॥
Responding to those words, Kousalyā, feeling very sad, with tears in her eyes, said to her dear son, out of motherly affection:
2.24.19
आसां राम सपत्नीनाम्
वस्तुं मध्ये न मे क्षमम् ।
नय मामपि काकुत्स्थ
वनं वन्यां मृगीं यथा ।
यदि ते गमने बुद्धिः
कृता पितुरपेक्षया ॥
O Rāma! I will not be able to manage in the midst of all of these co-wives. O scion of Kākutstha dynasty! If you are firm in your mind to go to the forest to fulfill your father's wish, consider me as a deer that belongs to the forest and take me with you!
2.24.20
तां तथा रुदतीं रामो
रुदन्वचनमब्रवीत् ॥
To that lady who was thus crying, Rāma said, himself crying:
Kousalyā is the first among the queens. Yet she is soft and not the one to exercise her power to command over others. On the other hand, she is worried that her co-wives would mistreat her, as she explicitly stated earlier (2.20.39). She has endured it for years as Daṡaratha leaned towards Kaikēyee, and knows about it all too well.
So, her mind is filled again with the apprehension that she might be mistreated by her co-wives, in the absence of Rāma.
Rāma deflects her fear by bringing her focus on to a genuine and nobler purpose that she can live for, that of taking care of her husband, by exalting the virtues of serving the husband.
He also allays her fears that the regime of Bharata could especially be difficult for her, by saying that Bharata is a Dharmātma, who cares for everyone's wellbeing.
This is an example of how Rāma eases things (as stated in 2.24.14), yet being truthful and honest about what he says.
2.24.20
जीवन्त्या हि स्त्रिया भर्ता
दैवतं प्रभुरेव च ॥
Husband is the lord and destiny for a woman in her life!
2.24.21
भवत्या मम चैवाद्य
राजा प्रभवति प्रभुः ।
न ह्यनाथा वयं राज्ञा
लोकनाथेन धीमता ॥
The king who is the lord of all, is also our lord. We are not destitute, as long as the diligent king is taking care of everyone.
2.24.22
भरतश्चापि धर्मात्मा
सर्वभूतप्रियंवदः ।
भवतीमनुवर्तेत
स हि धर्मरतस्सदा ॥
Bharata is also a Dharmātma. He adheres to Dharma always. He speaks for the welfare of every being. He will take good care of you.
2.24.23
यथा मयि तु निष्क्रान्ते
पुत्रशोकेन पार्थिवः ।
श्रमं नावाप्नुयात्किञ्चित्
अप्रमत्ता तथा कुरु ॥
After I leave, may you, vigilantly do whatever is required to keep the king off the stress of the sorrow of not having his son around!
2.24.24
दारुणश्चाप्ययं शोको
यथैनं न विनाशयेत् ।
राज्ञो वृद्धस्य सततम्
हितं चर समाहिता ॥
Do constantly, in full diligence, the best for the aged king, so that this dreadful misery would not destroy him!
2.24.25
व्रतोपवासनिरता
या नारी परमोत्तमा ।
भर्तारं नानुवर्तेत
सा तु पापगतिर्भवेत् ॥
A woman, even if she is of the best character and observant of all Vratas and fasts, would only end up in evil hell, if she is not in step with him.
2.24.26
भर्तु श्शुश्रूषया नारी
लभते स्वर्गमुत्तमम् ।
अपि या निर्नमस्कारा
निवृत्ता देवपूजनात् ॥
On the other hand, a woman who serves her husband, would attain Heaven, the best of all, even if she stops doing Pūjas and offering salutations to the Dēvas!
2.24.27
शुश्रूषामेव कुर्वीत
भर्तुः प्रियहिते रता ।
एष धर्मः पुरा दृष्टो
लोके वेदे श्रुतः स्मृतः ॥
A woman shall serve her husband with complete interest in his welfare. This is seen as the Dharma from time immemorial in the world. It is what is heard from Vedas and found in Smṛtis.
2.24.28
अग्निकार्येषु च सदा
सुमनोभिश्च देवताः ।
पूज्यास्ते मत्कृते देवि
ब्राह्मणाश्चैव सुव्रताः ॥
For my sake, please perform Pūjas, fire rituals and flower offerings to the Dēvas. And may you also honor Brāhmaṇas, who perform good Vratas.
2.24.29
एवं कालं प्रतीक्षस्व
ममागमनकाङ्क्षिणी ।
नियता नियताहारा
भर्तृशुश्रूषणे रता ॥
Disciplined in time and food, and taking care of the husband, may you thus occupy yourself, while waiting for my return!
2.24.30
प्राप्स्यसे परमं कामम्
मयि प्रत्यागते सति ।
यदि धर्मभृतां श्रेष्ठो
धारयिष्यति जीवितम् ॥
You will have the best of your dreams realized upon my return, hoping that the best among the patrons of Dharma (Daṡaratha), would be able to hold on to his life till then.
2.24.31
एवमुक्ता तु रामेण
बाष्पपर्याकुलेक्षणा ।
कौसल्या पुत्रशोकार्ता
रामं वचनमब्रवीत् ॥
Thus told by Rāma, Kousalyā, with her eyes clouded with tears, her heart wrung by a mother's grief, said to him:
2.24.32
गमने सुकृतां बुद्धिम्
न ते शक्नोमि पुत्रक ।
विनिवर्तयितुं वीर
नूनं कालो दुरत्ययः ॥
My son! O Veera! I do not seem to be able to divert you from your firm decision to leave, for no one can change what time has in store!
What else can a mother say to a son who is going to be away for several years, completely out of sight and out of reach, than that which is described in these Ṡlōkas?
Salutations to Vālmeeki, the first poet, for capturing a mother's mind so beautifully in these few Ṡlōkas!
2.24.33
गच्छ पुत्र त्वमेकाग्रो
भद्रं तेऽस्तु सदा विभो ।
पुनस्त्वयि निवृत्ते तु
भविष्यामि गतव्यथा ॥
My son, may you now leave single mindedly! May everything bode well for you always, O lord! May I be relieved of all my sufferings upon your return!
2.24.34
प्रत्यागते महाभागे
कृतार्थे चरितव्रते ।
पितुरानृण्यतां प्राप्ते
त्वयि लप्स्ये परं सुखम् ॥
O blessed one! I shall find the ultimate comfort in you, when you return with celebrated character, having accomplished your purpose, and having redeemed your debt to your father.
2.24.35
कृतान्तस्य गतिः पुत्र
दुर्विभाव्या सदा भुवि ।
यस्त्वां सञ्चोदयति मे
वच आच्छिद्य राघव ॥
The ways of destiny on this earth, my son, are beyond our comprehension. See how it urges you onward, O Rāghava, disregarding my entreaties!
2.24.36
गच्छेदानीं महाबाहो
क्षेमेण पुनरागतः ।
नन्दयिष्यसि मां पुत्र
साम्ना वाक्येन चारुणा ॥
O you of mighty arm! Please go now and come back safely my son, to delight me with your grace, charm and words!
2.24.37
अपीदानीं स कालस्स्यात्
वनात्प्रत्यागतं पुनः ।
यत्त्वां पुत्रक पश्येयम्
जटावल्कलधारिणम् ॥
I wish today was the day when I saw you returning, my son, with matted locks and clad in bark!
2.24.38
तथा हि रामं वनवासनिश्चितं
समीक्ष्य देवी परमेण चेतसा ।
उवाच रामं शुभलक्षणं वचो
बभूव च स्वस्त्ययनाभिकाङ्क्षिणी ॥
Thus the queen found him bent upon going to dwell in the forest. She spoke to Rāma, of auspicious appearance, out of a heart full of love, eager to invoke all the blessings upon him:
The blessings follow in the next Sarga.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे चतुर्विंशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the twenty fourth Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
