Aranya Kaanda - Sarga 19
In this Sarga, Khara gets enraged at the fate of his sister. He roars saying that someone has committed a serious blunder by poking him. He asks his sister who it is. Ṡūrpaṇakhā tells him about Rāma, Lakshmaṇa and Seetā. She tells him that she wants to see them dead in the battle, and that she would like to drink their blood. Khara orders fourteen Rākshasas to go to the brothers and kill them, and also that woman so that his sister can have her wish fulfilled.
3.19.1
तां तथा पतितां दृष्ट्वा
विरूपां शोणितोक्षिताम् ।
भगिनीं क्रोधसन्तप्तः
खरः पप्रच्छ राक्षसः ॥
Seeing his sister fall thus, disfigured and drenched in blood, Khara, the Rākshasa, burning with anger, asked:
3.19.2
उत्तिष्ठ तावदाख्याहि
प्रमोहं जहि सम्भ्रमम् ।
व्यक्तमाख्याहि केन त्वम्
एवं रूपा विरूपिता ॥
Get up, shake off your daze and bewilderment, tell me clearly who has disfigured you like this!
3.19.3
कः कृष्णसर्पमासीनं
आशीविषमनागसम् ।
तुदत्यभिसमापन्नम्
अङ्गुल्यग्रेण लीलया ॥
Who is it that tries to poke a black cobra with venom in its fangs that is (dangerously) close, lying motionless and doing no harm, with his finger?
3.19.4
कालपाशं समासज्य
कण्ठे मोहान्न बुध्यते ।
यस्त्वामद्य समासाद्य
पीतवान्विषमुत्तमम् ॥
Someone has put the noose of death around his neck foolishly and does not even know about it. He has drunk the most potent of poisons, by touching you!
3.19.5
बलविक्रमसम्पन्ना
कामगा कामरूपिणी ।
इमामवस्थां नीता त्वं
केनान्तकसमागता ॥
You are brave and full of strength. You are capable of taking any form and go anywhere as you please. You are equal to the God of Death himself. Who could bring you to this pass?
3.19.6
देवगन्धर्वभूतानाम्
ऋषीणां च महात्मनाम् ।
कोऽयमेवं विरूपां त्वां
महावीर्यश्चकार ह ॥
Was he of formidable strength? Was he a Dēva or a Gandharva or a Ṛshi, who has disfigured you like this?
3.19.7
न हि पश्याम्यहं लोके
यः कुर्यान्मम विप्रियम् ।
अमरेषु सहस्राक्षं
महेन्द्रं पाकशासनम् ॥
I cannot think of anyone in this world that would cause displeasure to me, even among the immortals, including the Mahēndra of thousand eyes, who could hold Pāka.
Pāka is a mighty Rākshasa, brother of Vṛtra. Indra subdued him in a great battle.
3.19.8
अद्याहं मार्गणैः प्राणान्
आदास्ये जीवितान्तकैः ।
सलिले क्षीरमासक्तं
निष्पिबन्निव सारसः ॥
I shall take his life away right today with arrows that can put an end to any life, just as the Sārasa bird drinks up the milk that is mixed with water.
We see an interesting comparison here, which is reflective of Indian, especially Vēdāntic thought. In this Ṡlōka, life spread in a body is compared to milk spread in water.
Is there a way to separate milk from water, once mixed? Yes, the Sārasa birds have the ability to drink the milk and leave the water alone. Similarly, an arrow has the ability to take out the life and leave the body alone.
3.19.9
निहतस्य मया सङ्ख्ये
शरसंकृत्तमर्मणः ।
सफेनं रुधिरं रक्तं
मेदिनी कस्य पास्यति ॥
Who is it whose vitals I am going to hit in a battle with my arrows and whose red blood the earth wants to drink along with the froth?
3.19.10
कस्य पत्ररथाः कायान्
मांसमुत्कृत्य सङ्गताः ।
प्रहृष्टा भक्षयिष्यन्ति
निहतस्य मया रणे ॥
Who is it who will be slain by me in fight and whose flesh will be extricated from his body and eaten by happy swarms of birds?
3.19.11
तं न देवा न गन्धर्वा
न पिशाचा न राक्षसाः ।
मयाऽपकृष्टं कृपणं
शक्तास्त्रातुमिहाऽहवे ॥
Neither the Dēvas nor the Gandharvas, neither the ghouls nor the Rākshasas can save the miserable wretch whom I shall drag (on the ground) in the battlefield.
3.19.12
उपलभ्य शनैस्संज्ञां
तं मे शंसितुमर्हसि ।
येन त्वं दुर्विनीतेन
वने विक्रम्य निर्जिता ॥
Come to your senses slowly and tell me who the rogue was, who had the grit to overpower you in the Vana!
3.19.13
इति भ्रातुर्वचश्श्रुत्वा
क्रुद्धस्य च विशेषतः ।
ततश्शूर्पणखा वाक्यं
सबाष्पमिदमब्रवीत् ॥
On hearing those words of her brother who was in a towering rage, Ṡūrpaṇakhā tearfully said these words:
3.19.14
तरुणौ रूपसम्पन्नौ
सुकुमारौ महाबलौ ।
पुण्डरीकविशालाक्षौ
चीरकृष्णाजिनाम्बरौ ।
फलमूलाशनौ दान्तौ
तापसौ धर्मचारिणौ ।
पुत्रौ दशरथस्यास्तां
भ्रातरौ रामलक्ष्मणौ ॥
They are the brothers Rāma and Lakshmaṇa, sons of Daṡaratha, adherents of Dharma. They are youthful, handsome, delicately formed and yet of immense strength, with eyes wide like lotus petals. They are dressed in bast wear and the skin of the black antelope. The are very disciplined, living like Tapasvis with their food limited to fruits and roots.
3.19.16
गन्धर्वराजप्रतिमौ
पार्थिवव्यञ्जनान्वितौ ।
देवौ वा मानुषौ वा तौ
न तर्कयितुमुत्सहे ॥
They looked like the King of Gandharvas and bore all the marks of royalty. I cannot even guess whether they are Dēvas or humans.
3.19.17
तरुणी रूपसम्पन्ना
सर्वाभरणभूषिता ।
दृष्टा तत्र मया नारी
तयोर्मध्ये सुमध्यमा ॥
I have seen, with them, a youthful and beautiful woman of slender waist, wearing every kind of ornament.
3.19.18
ताभ्यामुभाभ्यां सम्भूय
प्रमदामधिकृत्य ताम् ।
इमामवस्थां नीताऽहं
यथाऽनाथाऽसती तथा ॥
Both of them together, making that woman as the pretext, brought me to this pass, as if I had no protector and recourse.
3.19.19
तस्याश्चानृजुवृत्तायाः
तयोश्च हतयोरहम् ।
सफेनं पातुमिच्छामि
रुधिरं रणमूर्धनि ॥
I want to drink, in the frontlines of battle, the frothy blood of that crafty woman and of those slaughtered men.
3.19.20
एष मे प्रथमः कामः
कृतस्तात त्वया भवेत् ।
तस्यास्तयोश्च रुधिरं
पिबेयमहमाहवे ॥
My dear! May you fulfill this first and foremost of my dearest wishes! May I drink their blood and hers on the battlefield!
3.19.21
इति तस्यां ब्रुवाणायां
चतुर्दश महाबलान् ।
व्यादिदेश खरः क्रुद्धो
राक्षसानन्तकोपमान् ॥
When she said this, the angry Khara ordered fourteen Rākshasas of formidable strength, who were as dreadful as death:
3.19.22
मानुषौ शस्त्रसम्पन्नौ
चीरकृष्णाजिनाम्बरौ ।
प्रविष्टौ दण्डकारण्यं
घोरं प्रमदया सह ॥
Two men armed with weapons and clad in bast and the skin of the black antelope have come into the horrid Daṇḍaka Araṇya with a woman.
3.19.23
तौ हत्वा तां च दुर्वृत्ताम्
अपावर्तितुमर्हथ ।
इयं च रुधिरं तेषां
भगिनी मम पास्यति ॥
Kill those two and that wretched woman, and return. This sister of mine shall drink their blood.
3.19.24
मनोरथोऽयमिष्टोऽस्या
भगिन्या मम राक्षसाः ।
शीघ्रं सम्पाद्यतां तौ च
प्रमथ्य स्वेन तेजसा ॥
O Rākshasas! Smash them with all your power and let my sister have her wish fulfilled.
3.19.25
इति प्रतिसमादिष्टा
राक्षसास्ते चतुर्दश ।
तत्र जग्मुस्तया सार्धं
घना वातेरिता यथा ॥
Thus ordered, those fourteen Rākshasas went there along with her, (instantly disappearing) like clouds driven by the wind.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अरण्यकाण्डे एकोनविंशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the nineteenth Sarga in Araṇya Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
