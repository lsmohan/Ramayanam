Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 72
In this Sarga, Bharata, not finding his father in his palace, goes to his mother's palace where he could usually be found. Kaikēyee is extremely happy to see him and receives him with all affection and inquires about his wellbeing, and the wellbeing of his grandfather and uncle.
After relating that all was well with them and about his seven day long journey, he asks his mother why he could not see his father on the couch as usual.
Kaikēyee, the conceited fool that she is, tells everything as it happened, that she obtained the kingdom for him and that she demanded Rāma should be banished to Daṇḍaka forest. She also tells that Seetā and Lakshmaṇa had gone along with Rāma and that the king died crying for his beloved son.
2.72.1
अपश्यंस्तु ततस्तत्र
पितरं पितुरालये ।
जगाम भरतो द्रष्टुम्
मातरं मातुरालये ॥
Then, not finding his father at his residence, Bharata went to his mother's house to see her.
2.72.2
अनुप्राप्तं तु तं दृष्ट्वा
कैकेयी प्रोषितं सुतम् ।
उत्पपात तदा हृष्टा
त्यक्त्वा सौवर्णमासनम् ॥
On seeing the son who had long been away, the delighted Kaikēyee sprang up from her golden seat.
2.72.3
स प्रविश्यैव धर्मात्मा
स्वगृहं श्रीविवर्जितम् ।
भरतः प्रतिजग्राह
जनन्याश्चरणौ शुभौ ॥
As soon as he entered his house that no more held its former charm, Bharata touched his mother's auspicious feet.
2.72.4
सा तं मूर्धन्युपाघ्राय
परिष्वज्य यशस्विनम् ।
अङ्के भरतमारोप्य
प्रष्टुं समुपचक्रमे ॥
And she, after smelling his head and embracing him, took that well regarded man on her lap, and asked him:
2.72.5
अद्य ते कतिचिद्रात्र्यः
च्युतस्यार्यकवेश्मनः ।
अपि नाध्वश्रमश्शीघ्रम्
रथेनापततस्तव ॥
How many nights has it been since you left the house of your grandfather? I hope you are not fatigued by the journey, coming on the chariot as rapidly as you did!
2.72.6
आर्यकस्ते सुकुशली
युधाजिन्मातुलस्तव ।
प्रवासाच्च सुखं पुत्र
सर्वं मे वक्तुमर्हसि ॥
Is your revered grandfather well, and your uncle Yudhājit? Hope you were doing well while you were away there. Tell me all about it, my child!
2.72.7
एवं पृष्टस्तु कैकेय्या
प्रियं पार्थिवनन्दनः ।
आचष्ट भरत स्सर्वम्
मात्रे राजीवलोचनः ॥
Thus asked by Kaikēyee with all affection, Bharata, the King's son, of lotus eyes, narrated everything to her:
2.72.8
अद्य मे सप्तमी रात्रिः
च्युतस्याऽर्यकवेश्मनः ।
अम्बायाः कुशली तात
युधाजिन्मातुलश्च मे ॥
Tonight will be the seventh night after I started from the house of grandfather. Your father as well as my uncle Yudhājit are doing well.
2.72.9
यन्मे धनं च रत्नं च
ददौ राजा परन्तपः ।
परिश्रान्तं पथ्यभवत्
ततोऽहं पूर्वमागतः ॥
The carnival of monies and the jewels that the King, harrier of the foe, gave me are still on their way, fatigued. I came ahead of them.
2.72.10
राजवाक्यहरैर्दूतैः
त्वर्यमाणोऽहमागतः ।
यदहं प्रष्टुमिच्छामि
तदम्बा वक्तुमर्हति ॥
I came hastened by the messengers who brought me the message of the King, mother! You must tell me what I want to know.
2.72.11
शून्योऽयं शयनीयस्ते
पर्यङ्को हेमभूषितः ।
न चायमिक्ष्वाकुजनः
प्रहृष्टः प्रतिभाति मा ॥
This couch of yours, well made for taking rest, adorned with gold embellishments, is empty! I do not get the feeling that the people of Ikshwāku are happy!
2.72.12
राजा भवति भूयिष्ठम्
इहाम्बाया निवेशने ।
तमहं नाद्य पश्यामि
द्रष्टुमिच्छन्निहाऽगतः ॥
The King was to be found most often here in your apartments, mother. Today I do not see him whom I came expressly to see.
2.72.13
पितुर्ग्रहीष्ये चरणौ
तं ममाऽख्याहि पृच्छतः ।
आहोस्विदम्ब ज्येष्ठायाः
कौसल्याया निवेशने ॥
I shall clasp the feet of my father, tell me, O mother, where he is! Or, is he perchance in the house of the eldest one, Kousalyā?
2.72.14
तं प्रत्युवाच कैकेयी
प्रियवद्घोरमप्रियम् ।
अजानन्तं प्रजानन्ती
राज्यलोभेन मोहिता ॥
Kaikēyee, who knew everything, answered the one who knew nothing, with words that were terrible and detestable as if they were the most pleasurable, overtaken by her zeal for the kingdom.
2.72.15
या गतिस्सर्वभूतानाम्
तां गतिं ते पिता गतः ।
राजा महात्मा तेजस्वी
यायजूकस्सतां गतिः ॥
Your father, the Mahātma, the radiant King, a great performer of Yajñas, the resort of all good people, went where every being goes ultimately.
2.72.16
तच्छ्रुत्वा भरतो वाक्यम्
धर्माभिजनवाञ्चुचिः ।
पपात सहसा भूमौ
पितृशोकबलार्दितः ॥
On hearing those words, Bharata, of clean character, who was born into a lineage of Dhārmikas, fell down on the ground immediately, overtaken by grief for his father.
2.72.17
हा हतोऽस्मीति कृपणाम्
दीनां वाचमुदीरयन् ।
निपपात महाबाहुः
बाहू विक्षिप्य वीर्यवान् ॥
'Alas, I am dead!' cried the heroic one in a pitiable and miserable voice. He of might arm threw up his arms and fell down.
2.72.18
ततश्शोकेन संवीतः
पितुर्मरणदुःखितः ।
विललाप महातेजा
भ्रान्ताकुलितचेतनः ॥
Inundated by grief, saddened by his father's death, that man of great radiance cried with his mind completely disoriented.
2.72.19
एतत्सुरुचिरं भाति
पितुर्मे शयनं पुरा ।
शशिनेवामलं रात्रौ
गगनं तोयदात्यये ॥
This bed of my father used to glisten like the clear moonlit skies of post-monsoon season.
2.72.20
तदिदं न विभात्यद्य
विहीनं तेन धीमता ।
व्योमेव शशिना हीनम्
अप्च्छुष्क इव सागरः ॥
Today, bereft of the wise one, it is as lackluster as the moonless sky and is like an ocean whose waters have dried up.
2.72.21
बाष्पमुत्सृज्य कण्ठेन
स्वात्मना परिपीडितः ।
आच्छाद्य वदनं श्रीमत्
वस्त्रेण जयतां वरः ॥
And he, the foremost of victors, with his throat choked with tears, his being tormented absolutely, covered his gracious face with his garment!
2.72.22
तमार्तं देवसङ्काशम्
समीक्ष्य पतितं भुवि ।
निकृत्तमिव सालस्य
स्कन्धं परशुना वने ।
मत्तमातङ्गसङ्काशम्
चन्द्रार्कसदृशं भुवः ।
उत्थापयित्वा शोकार्तम्
वचनं चेदमब्रवीत् ॥
Seeing him, the one who was comparable to an elephant in rut and to the moon and the sun, and who was like an angel fallen on the ground and like the trunk of an axed Sāla tree fallen in a forest, (she) raised him, the one stricken by grief, from the ground and said these words:
2.72.24
उत्तिष्ठोत्तिष्ठ किं शेषे
राजपुत्र महायशः ।
त्वद्विधा नहि शोचन्ति
सन्तस्सदसि सम्मताः ॥
Get up! Get up, O prince of great renown! Why do you lie down like this? Noble men like you, who are well regarded in assemblies, must not cry like this!
2.72.25
दानयज्ञाधिकारा हि
शीलश्रुतिवचोऽनुगा ।
बुद्धिस्ते बुद्धिसम्पन्न
प्रभेवार्कस्य मन्दिरे ॥
O wise one! Your wisdom, which sides with charitable activities and Yajñas, with the pursuit of noble conduct and with the study of Vedas, has always been the sunshine of the house.
2.72.26
स रुदित्वा चिरं कालम्
भूमौ विपरिवृत्य च ।
जननीं प्रत्युवाचेदम्
शोकैर्बहुभिरावृतः ॥
He wept for a long time rolling on the ground. Hit by many bouts of grief, he said to his mother again:
2.72.27
अभिषेक्ष्यति रामं नु
राजा यज्ञं नु यक्ष्यते ।
इत्यहं कृतसङ्कल्पो
हृष्टो यात्रामयासिषम् ॥
I came here happily thinking that the King was going to perform either the consecration of Rāma or a Yajña.
2.72.28
तदिदं ह्यन्यथा भूतम्
व्यवदीर्णं मनो मम ।
पितरं यो न पश्यामि
नित्यं प्रियहिते रतम् ॥
Now, everything has gone topsy-turvy. My mind is shattered. I do not see my father, who was ever solicitous of my happiness and wellbeing!
2.72.29
अम्ब केनात्यगाद्राजा
व्याघिना मय्यनागते ।
धन्या रामादयस्सर्वे
यैः पिता संस्कृतस्स्वयम् ॥
Mother, to what ailment did the king succumb before I could reach? Fortunate indeed are Rāma and others who could, in person, perform the last rites for my father!
2.72.30
न नूनं मां महाराजः
प्राप्तं जानाति कीर्तिमान् ।
उपजिघ्रेद्धि मांमूर्ध्नि
तातस्सन्नम्य सत्वरम् ॥
Assuredly the great renowned King does not know that I have come; for father would have instantly drawn me towards him and smelled my head.
2.72.31
क्व स पाणिस्सुखस्पर्शः
तातस्याक्लिष्टकर्मणः ।
येन मां रजसा ध्वस्तम्
अभीक्ष्णं परिमार्जति ॥
Ah! Where is that soothing touch of the hand of my father, who makes even difficult things appear easy, that used to wipe away the dust on me always!
2.72.32
यो मे भ्राता पिता बन्धुः
यस्य दासोऽस्मि धीमतः ।
तस्य मां शीघ्रमाख्याहि
रामस्याक्लिष्टकर्मणः ॥
Let Rāma, the wise one, one who makes even difficult things appear easy, who is brother, father and kin to me whose servant I am, know at once that I am here.
2.72.33
पिता हि भवति ज्येष्ठो
धर्ममार्यस्य जानतः ।
तस्य पादौ गृहीष्यामि
स हीदानीं गतिर्मम ॥
A noble man who knows Dharma, surely regards an elder brother as the father. I must clasp his feet, he is my refuge now.
2.72.34
धर्मविद्धर्मनित्यश्च
सत्यसन्धो दृढव्रतः ।
आर्यः किमब्रवीद्राजा
पिता मे सत्यविक्रमः ॥
What did my noble father, who knows Dharma and constantly adheres to it, who was wedded to his word, who had a persistent resolve and whose prowess was truly count worthy, say ?
2.72.35
पश्चिमं साधु सन्देशम्
इच्छामि श्रोतुमात्मनः ।
इति पृष्टा यथातत्त्वम्
कैकेयी वाक्यमब्रवीत् ॥
I long to hear his last words of message and counsel to me! Thus asked, Kaikēyee related faithfully what had passed.
2.72.36
रामेति राजा विलपन्
हा सीते लक्ष्मणेति च ।
स महात्मा परं लोकम्
गतो गतिमतां वरः ॥
That Mahātma, who can be counted as the best among those who had passed, went to the highest of the worlds, lamenting: 'Alas, Rāma! Alas, Seetā and Lakshmaṇa!'
2.72.37
इमां तु पश्चिमां वाचम्
व्याजहार पिता तव ।
कालधर्मपरिक्षिप्तः
पाशैरिव महागजः ॥
Like a tusker bound by ropes, your father, bound by the laws of time, uttered these final words:
2.72.38
सिद्धार्थास्ते नरा रामम्
आगतं सह सीतया ।
लक्ष्मणं च महाबाहुम्
द्रक्ष्यन्ति पुनरागतम् ॥
Blessed are the men that shall see Rāma return along with Seetā and Lakshmaṇa of the mighty arm!
2.72.39
तच्छ्रुत्वा विषसादैव
द्वितीयाप्रियशंसनात् ।
विषण्णवदनो भूत्वा
भूयः पप्रच्छ मातरम् ॥
Hearing that second unpleasant piece of news made him extremely miserable. With his face downcast, he asked his mother again:
2.72.40
क्व चेदानीं स धर्मात्मा
कौसल्यानन्दवर्धनः ।
लक्ष्मणेन सह भ्रात्रा
सीतया च समं गतः ॥
Where is that Dharmātma, Kousalyā's perpetual joy, gone now along with Seetā and brother Lakshmaṇa ?
2.72.41
तथा पृष्टा यथातत्त्वम्
आख्यातुमुपचक्रमे ।
मातास्य सुमहऽद्वाक्यम्
विप्रियं प्रियशङ्कया ॥
Thus asked, his mother started relating everything as it happened, presuming that the extremely unpleasant news would be pleasing to him.
2.72.42
स हि राजसुतः पुत्र
चीरवासा महावनम् ।
दण्डकान्सह वैदेह्या
लक्ष्मणानुचरो गतः ॥
The prince, clad in bark, my son, has gone to the great Daṇḍaka forest, along with the princess of Vidēha, with Lakshmaṇa following him.
2.72.43
तच्छ्रुत्वा भरतस्त्रस्तो
भ्रातुश्चारित्रशङ्कया ।
स्वस्य वंशस्य महात्म्यात्
प्रष्टुं समुपचक्रमे ॥
On hearing that, Bharata shuddered at the thought of possible misconduct by Rāma. Knowing fully well the greatness of his lineage, he further inquired:
2.72.44
कच्चिन्न ब्राह्मणधनम्
हृतं रामेण कस्यचित् ।
कच्चिन्नाढ्यो दरिद्रो वा
तेनापापो विहिंसितः ॥
I hope the money of no Brāhmaṇa was snatched by Rāma! I hope no innocent person, rich or poor, was harmed by him!
An innocent person, whether poor or rich, should not be punished, even if he is the heir apparent. Period. Those were the standards to which India held itself up to, in the bygone eras.
After communism crept in, it became acceptable to harm a rich person for being rich. In the name of protecting oppressed sections, it became acceptable to throw an innocent person in jail, if the complaint was filed by someone belonging to a poorer section of society. In the name of protecting excesses of in-laws against a daughter-in-law, it became acceptable to put every named party from the in-law's side in jail, regardless of whether they were guilty or innocent.
Our rightful zeal to protect the weak and the disadvantaged, unfortunately, has caused us to make laws that are off-balance. The lives of many innocent people, for the fault of being born into a rich family, or into the husband's side of the family, are being ruined.
We are trying to compensate for our inability to enforce a balanced legislation, by making laws that are off-balance, and presumptive and prejudiced against one side.
Laws and their enforcement should be aimed at protecting the innocent and punishing the guilty. They should not be presumptive against rich or poor, wife or husband, or anyone else for that matter. Laws should be blind to the background of the person and cognitive only of the offense.
2.72.45
कच्चिन्न परदारान्वा
राजपुत्रोऽभिमन्यते ।
कस्मात्स दण्डकारण्ये
भ्रूणहेव विवासितः ॥
I hope the prince did not covet another man's wife! Why was he banished to the Daṇḍaka forest, like someone who killed a fetus!
2.72.46
अथास्य चपला माता
तत्स्वकर्म यथातथम् ।
तेनैव स्त्रीस्वभावेन
व्याहर्तुमुपचक्रमे ॥
Then his fickle minded mother started recounting what she did as it happened, consumed by her feminine impulses.
2.72.47
एवमुक्ता तु कैकेयी
भरतेन महात्मना ।
उवाच वचनं हृष्टा
मूढा पण्डितमानिनी ॥
Conceited fool that she was, Kaikēyee answered Mahātma Bharata with great complacence:
2.72.48
न ब्राह्मणधनं किञ्चित्
धृतं रामेण कस्यचित् ।
कश्चिन्नाढ्यो दरिद्रो वा
तेनापापो विहिंसितः ।
न रामः परदारांश्च
चक्षुर्भ्यामपि पश्यति ॥
No, no Brāhmaṇa's money was taken by Rāma. No, no innocent person, rich or poor, was abused by him. No, Rāma never cast his eyes on another man's wife.
2.72.49
मया तु पुत्र श्रुत्वैव
रामस्यैवाभिषेचनम् ।
याचितस्ते पिता राज्यम्
रामस्य च विवासनम् ॥
It was I, my son, after hearing about Rāma's consecration, who asked your father for the kingdom for you and for the banishment of Rāma.
2.72.50
स स्ववृत्तिं समास्थाय
पिता ते तत्तऽथाकरोत् ।
रामश्च सह सौमित्रिः
प्रेषितस्सह सीतया ॥
And your father, who was true to his principles, obliged. Rāma was sent away along with the son of Sumitrā and Seetā.
2.72.51
तमपश्यन्प्रियंपुत्रम्
महीपालो महायशाः ।
पुत्रशोकपरिद्यूनः
पञ्चत्वमुपपेदिवान् ॥
When he, the ruler of the earth, of great renown, could no longer see his beloved son, giving in to grief for his son, dissipated into the five elements.
2.72.52
त्वयात्विदानीं धर्मज्ञ
राजत्वमवलम्ब्यताम् ।
त्वत्कृते हि मया सर्वम्
इदमेवं विधं कृतम् ॥
You are well versed in Dharma! You shall right away take over the reins of the kingdom! It was for your sake that I did all this in this manner.
2.72.53
मा शोकं मा च सन्तापम्
धैर्यमाश्रय पुत्रक ।
त्वदधीना हि नगरी
राज्यं चैतदनामयम् ॥
Do not give way to grief or regrets. Be brave, my son! This city is yours now. This kingdom, free from all claims and encumbrances, is also yours.
2.72.54
तत्पुत्र शीघ्रं विधिना विधिज्ञै-
र्वसिष्ठमुख्यैस्सहितो द्विजेन्द्रैः ।
सङ्काल्य राजानमदीनसत्त्व
मात्मानमुर्व्यामभिषेचयस्व ॥
Therefore, son, quickly perform the final rites to the king in the customary manner assisted by Vasishṭha and other prominent Brāhmaṇas who are well versed in the custom. Free yourself from any dejection and consecrate yourself as the ruler of the earth.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे द्विसप्ततितमस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the seventy second Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
