Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 53
In this Sarga, being all by himself in a desolate Vana, with no one other than Seetā and Lakshmaṇa with him, Rāma reflects and broods over all that had happened.
The sadness of leaving her mother alone, with no one to fend for her, overtakes him. He even requests Lakshmaṇa to go back to Ayōdhyā to take care of Sumitrā and Kousalyā.
Lakshmaṇa consoles him and reiterates that neither he, nor Seetā can live without him.
2.53.1
स तं वृक्षं समासाद्य
सन्ध्यामन्वास्यपश्चिमाम् ।
रामो रमयतां श्रेष्ठ
इति होवाच लक्ष्मणम् ॥
Reaching that tree, Rāma, the foremost of pleasing personalities, paid salutations to sun setting in the west, and said these words to Lakshmaṇa:
2.53.2
अद्येयं प्रथमा रात्रिः
याता जनपदाद्बहिः ।
या सुमन्त्रेण रहिता
तां नोत्कण्ठितुमर्हसि ॥
This would be the first of the nights that we would spend outside of all human habitation and even without the company of Sumantra. But there shall be nothing for you to feel tensed about it.
2.53.3
जागर्तव्यमतन्द्रिभ्याम्
अद्यप्रभृति रात्रिषु ।
योगक्षेमौ हि सीताया
वर्तेते लक्ष्मणावयोः ॥
From today onwards, we shall be on full vigil at night. The safety and wellbeing of Seetā depends on both of us alone.
2.53.4
रात्रिं कथञ्चिदेवेमाम्
सौमित्रे वर्तयामहे ।
अपावर्तामहे भूमौ
आस्तीर्य स्वयमार्जितैः ॥
O son of Sumitrā! We shall pass this night somehow! We shall cover the ground (with grass) by ourselves to lie down.
2.53.5
स तु संविश्य मेदिन्याम्
महार्हशयनोचितः ।
इमाः सौमित्रये रामो
व्याजहार कथाः शुभाः ॥
Then he, who was used to the most luxurious bedding, sat down directly on the ground. Rāma then said these well meaning words to the son of Sumitrā:
2.53.6
धृवमद्य महाराजो
दुःखं स्वपिति लक्ष्मण ।
कृतकामा तु कैकेयी
तुष्टा भवितुमर्हति ॥
Tonight, O Lakshmaṇa, for sure, the great king will sleep with grief and Kaikēyee will be happy with her wish fulfilled.
2.53.7
सा हि देवी महाराजम्
कैकेयी राज्यकारणात् ।
अपि न च्यावयेत्प्राणान्
दृष्ट्वा भरतमागतम् ॥
Once Bharata arrives, I hope the queen Kaikēyee will not tear the life out of the great king for the sake of the kingdom!
2.53.8
अनाथश्च हि वृद्धश्च
मया चैव विनाकृतः ।
किं करिष्यति कामात्मा
कैकेयी वशमागतः ॥
He is aged and has no one to turn to, and even I am taken out of his life. What can he, who fell under the sway of Kaikēyee out of lust, do?
Daṡaratha's infatuation with Kaikēyee must have been the talk of the town for some years and there must have been some prevailing resentment about it.
That can be inferred from the many references made to it not only by Rāma, but also by Lakshmaṇa and all the people of Ayōdhyā.
Lakshmaṇa even goes so far as to suggest that this must be a well meditated plan and did not come up all of a sudden (2.23.9).
As if to give credibility to this perception, Daṡaratha goes to Kaikēyee's palace, instead of to Kousalyā's palace, even after announcing the installation of Rāma as the crown prince. It is only after the departure of Rāma to the Vana, that he goes to Kousalyā's palace.
In these Ṡlōkas also, Rāma blames the turn of events to be the making of Kaikēyee and Daṡaratha's infatuation with her, even though, in the big scheme of things, he acknowledges that it is the will of Providence (2.22.15).
2.53.9
इदं व्यसनमालोक्य
राज्ञश्च मतिविभ्रमम् ।
काम एवार्थ धर्माभ्याम्
गरीयानिति मे मतिः ॥
Seeing this calamity and the aberration in the mind of the king, I cannot help but think that Kāma is more powerful than Artha and Dharma.
2.53.10
को ह्यविद्वानपि पुमान्
प्रमदायाः कृते त्यजेत् ।
छन्दानुवर्तिनं पुत्रम्
तातो मामिव लक्ष्मण ॥
My dear Lakshmaṇa! How stupid a man should be, to abandon an obedient son like me for the sake of a woman!
2.53.11
सुखी बत सभार्यश्च
भरतः केकयीसुतः ।
मुदितान् कोसलानेको
यो भोक्ष्यत्यधिराजवत् ॥
Alas! Bharata, the son of Kaikēyee, would be the only one, along with his wife, that enjoys the jolly kingdom of Kōsala, like a sovereign!
2.53.12
स हि सर्वस्य राज्यस्य
मुखमेकं भविष्यति ।
ताते च वयसाऽतीते
मयि चारण्यमास्थिते ॥
With father being too old and me being away in the forest, he will become the sole face of the kingdom.
2.53.13
अर्थधर्मौ परित्यज्य
य काममनुवर्तते ।
एवमापद्यते क्षिप्रम्
राजा दशरथो यथा ॥
Whoever follows Kāma, keeping aside Artha and Dharma, will, in no time, perish like king Daṡaratha.
2.53.14
मन्ये दशरथान्ताय
मम प्रव्राजनाय च ।
कैकेयी सौम्य सम्प्राप्ता
राज्याय भरतस्य च ॥
O gentle one! I have to think that Kaikēyee came along for nothing other than the destruction of Daṡaratha, for my exile and for bringing the rule of Bharata!
2.53.15
अपीदानीं तु कैकेयी
सौभाग्यमदमोहिता ।
कौसल्यां च सुमित्रां च
सम्प्रबाधेत मत्कृते ॥
How can one say Kaikēyee, intoxicated by her good fortune, will now not make life miserable for Kousalyā and Sumitrā, because of me?
2.53.16
मा स्म मत्कारणाद्देवी
सुमित्रा दुःखमावसेत् ।
अयोध्यामित एव त्वम्
काल्ये प्रविश लक्ष्मण ॥
Let the lady Sumitrā not be put to suffering because of me. Go to Ayōdhyā in the morning straight from here, O Lakshmaṇa!
2.53.17
अहमेको गमिष्यामि
सीतया सह दण्डकान् ।
अनाथाया हि नाथस्त्वम्
कौसल्याया भविष्यसि ॥
I will go alone to Daṇḍaka, along with Seetā. May you be the guardian for Kousalyā, who has no one left to care for her.
2.53.18
क्षुद्रकर्मा हि कैकेयी
द्वेष्यमन्याय्यमाचरेत् ।
परिदद्याहि धर्मज्ञे
भरते मम मातरम् ॥
Kaikēyee, being the vile one she is, can be expected to be detestably unfair. You must put my mother under the direct protection of Bharata who knows Dharma.
Even at a moment of dejection like this, Rāma does not fail to show confidence in Bharata.
2.53.19
नूनं जात्यन्तरे कस्मिन्
स्त्रियः पुत्रैर्वियोजिताः ।
जनन्या मम सौमित्रे
तस्मादेतदुपस्थितम् ॥
My mother, in some other former life, must have separated women from their sons, and hence this must be the consequence, O son of Sumitrā!
2.53.20
मया हि चिरपुष्टेन
दुखसंवर्धितेन च ।
विप्रयुज्यत कौसल्या
फलकाले धिगस्तु माम् ॥
Kousalyā, having nourished me so long and taken so much pains to bring me up, has had me taken away from her, just when she might have hoped to reap the fruit. Shame on me!
2.53.21
मा स्म सीमन्तिनी काचित्
जनयेत्पुत्रमीदृशम् ।
सौमित्रे योऽहमम्बाया
दद्मि शोकमनन्तकम् ॥
May no fertile woman give birth to such a son as me, who has brought such an endless grief upon his mother, O son of Sumitrā!
2.53.22
मन्ये प्रीतिविशिष्टा सा
मत्तो लक्ष्मण शारिका ।
यस्यास्तच्छ्रूयते वाक्यम्
शुक पादमरेर्दश ॥
O Lakshmaṇa! I consider that the Ṡarika bird that shouted, 'O parrot, bite the foot of the enemy!' showed more love than I did.
2.53.23
शोचन्त्या अल्पभाग्याया
न किञ्चिदुपकुर्वता ।
पुत्रेण किमपुत्राया
मया कार्यमरिन्दम ॥
Of what earthly use am I, a son, who has done so little, to that grieving woman of little fortune, who has no sons now, O harrier of the foe?
2.53.24
अल्पभाग्या हि मे माता
कौसल्या रहिता मया ।
शेते परमदुःखार्ता
पतिता शोकसागरे ॥
Scant, indeed, are the fortunes of my mother Kousalyā, without me! With measureless grief, she must be lying, plunged in an ocean of misery.
2.53.25
एको ह्यहमयोध्यां च
पृथिवीं चापि लक्ष्मण ।
तरेयमिषुभिः क्रुद्धो
ननु वीर्यमकारणम् ॥
Single-handedly, with my arrows, O Lakshmaṇa, I can take over Ayōdhyā, nay, the entire earth, if I were to become angry enough. But there must be a reason for a show of strength.
2.53.26
अधर्मभयभीतश्च
परलोकस्य चानघ ।
तेन लक्ष्मण नाद्याह
मात्मानमभिषेचये ॥
O sinless Lakshmaṇa! For fear of not following Dharma and for fear of the world beyond, I am not consecrating myself (as the king)!
2.53.27
एतदन्यश्च करुणम्
विलप्य विजने वने ।
अश्रुपूर्णमुखो रामो
निशि तूष्णीमुपाविशत् ॥
Having spoken of these and many other things in words of piteous lamentation, Rāma, his face covered with tears, sat silently in that desolate Vana that night.
2.53.28
विलप्योपरतं रामम्
गतार्चिषमिवानलम् ।
समुद्रमिव निर्वेगम्
आश्वासयत लक्ष्मणः ॥
When Rāma had ceased lamenting, like a fire that had died down and a sea that had quieted down, Lakshmaṇa consoled him (saying):
2.53.29
ध्रुवमद्य पुरी राजन्
अयोध्यायुधिनां वर ।
निष्प्रभा त्वयि निष्क्रान्ते
गतचन्द्रेव शर्वरी ॥
O King, O foremost of warriors! The city of Ayōdhyā, with you gone away, will, without a doubt, look shorn of her splendor today, like a night without the moon.
2.53.30
नैतदौपयिकं राम
यदिदं परितप्यते ।
विषादयसि सीतां च
मां चैव पुरुषर्षभ ॥
This kind of distress, O Rāma, does not befit you. It only makes, O tiger among men, Seetā and me sad.
2.53.31
न च सीता त्वया हीना
न चाहमपि राघव ।
मुहूर्तमपि जीवावो
जलान्मत्स्याविनोद्धृतौ ॥
Neither Seetā nor I, O Rāghava, can live without you, even for a moment, any more than a fish taken out of the water.
2.53.32
नहि तातं न शत्रुघ्नम्
न सुमित्रां परन्तप ।
द्रष्टुमिच्छेयमद्याहम्
स्वर्गं चापि त्वया विना ॥
Neither father nor Ṡatrughna, nor Sumitrā, nor Heaven itself O harrier of the foe, do I wish to see, this day, without you.
2.53.33
ततस्तत्र सुखासीनौ
नातिदूरे निरीक्ष्य ताम् ।
न्यग्रोधे सुकृतां शय्याम्
भेजाते धर्मवत्सलौ ॥
Having sat there for some time quietly, the two, who loved Dharma dearly, saw the well-made bed under the banyan tree nearby and went and lay down on it.
2.53.34
स लक्ष्मणस्योत्तमपुष्कलं वचो
निशम्य चैवं वनवासमादरात् ।
समाः समस्ता विदधे परन्तपः
प्रपद्य धर्मं सुचिराय राघवः ॥
Appreciating those well meaning words of Lakshmaṇa, Rāghava, harrier of the foe, focused his mind on completing the many years of life in Vana ahead in accordance with Dharma.
2.53.35
ततस्तु तस्मिन् विजने वने तदा
महाबलौ राघववंशवर्धनौ ।
न तौ भयं सम्भ्रममभ्युपेयतु
र्यथैव सिंहौ गिरिसानुगोचरौ ॥
That Vana, as desolate as it was, was no source of fear or apprehension for those mighty strong illustrious scions of the race of Raghu just as a mountain lair would not be for lions.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे त्रिपञ्चाशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the fifty third Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
