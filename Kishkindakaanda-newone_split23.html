Kishkindha Kaanda - Sarga 23
In this Sarga, Tārā laments over the dead body of her husband. She resents that he lost his life, that her life, along with that of Aṅgada, is left in tatters, by his not heeding her advice. She resents that Vāli, at the end, had to yield his supremacy to Sugreeva. She asks Aṅgada to prostrate to his father.
She laments that she could not hug his body, prevented by the arrow. Neela then removes the arrow. Tārā wipes the dust of the battle off Vali’s body and washes it off with her tears.
4.23.1
ततस्समुपजिघ्रन्ती
कपिराजस्य तन्मुखम् ।
पतिं लोकश्रुतां तारा
मृतं वचनमब्रवीत् ॥
Smelling the face of her dead husband, the Lord of Vānaras,, Tārā, of great renown in the world, said these words:
4.23.2
शेषे त्वं विषमे दुःखम्
अकृत्वा वचनं मम ।
उपलोपचिते वीर
सुदुःखे वसुधातले ॥
O Veera! Not following my advice, you lie down so pitiably on this bare hard ground littered with stones.
4.23.3
मत्तः प्रियतरा नूनम्
वानरेन्द्र मही तव ।
शेषे हि तां परिष्वज्य
मां च न प्रतिभाषसे ॥
O Lord of Vānaras! Indeed, you love this earth more than you do me; for you lie down embracing her not saying even a word to me!
4.23.4
सुग्रीवस्य वशं प्राप्तो
विधिरेषभवत्यहो ।
सुग्रीव एव विक्रान्तो
वीर साहसिकप्रिय ॥
Alas! Your fate has brought you to this, to be lorded over by Sugreeva! O Veera, O lover of adventure! Sugreeva has proved to be the supreme.
4.23.5
ऋक्षवानरमुख्यास्त्वाम्
बलिनं पर्युपासते ।
तेषां विलपितं कृच्छ्रम्
अङ्गदस्य च शोचतः ।
मम चेमा गिरः श्रुत्वा
किं त्वं न प्रतिबुध्यसे ॥
The chiefs of Vānaras and bears wait upon you, the mighty strong one! Why are you not responding either to their horrible cries or to the wailing of Aṅgada or to my laments!
4.23.6
इदं तवीरशयनम्
तत्र शेषे हतो युधि ।
शायिता निहता यत्र
त्वयैव रिपवः पुरा ॥
You are now lying on the hero’s bed on which you made your enemies lie dead, killed by you, earlier.
4.23.7
विशुद्धसत्त्वाभिजन
प्रिययुद्ध मम प्रिय ।
मामनाथां विहायैकाम्
गतस्त्वमसि मानद ॥
O Mānada! O my beloved! O lover of battles! O you of pure and powerful lineage! You are gone, leaving me alone like a forlorn!
4.23.8
शूराय न प्रदातव्या
कन्या खलु विपश्चिता ।
शूरभार्यां हतां पश्य
सद्यो मां विधवां कृताम् ॥
No man of sense should wed his daughter to a valiant fighter; look at me, the wife of a hero, widowed and undone instantly!
4.23.9
अवभग्नश्च मे मानो
भग्ना मे शाश्वती गतिः ।
अगाधे च निमग्नाऽस्मि
विपुले शोकसागरे ॥
My stature and the course of my life is shattered, once and for all. I am sunk in a deep and endless ocean of sorrow.
4.23.10
अश्मसारमयं नूनम्
इदं मे हृदयं दृढम् ।
भर्तारं निहतं दृष्ट्वा
यन्नाद्य शतधा कृतम् ॥
This heart of mine, for sure, is made of hard metal; for it is not broken into a hundred pieces now, even after seeing the husband fallen, killed.
4.23.11
सुहृच्चैव हि भर्ता च
प्रकृत्या मम च प्रियः ।
आहवे च पराक्रान्तः
शूरः पञ्चत्वमागतः ॥
You, my husband, a man of good heart who won me over with your character, a domineering fighter in battles, are dead.
4.23.12
पतिहीना तु या नारी
कामं भवतु पुत्रिणी ।
धनधान्यैस्सुपूर्णाऽपि
विधवेत्युच्यते बुधैः ॥
A woman who has lost her husband is called a widow by the revered, regardless of whether she has children or has an abundance of wealth and grain.
4.23.13
स्वगात्रप्रभवे वीर
शेषे रुधिरमण्डले ।
क्रिमिरागपरिस्तोमे
स्वकीये शयने यथा ॥
O Veera! You lie in a pool of blood from your own body as if it is your own bed with a crimson spread.
4.23.14
रेणुशोणितसंवीतम्
गात्रं तव समन्ततः ।
परिरब्धुं न शक्नोमि
भुजाभ्यां प्लवगर्षभ ॥
Your body is covered all over with blood and dust. I am not able to embrace your body (fully) with my arms, O bull among Vānaras!
4.23.15
कृतकृत्योऽद्य सुग्रीवो
वैरेऽस्मिन्नतिदारुणे ।
यस्य रामविमुक्तेन
हृतमेकेषुणा भयम् ॥
Sugreeva has emerged as the winner in this terrible feud. With the single arrow shot by Rāma, he is freed from fear.
4.23.16
शरेण हृदि लग्नेन
गात्रसंस्पर्शने तव ।
वार्यामि त्वां निरीक्षन्ती
त्वयि पञ्चत्वमागते ॥
You are dead; here I just look at you, unable to embrace you, prevented by the arrow buried in your heart.
4.23.17
उद्बबर्ह शरं नीलः
तस्य गात्रगतं तदा ।
गिरिगह्वरसंलीनम्
दीप्तमाशीविषं यथा ॥
Thereupon Neela pulled out the arrow that was stuck in his body like a dazzling venomous serpent in the crevice of a mountain.
4.23.18
तस्य निष्कृष्यमाणस्य
बाणस्य च बभौ द्युतिः ।
अस्तमस्तकसंरुद्धो
रश्मिर्दिनकरादिव ॥
As the arrow was being pulled out, its glow shined like the rays of the setting sun emerging from behind the crest of a mountain.
4.23.19
पेतुः क्षतजधारास्तु
व्रणेभ्यस्तस्य सर्वशः ।
ताम्रगैरिकसम्पृक्ता
धारा इव धराधरात् ॥
Streams of blood from the wounds on his body gushed out in every direction like torrents tinted with the red-hued minerals on a mountain-side.
4.23.20
अवकीर्णं विमार्जन्ती
भर्तारं रणरेणुना ।
अस्रैर्नयनजैश्शूरम्
सिषेचास्त्रसमाहतम् ॥
Wiping the dust of the battle off her husband, she washed that Ṡūra, who was slain by the arrow, with her tears.
4.23.21
रुधिरोक्षितसर्वाङ्गम्
दृष्ट्वा विनिहतं पतिम् ।
उवाच तारा पिङ्गाक्षम्
पुत्रमङ्गदमङ्गना ॥
Continuing to look at her slain husband whose body was covered with blood, that woman, Tārā, told her son Aṅgada, of yellow-tinted eyes:
4.23.22
अवस्थां पश्चिमां पश्य
पितुः पुत्र सुदारुणाम् ।
सम्प्रसक्तस्य वैरस्य
गतोऽन्तः पापकर्मणा ॥
My son! Look at the terrible fate of your father at the end, as the feud born out of a sinful deed came to a close.
4.23.23
बाल सूर्योज्ज्वल तनुम्
प्रयान्तं यमसादनम् ।
अभिवादय राजानम्
पितरं पुत्र मानदम् ॥
Make your prostrations, my son, to the King, your father, who upholds everyone’s dignity, and whose body shines like the morning sun, as he goes to the abode of Yama!
4.23.24
एवमुक्तस्समुत्थाय
जग्राह चरणौ पितुः ।
भुजाभ्यां पीनवृत्ताभ्याम्
अङ्गदोऽहमिति ब्रुवन् ॥
Thus told, Aṅgada got up and clasped the feet of his father with his stout and rounded arms, saying, ‘This is me, Aṅgada!’
4.23.25
अभिवादयमानं त्वाम्
अङ्गदं त्वं यथा पुरा ।
दीर्घायुर्भव पुत्रेति
किमर्थं नाभिभाषसे ॥
(And then Tārā said:) Why do you not bless Aṅgada, who prostrates to you, as you used to do, saying “May you live long, child!”
4.23.26
अहं पुत्रसहाया त्वाम्
उपासे गतचेतसम् ।
सिंहेन निहतं सद्यो
गौस्सवत्सेव गोवृषम् ॥
I, with my son linger by the side of your lifeless body like a cow that stands with her calf near the bull that has just been killed by a lion.
4.23.27
इष्ट्वा सङ्ग्रामयज्ञेन
रामप्रहरणाम्भसा ।
अस्मिन्नवभृथे स्नातः
कथं पत्न्या मया विना ॥
Having completed the Yajña of battle, how could you take the Avabhṛtha bath in the waters called Rāma’s strike, without me, your wife, by your side?
Tārā is saying, in a figurative manner, that she wishes she has also been killed by Rāma.
4.23.28
या दत्ता देवराजेन
तव तुष्टेन संयुगे ।
शातकौम्भीं प्रियां मालाम्
तां ते पश्यामि नेह किम् ॥
Why do I not see the golden chain given by the king of Dēvas pleased with you in battle?
4.23.29
राज्यश्रीर्नजहाति त्वाम्
गतासुमपि मानद ।
सूर्यस्यावर्तमानस्य
शैलराजमिव प्रभा ॥
O Mānada! But the splendor of royalty does not desert you even after you have lost your life, any more than the rays of the sun do when he goes behind the king of mountains.
4.23.30
न मे वचः पथ्यमिदं त्वया कृतं
न चास्मि शक्ता हि निवारणे तव ।
हता सपुत्राऽस्मि हतेन संयुगे
सह त्वया श्रीर्विजहाति मामपि ॥
Neither did you heed my beneficial advice nor was I able to stop you. With you losing your life in battle, I have been undone, along with my son. Along with you, Lakshmee has also left me.
Lakshmee is the goddess of prosperity, opulence, material wealth, resources and glory.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
किष्किन्धाकाण्डे त्रयोविंशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the twenty third Sarga in Kishkindhā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
