Baala Kaanda - Sarga 3
A repetition in prose feels redundant and annoying. But repetition in poetry and songs is enjoyable and reinforcing. Vālmeeki's Rāmāyaṇa is known as a poem and a song. It is as much about the beauty of narration as it is of the story. You will see many repetitions through out Rāmāyaṇa. As you get aligned with the characters and the story, you would like to hear it again and again. That, simply, is how celebration of goodness goes.
This Sarga, in a way, gives the summary of Rāmāyaṇa just as Sarga 1 did. But if you read it from the perspective of Vālmeeki, who just received the blessings from Brahma Dēva (in Sarga 2), you would identify yourself with the elation he is experiencing in envisioning the sequences in the story.
1.3.1
श्रुत्वा वस्तु समग्रं तत्
धर्मात्मा धर्मसंहितम् ।
व्यक्तमन्वेषते भूयो
यद्वृत्तं तस्य धीमतः ॥
That Dharmātma (Vālmeeki), having heard the story of Rāma in its entirety, that is full of deliberations on Dharma, sought for more detail that there could be.
It can be said that the entire world now knows the Sanskrit word Mahātma, because Gandhi, the apostle of non-violence, was honored with that title by Ravindranath Tagore.
Dharmātma is a similar word. It is a composite of two words Dharma and Ātma. It refers to a person for whom righteous behavior, righteous thoughts and focus on the nuances of righteousness is second nature.
We will use the word Dharmātma, as is, in this translation, since it is difficult to find an equivalent word in English.
1.3.2
उपस्पृश्योदकं सम्यक्
मुनिस्स्थित्वा कृताञ्जलिः ।
प्राचीनाग्रेषु दर्भेषु
धर्मेणान्वेषते गतिम् ॥
The Muni touched water and contemplated on the flow of events of the story sitting with folded hands on strands Darbha with their ends pointing to east.
Darbha is a type of grass, with long strands. It is widely used in Vēdic rituals of India for many symbolic purposes.
1.3.3
रामलक्ष्मणसीताभी
राज्ञा दशरथेन च ।
सभार्येण सराष्ट्रेण
यत् प्राप्तं तत्र तत्त्वतः ।
हसितं भाषितं चैव
गतिर्या यच्च चेष्टितम् ।
तत्सर्वं धर्मवीर्येण
यथावत्सम्प्रपश्यति ॥
He perceived objectively, through his mystical powers, the course of the lives of Rāma, Lakshmaṇa, Seetā, Daṡaratha and the kingdom, as it happened. He perceived all the conversations, activities and fun times that were part of their lives.
1.3.5
स्त्रीतृतीयेन च तथा
यत्प्राप्तं चरता वने ।
सत्यसन्धेन रामेण
तत्सर्वं चान्ववेक्षितम् ॥
He perceived all that had happened to Rāma, the one committed to his word, when he was in the Vana along with Seetā, a female in a party of three.
1.3.6
ततः पश्यति धर्मात्मा
तत्सर्वं योगमास्थितः ।
पुरा यत्तत्र निर्वृत्तम्
पाणावामलकं यथा ॥
The Dharmātma (Vālmeeki), established in Yoga, then perceived everything that had happened in detail as clearly as an Āmalaka fruit held in the palm.
1.3.7
तत्सर्वं तत्त्वतो दृष्ट्वा
धर्मेण स महाद्युतिः ।
अभिरामस्य रामस्य
चरितं कर्तुमुद्यतः ।
कामार्थगुणसंयुक्तम्
धर्मार्थगुणविस्तरम् ।
समुद्रमिव रत्नाढ्यम्
सर्वश्रुतिमनोहरम् ॥
Having perceived everything that there is and as is, the brilliant Vālmeeki set himself to write the story of Rāma, the beloved of all peoples, that delves into innumerable aspects of the pursuit of Dharma and the pursuit of Kāma, filled with gems like an ocean and flowing like sweet music to everyone’s ears.
Indic tradition is multi-dimensional. The prevalence of white and saffron robed unmarried Gurus and Swamis, and the popularity of Bhagavad Geeta and Yoga, in the recent decades, caused many people to think of Indic tradition as uni-dimensional, as if it is all about spirituality.
That is certainly far from truth. Indic tradition is a balanced and multi-dimensional outlook of life. It covers the entire gamut of life, by dividing it into four aspects: 1) the aspect of Dharma (responsibilities and righteousness), 2) the aspect of Artha (purpose and wealth), 3) the aspect of Kāma (desires and fancies) and 4) the aspect of Mōksha (self-inquiry and liberation).
Since the words Dharma, Artha, Kāma and Mōksha are popular enough now a days, we will use them without translating.
Note that only Dharma, Artha, Kāma are mentioned here, but not Mōksha. That is because the main focus of Rāmāyaṇa is about describing the life here, rather than the life beyond.
1.3.9
स यथा कथितं पूर्वम्
नारदेन महर्षिणा ।
रघुवंशस्य चरितम्
चकार भगवानृषिः ॥
Bhagawān and Ṛshi Vālmeeki then composed the story of the prince of Raghu dynasty as was given to him by Maharshi Nārada.
1.3.10
जन्म रामस्य सुमहत्
वीर्यं सर्वानुकूलताम् ।
लोकस्य प्रियतां क्षान्तिम्
सौम्यतां सत्यशीलताम् ॥
In the poem, he expounded on Rāma's life that can be held as a great example of forbearance, humility and truthfulness and that became acceptable to everyone and appreciated by everyone.
This entire block of ~30 Ṡlōkas here is actually one long sentence consisting the outline of all that Vālmeeki incorporated into the poem.
1.3.11
नानाचित्रकथाश्चान्या
विश्वामित्रसमागमे ।
जानक्याश्च विवाहं च
धनुषश्च विभेदनम् ॥
And on the captivating and exciting episodes of and heard from Viṡwāmitra, and about the breaking of the bow and wedding with the daughter of Janaka.
Seetā is the daughter of King Janaka and hence called Jānaki.
1.3.12
रामरामविवादं च
गुणान् दाशरथेस्तथा ।
तथाऽभिषेकं रामस्य
कैकेय्या दुष्टभावताम् ॥
And about the encounter between Rāma and Rāma, about the many virtues of Rāma, about the coronation of Rāma and about the evil plans of Kaikēyee.
The second Rāma in 'Rāma and Rāma' refers to Paraṡu Rāma.
1.3.13
विघातं चाभिषेकस्य
राघवस्य विवासनम् ।
राज्ञः शोकविलापं च
परलोकस्य चाश्रयम् ॥
And about how the prince of Raghu dynasty went in exile after the coronation was disrupted and about the king dying out of grief.
1.3.14
प्रकृतीनां विषादं च
प्रकृतीनां विसर्जनम् ।
निषादाधिपसंवादम्
सूतोपावर्तनं तथा ॥
And about the grief of the people, about taking leave of the people about meeting the Nishāda king and about prodding the charioteer to take leave.
Guha is the Nishāda king referred to here.
1.3.15
गङ्गायाश्चापि सन्तारम्
भरद्वाजस्य दर्शनम् ।
भरद्वाजाभ्यनुज्ञानात्
चित्रकूटस्य दर्शनम् ॥
And about crossing the river Gaṅgā, about visiting Bharadwāja, and taking leave of him to proceed to Citrakūṭa.
1.3.16
वास्तुकर्म निवेशं च
भरतागमनं तथा ।
प्रसादनं च रामस्य
पितुश्च सलिलक्रियाम् ॥
And about building a house to live, about the arrival of Bharata to reconcile with Rāma and about the customary water oblations to the deceased father.
1.3.17
पादुकाग्र्याभिषेकं च
नन्दिग्रामनिवासनम् ।
दण्डकारण्यगमनम्
विराधस्य वधं तथा ॥
And about the coronation of Rāma's footwear symbolizing the sovereignty of Rāma, about the stay of Bharata in Nandigrāma, about moving to Daṇḍaka Araṇya and about killing Virādha.
1.3.18
दर्शनं शरभङ्गस्य
सुतीक्ष्णेनाभिसङ्गमम् ।
अनसूयासहास्याम -
प्यङ्गरागस्य चार्पणम् ॥
And about meeting Ṡarabhaṅga and Suteekshṇa and about spending time with Anasūyā and receiving a skin cream or lotion called 'Aṅga Rāga' from her.
1.3.19
अगस्त्यदर्शनं चैव
जटायोरभिसङ्गमम् ।
पञ्चवट्याश्च गमनम्
शूर्पणख्याश्च दर्शनम् ॥
And about visiting Agastya, about meeting Jaṭāyu, about going to Pancavaṭi and about seeing Ṡūrpaṇakhā.
Pancavaṭi is a place in Daṇḍaka Araṇya, so named because of the five banyan trees there.
1.3.20
शूर्पणख्याश्च संवादम्
विरूपकरणं तथा ।
वधं खरत्रिशिरसोः
उत्थानं रावणस्य च ॥
And about the conversation with her and defacing her and about killing the Rākshasas Khara and Triṡira, triggering Rāvaṇa onto the scene.
1.3.21
मारीचस्य वधं चैव
वैदेह्या हरणं तथा ।
राघवस्य विलापं च
गृध्रराजनिबर्हणम् ॥
And about the killing of Māreeca, about the abduction of Seetā, about the grief of the prince of Raghu dynasty and about the death of Jaṭāyu, the king among eagles.
1.3.22
कबन्धदर्शनं चापि
पम्पायाश्चापि दर्शनम् ।
शबर्या दर्शनं चैव
हनूमद्दर्शनं तथा ॥
And about seeing Kabandha, about reaching the river Paṃpā about visiting Ṡabari and about meeting Hanumān.
1.3.23
ऋश्यमूकस्य गमनम्
सुग्रीवेण समागमम् ।
प्रत्ययोत्पादनं सख्यम्
वालिसुग्रीवविग्रहम् ॥
And about going to Ṛshyamūka mountain, about meeting Sugreeva and earning his confidence and friendship and about the feud between Sugreeva and Vāli.
1.3.24
वालिप्रमथनं चैव
सुग्रीवप्रतिपादनम् ।
ताराविलापं समयम्
वर्षरात्रनिवासनम् ॥
And about killing Vāli and establishing Sugreeva as the king, about the grief of Tāra, about the agreement and about getting through the rainy season.
1.3.25
कोपं राघवसिंहस्य
बलानामुपसङ्ग्रहम् ।
दिशः प्रस्थापनं चैव
पृथिव्याश्च निवेदनम् ॥
And about the wrath of the lion of Raghu dynasty, about consolidating of forces and sending them in all directions and about describing the places on the earth.
1.3.26
अङ्गुलीयकदानं च
ऋक्षस्य बिलदर्शनम् ।
प्रायोपवेशनं चापि
सम्पातेश्चापि दर्शनम् ॥
And about the passing of the ring, about seeing the Ṛksha cave about fasting unto death and about meeting Saṃpāti.
1.3.27
पर्वतारोहणं चैव
सागरस्य च लङ्घनम् ।
समुद्रवचनाच्चैव
मैनाकस्य च दर्शनम् ॥
And about climbing the mountain and jumping over the ocean and about meeting Maināka per the word of the God of Ocean.
1.3.28
सिंहिकायाश्च निधनम्
लङ्कामलयदर्शनम् ।
रात्रौ लङ्काप्रवेशं च
एकस्य च विचिन्तनम् ॥
And about the killing of Simhika, about seeing the mountains of Laṅkā, about entering Laṅkā in the night and about thinking through everything alone.
1.3.29
दर्शनं रावणस्यापि
पुष्पकस्य च दर्शनम् ।
आपानभूमिगमनः
अवरोधस्य दर्शनम् ॥
And about seeing Rāvaṇa and his Pushpaka Vimāna and about the drinking bar in the inner quarters.
1.3.30
अशोकवनिकायानम्
सीतायाश्चपि दर्शनम् ।
अभिज्ञानप्रदानं च
रावणस्य च दर्शनम् ॥
And about entering the Aṡōka Vana, about seeing Seetā, about giving the identifying ring and about seeing Rāvaṇa.
1.3.31
राक्षसीतर्जनं चैव
त्रिजटास्वप्नदर्शनम् ।
मणिप्रदानं सीताया
वृक्षभङ्गं तथैव च ॥
And about Rākshasis scaring (Seetā), about the portending dream of Trijaṭa about the giving of the diamond by Seetā (to Hanumān) and about smashing the trees.
1.3.32
राक्षसीविद्रवं चैव
किङ्कराणां निबर्हणम् ।
ग्रहणं वायुसूनोश्च
लङ्कादाहाभिगर्जनम् ॥
And about the fleeing of Rākshasis, about killing of the henchmen, about the capture of Hanumān and about the burning of Laṅkā.
1.3.33
प्रतिप्लवनमेवाथ
मधूनां हरणं तथा ।
राघवाश्वासनं चैव
मणिनिर्यातनं तथा ॥
And about jumping back over the ocean, about the grabbing of the liquor about consoling Rāma and about giving the diamond to him.
The reference to liquor in this Ṡlōka is about the episode where Vānaras enter into the guarded storehouse of the finest liquors and drink wildly in celebration of the success of their mission.
1.3.34
सङ्गमं च समुद्रेण
नलसेतोश्च बन्धनम् ।
प्रतारं च समुद्रस्य
रात्रौ लङ्कावरोधनम् ॥
And about going to the ocean, about the bridge built by Nala, about crossing the ocean and about descending upon Laṅkā in the night.
1.3.35
विभीषणेन संसर्गम्
वधोपायनिवेदनम् ।
कुम्भकर्णस्य निधनम्
मेघनादनिबर्हणम् ॥
And about the rendezvous with Vibheeshaṇa, about the strategy to kill and about the killing of Kumbhakarṇa and Mēghanātha.
1.3.36
रावणस्य विनाशं च
सीतावाप्तिमरेः पुरे ।
विभीषणाभिषेकं च
पुष्पकस्य च दर्शनम् ॥
And about destroying Rāvaṇa, about securing Seetā, about crowning Vibheeshaṇa in the city of the foe and about seeing the Pushpaka.
1.3.37
अयोध्यायाश्च गमनम्
भरतेन समागमम् ।
रामाभिषेकाभ्युदयम्
सर्वसैन्यविसर्जनम् ।
स्वराष्ट्ररञ्जनं चैव
वैदेह्याश्च विसर्जनम् ॥
And about going to Ayōdhyā, about meeting Bharata about the emergence of Rāma as the king, about dismissing the army, about the glorious rule of the kingdom and about giving up the princess of Vidēha.
1.3.38
अनागतं च यत्किञ्चित्
रामस्य वसुधातले ।
तच्चकारोत्तरे काव्ये
वाल्मीकिर्भगवानृषिः ॥
And then, Vālmeeki, the Bhagawān and the Ṛshi wrote as an appendix to the poem all that would happen hence, in the rest of Rāma's mortal life.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
बालकाण्डे तृतीयस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the third Sarga in Bāla Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
