Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 47
In this Sarga, the citizens wake up in the morning only to find that Rāma has left. Cursing themselves for their sound sleep, they turn back with heavy hearts and enter Ayōdhyā sad and disoriented.
2.47.1
प्रभातायां तु शर्वर्याम्
पौरास्ते राघवं विना ।
शोकोपहतनिश्चेष्टा
बभूवुर्हतचेतसः ॥
At dawn those inhabitants of the city, finding themselves without Rāghava, were bewildered and paralyzed by grief.
2.47.2
शोकजाश्रुपरिद्यूना
वीक्षमाणास्ततस्ततः ।
आलोकमपि रामस्य
न पश्यन्ति स्म दुःखिताः ॥
Drenched in tears of misery, they searched around everywhere. But to their great sorrow they found no trace of Rāma.
2.47.3
ते विषादार्तवदना
रहितास्तेन धीमता ।
कृपणाः करुणा वाचो
वदन्ति स्म मनस्विनः ॥
Deprived of the company of the prudent one, with their faces pale in dejection, those thoughtful souls spoke these helpless and piteous words:
2.47.4
धिगस्तु खलु निद्रां ताम्
ययाऽपहृतचेतसः ।
नाद्य पश्यामहे रामम्
पृथूरस्कं महाभुजम् ॥
Dhik! What a wretched sleep that completely took away our senses? We have now lost the sight of Rāma of broad chest and mighty arms!
2.47.5
कथं नाम महाबाहु
स्स तथावितथक्रियः ।
भक्तं जनं परित्यज्य
प्रवासं राघवो गतः ॥
How could he of mighty arm who would not do anything that does not serve a purpose, go into exile deserting his devoted subjects?
2.47.6
यो नः सदा पालयति
पिता पुत्रानिवौरसान् ।
कथं रघूणां स श्रेष्ठः
त्यक्त्वा नो विपिनं गतः ॥
How could he, the foremost of the Raghus, who always looked after us like a father does his own progeny, abandon us and go to the forest?
​THE Abrahamic world uses the metaphor of the father for god. Whereas, Indian civilization uses the metaphor of all relationships, mother, father, friend, teacher, etc. for god and the metaphor of father exclusively for a king. This civilization exercised the moral binding on the king to take care of his subjects, just as a father would his children. The social relationship was made as thick as a family relationship. It can be surmised that this is one of the factors why Indian civilization has thrived for so long, attracting people, including invaders, from all over the world.
2.47.7
इहैव निधनं यामो
महाप्रस्थानमेव वा ।
रामेण रहितानां हि
किमर्थं जीवितं हि नः ॥
We shall lay down our lives here. We shall go on the final journey of no return. For what good would life be to us, who have lost Rāma?
2.47.8
सन्ति शुष्काणि काष्ठानि
प्रभूतानि महान्ति च ।
तैः प्रज्वाल्य चितां सर्वे
प्रविशामोऽथ पावकम् ॥
There are enough big and dry logs here. Let us ignite them and throw ourselves into that fire now.
2.47.9
किं वक्ष्यामो महाबाहुः
अनसूयः प्रियंवदः ।
नीत स्स राघवोऽस्माभिः
इति वक्तुं कथं क्षमम् ॥
What can we say (to the people of Ayōdhyā)? How can we say that we have seen off the sweet-spoken Rāghava of mighty arm, who knows no envy, to the forest?
2.47.10
सा नूनं नगरी दीना
दृष्ट्वाऽस्मान् राघवं विना ।
भविष्यति निरानन्दा
सस्त्रीबालवयोधिका ॥
The wretched city, seeing us back without Rāghava, will, including its women, children and aged, be assuredly drained of all joy.
2.47.11
निर्यातास्तेन वीरेण
सह नित्यं जितात्मना ।
विहीनास्तेन च पुनः
कथं पश्याम तां पुरीम् ॥
Having left it with that Veera of complete restraint, how can we bear to see it again without him?'
2.47.12
इतीव बहुधा वाचो
बाहुमुद्यम्य ते जनाः ।
विलपन्ति स्म दुःखार्ता
विवत्सा इव धेनवः ॥
Thus, flinging their arms into the air, wailed those people in many words, tormented by grief, like cows that had lost their calves.
2.47.13
ततो मार्गानुसारेण
गत्वा किञ्चित् क्षणं पुनः ।
मार्गनाशाद्विषादेन
महता समभिप्लुताः ॥
They followed the tracks (of the chariot) for a while. And then finding them obliterated, they were overwhelmed by distress.
2.47.14
रथस्य मार्गनाशेन
न्यवर्तन्त मनस्विनः ।
किमिदं किं करिष्यामो
दैवेनोपहता इति ॥
As the tracks of the chariot were obliterated, resigned to their fate, they turned back saying: "Alas, what are we to do now, confounded by destiny?"
2.47.15
ततो यथागतेनैव
मार्गेण क्लान्तचेतसः ।
अयोध्यामगमन्सर्वे
पुरीं व्यथितसज्जनाम् ॥
Then, weary in mind, they traced back their path to the city of Ayōdhyā where every man of heart was troubled.
2.47.16
आलोक्य नगरीं तां च
क्षयव्याकुलमानसाः ।
आवर्तयन्त तेऽश्रूणि
नयनैः शोकपीडितैः ॥
With their minds drained and agitated, they shed tears, distressed upon seeing that city.
2.47.17
एषा रामेण नगरी
रहिता नातिशोभते ।
आपगा गरुडेनेव
ह्रदादुद्धृतपन्नगा ॥
Bereft of Rāma, this city is no more splendid, like a river whose protecting serpent was snatched away by Garuḍa.
2.47.18
चन्द्रहीनमिवाकाशम्
तोयहीनमिवार्णवम् ।
अपश्यन्निहतानन्दम्
नगरं ते विचेतसः ॥
With their minds so disoriented, they saw a city whose sense of joy was completely destroyed, resembling a sky without the moon and an ocean without water.
2.47.19
ते तानि वेश्मानि महाधनानि
दुःखेन दुःखोपहता विशन्तः ।
नैव प्रजज्ञुः स्वजनं जनं वा
निरीक्षमाणाः प्रविनष्टहर्षाः ॥
They entered their opulent houses grieving and struck by sadness. With all traces of joy evaporated, they were at a loss even to recognize their own people, or anyone else for that matter.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे सप्तचत्वारिंशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the forty-seventh Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
