Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 57
In this Sarga, after Rāma has crossed to the other side of Gaṅgā, Guha talks at length with Sumantra and goes back home.
Everyone there, then learn that Rāma has reached Citrakūṭa. Sumantra goes back to Ayōdhyā, which is joyless and gripped by silence.
Sumantra conveys to the king everything verbatim, from Rāma. Daṡaratha faints once again and is unable to say a word in response.
Kousalyā speaks in a very caustic tone for all that the king has brought upon himself and all others.
2.57.1
कथयित्वा सुदुःखार्तः
सुमन्त्रेण चिरं सह ।
रामे दक्षिणकूलस्थे
जगाम स्वगृहं गुहः ॥
After Rāma had gone to the southern side, Guha, greatly grieving, talked to Sumantra for a great while, and then went to his house.
2.57.2
भरद्वाजाभिगमनम्
प्रयागे च सहाऽसनम् ।
आगिरेर्गमनं तेषाम्
तत्रस्थैरुपलक्षितम् ॥
Everyone there, then, learnt of their (Rāma, Lakshmaṇa and Seetā's) meeting with Bharadwāja, their stay with him at Prayāga and about their reaching the mountain.
2.57.3
अनुज्ञातस्सुमन्त्रोऽथ
योजयित्वा हयोत्तमान् ।
अयोध्यामेव नगरीम्
प्रययौ गाढदुर्मनाः ॥
Sumantra, with a heavy heart yoked the exquisite horses and started his journey back to the city of Ayōdhyā, as had been instructed by Rāma.
2.57.4
स वनानि सुगन्धीनि
सरितश्च सरांसि च ।
पश्यन्नतिययौ शीघ्रम्
ग्रामाणि नगराणि च ॥
He swiftly proceeded, seeing streams and lakes and fragrant woods, and villages and towns on his way.
2.57.5
ततस्सायाह्न समये
तृतीयेऽहनि सारधिः ।
अयोध्यां समनुप्राप्य
निरानन्दां ददर्श ह ॥
On the evening of the third day, the charioteer reached Ayōdhyā and found it joyless.
2.57.6
स शून्यामिव निश्शब्दाम्
दृष्ट्वा परमदुर्मनाः ।
सुमन्त्रश्चिन्तयामास
शोकवेगसमाहतः ॥
Sumantra's spirits sank into an abyss seeing it silent, as if it was empty. Hit by a tide of grief, he wondered:
2.57.7
कच्चिन्न सगजा साऽश्वा
सजना सजनाधिपा ।
रामसन्तापदुःखेन
दग्धा शोकाग्निना पुरी ॥
I hope the city, along with its elephants, horses, people, and their lords was not burnt down by the fire of sorrow and the pain and sadness for Rāma!
2.57.8
इति चिन्तापरस्सूतो
वाजिभिश्शीघ्रपातिभिः ।
नगरद्वारमासाद्य
त्वरितः प्रविवेश ह ॥
Consumed thus by grief, the charioteer reached the gates of the city borne by the fleet-footed horses, and entered it in all haste.
2.57.9
सुमन्त्रमभियान्तं तम्
शतशोऽथ सहस्रशः ।
क्व राम इति पृच्छन्तः
सूतमभ्यद्रवन्नराः ॥
On seeing Sumantra coming, hundreds and thousands of people ran after him asking, "Where is Rāma?"
2.57.10
तेषां शशंस गङ्गायाम्
अहमापृच्छ्य राघवम् ।
अनुज्ञातो निवृत्तोऽस्मि
धार्मिकेण महात्माना ॥
He told them, "I asked Mahātma Rāghava, the Dhārmika, on the banks of Gaṅgā, (about what I should do) and by his order, I returned!"
2.57.11
ते तीर्णा इति विज्ञाय
बाष्पपूर्णमुखा जनाः ।
अहो धिगिति निश्श्वस्य
हा रामेति च चुक्रुशुः ॥
On coming to know that they had crossed Gaṅgā, they sighed, exclaiming, 'Dhik!'; and with their faces full of tears, they cried aloud, 'Oh Rāma!'
2.57.12
शुश्राव च वचस्तेषाम्
बृन्दं बृन्दं च तिष्ठताम् ।
हतास्म खलु ये नेह
पश्याम इति राघवम् ॥
And he heard them gathered in groups saying, "We are indeed undone, for Rāma is not to be seen here".
2.57.13
दानयज्ञविवाहेषु
समाजेषु महत्सु च ।
न द्रक्ष्यामः पुनर्जातु
धार्मिकं राममन्तरा ॥
We will not see Rāma the Dhārmika, in weddings, Yajñas, charitable giveaways and in the major assemblies.
Vālmeeki's technique is to introduce us to the past by references to it from the events of the present.
In this Ṡlōka, he gives us an idea of how Rāma became part of the lives of the people of Ayōdhyā, by his appearances in various events and gatherings.
Notice the reference to Dāna, or charitable giveaways, here.
Of late, youth are being fed the capitalistic idea that everyone has to earn their own food and work for their own growth and grandeur.
The order of things in India of yore was different. Everyone worked according to their natural abilities. The more able earned more and the less able earned less.
Those who earned more had to feel no guilt. Those who earned less had to feel no shame. No one was demonized for being rich and no one was berated for being poor.
And through charitable activities conducted at events like weddings, Yajñas and so on, the people who earned more used to share their riches with people who earned less.
And the sharing used to create a social bonding and appreciation that is free from economic calculations. This order of things is still somewhat present in India and one hopes that it will sustain.
Rich people are criticized in India for lavish weddings, by neo-activists. But they should understand one thing. It is a mechanism for money to flow from the haves into the economy. If we make them feel guilty for being lavish, and if we make the spending unfashionable, where would we think the money goes?
It will go into building golf courses, which only rich people use and into lavish mansions in which two rich people live with ten rich imported dogs.
Traditional marriage in India employs people of every trade and profession, and connects them together, besides making the money flow to everyone.
2.57.14
किं समर्थं जनस्यास्य
किं प्रियं किं सुखावहम् ।
इति रामेण नगरम्
पितृवत्परिपालितम् ॥
"What is useful for the people? What pleases them? What gives them comfort?" Such was the constant thought with which Rāma looked after this city like a father.
2.57.15
वातायनगतानां च
स्त्रीणामन्वन्तरापणम् ।
रामशोकाभितप्तानाम्
शुश्राव परिदेवनम् ॥
He (Sumantra) heard the weeping cries of people in the bazaars and of women from the windows (of the houses), who were burnt by grief on account of Rāma.
2.57.16
स राजमार्गमध्येन
सुमन्त्रः पिहिताननः ।
यत्र राजा दशरथः
तदेवोपययौ गृहम् ॥
Sumantra, covering his face, drove along the royal pathway straight to the mansion where king Daṡaratha was.
2.57.17
सोऽवतीर्य रथाच्छीघ्रम्
राजवेश्म प्रविश्य च ।
कक्ष्यास्सप्ताभिचक्राम
महाजनसमाकुलाः ॥
Quickly alighting from the chariot, he entered the king's residence and passed through seven courts that were thronged by sobbing crowds of people.
2.57.18
हर्म्यै र्विमानैः प्रासादैः
अवेक्ष्याथ समागतम् ।
हाहाकारकृता नार्यो
रमादर्शनकर्शिताः ॥
On seeing him who thus arrived, the women, from their mansions, high towers and palaces, wailed, "Alas, alas!', pining for the sight of Rāma's face.
2.57.19
आयतैर्विमलैर्नेत्रैः
अश्रुवेगपरिप्लुतैः ।
अन्योन्यमभिवीक्षन्तेऽ -
व्यक्तमार्ततराः स्त्रियः ॥
With their wide and lustrous eyes blurred by swift-flowing tears, the sorely distressed women looked at each other, feeling at a loss.
2.57.20
ततो दशरथस्त्रीणाम्
प्रासादेभ्य स्तत स्ततः ।
रामशोकाभितप्तानाम्
मन्दं शुश्राव जल्पितम् ॥
He heard along his way from the palaces, Daṡaratha's women speaking in low voices burnt by the grief on account of Rāma:
2.57.21
सह रामेण निर्यातो
विना राम मिहागतः ।
सूतः किन्नाम कौसल्याम्
शोचन्तीं प्रतिवक्ष्यति ॥
Having gone forth with Rāma and come back without him, what will the charioteer say to the weeping Kousalyā ?
2.57.22
यथा च मन्ये दुर्जीवम्
एवं न सुकरं ध्रुवम् ।
आच्छिद्य पुत्रे निर्याते
कौसल्या यत्र जीवति ॥
When I find it so hard to go on living, how can Kousalyā find it easy to cling to life, with her son was taken away so forcibly from her!
2.57.23
सत्यरूपं तु तद्वाक्यम्
राज्ञः स्त्रीणां निशामयन् ।
प्रदीप्तमिव शोकेन
विवेश सहसा गृहम् ॥
Hearing those very true words of the women of the king, he entered that house without delay, which seemed to have been set ablaze with grief.
2.57.24
स प्रविश्याष्टमीं कक्ष्याम्
राजानं दीनमातुरम् ।
पुत्रशोकपरिम्लानम्
अपश्यत्पाण्डुरे गृहे ॥
Entering the eighth court, he saw the dejected king wilted by the grief for his son, against the backdrop of that white residence.
2.57.25
अभिगम्य तमासीनम्
नरेन्द्रे मभिवाद्य च ।
सुमन्त्रो रामवचनम्
यथोक्तं प्रत्यवेदयत् ॥
Approaching the seated King and saluting him, Sumantra relayed to him Rāma's words verbatim.
2.57.26
स तूष्णीमेव तच्छ्रुत्वा
राजा विभ्रान्तचेतनः ।
मूर्छितो न्यपतद्भूमौ
रामशोकाभिपीडितः ॥
The king heard those words silently. With his senses disoriented, he then fainted on the ground, extremely distressed by the grief for Rāma.
2.57.27
ततोऽन्तःपुरमाविद्धम्
मूर्छिते पृथिवीपतौ ।
उद्धृत्य बाहू चुक्रोश
नृपतौ पतिते क्षितौ ॥
As that lord of the lands fainted, the entire inner quarters felt a whip of grief. And as the king fell down on the ground, the cries went up with hands flung upwards.
It is hard not to get captivated by the imagery that Vālmeeki paints.
The King fainted; and it was like a whiplash on everyone.
The King fell down; and everyone's hands went up.
Screenplay is ready for a cinematographer.
2.57.28
सुमित्रया तु सहिता
कौसल्या पतितं पतिम् ।
उत्थापयामास तदा
वचनं चेदमब्रवीत् ॥
Kousalyā, with the aid of Sumitrā, raised her fallen husband up from the ground, and said to him:
2.57.29
इमं तस्य महाभाग
दूतं दुष्करकारिणः ।
वनवासादनुप्राप्तम्
कस्मान्न प्रतिभाषसे ॥
O blessed one! He (Rāma) has done something that is impossible even to imagine. From his dwelling in the Vana, came now his emissary. Why don't you respond?
2.57.30
अद्यैवमनयं कृत्वा
व्यपत्रपसि राघव ।
उत्तिष्ठ सुकृतं तेस्तु
शोके नस्या त्सहायता ॥
You are now feeling ashamed, after having done the improbable. O Rāghava! Get up! I hope it adds to the count of your good deeds! Crying is going to be of no use!
The king has been exclusively spending time with Kaikēyee, which brought all this upon the king, and everyone else. The innuendo and scorn in these Ṡlōkas by the other wives is hardly subtle.
Also these Ṡlōkas are an indication that women, in those days, enjoyed the power and freedom to speak up to their husbands, when it mattered and is justified.
2.57.31
देव यस्या भयाद्रामम्
नानुपृच्छसि सारथिम् ।
नेह तिष्ठिति कैकेयी
विस्रब्धं प्रतिभाष्यताम् ॥
My lord! Kaikēyee, for fear of whom you do not ask the charioteer of Rāma, is not here. You can speak without fear!
2.57.32
सा तथोक्त्वा महाराजम्
कौसल्या शोकलालसा ।
धरण्यां निपपाताऽशु
बाष्पविप्लुतभाषिणी ॥
Having said this to the esteemed king, Kousalyā quickly fell on the ground, her words drowned in tears.
2.57.33
एवं विलपतीं दृष्ट्वा
कौसल्यां पतितां भुवि ।
पतिं चावेक्ष्य ता स्सर्वा
सुस्वरं रुरुदुः स्त्रियः ॥
Seeing Kousalyā fallen on the ground thus lamenting, all the women wept aloud looking at their husband.
2.57.34
ततस्तमन्तःपुरनादमुत्थितं
समीक्ष्य वृद्धास्तरुणाश्च मानवाः ।
स्त्रियश्च सर्वा रुरुदु स्समन्ततः
पुरं तदासीत्पुनरेव सङ्कुलम् ॥
Moved by those sounds that emerged from the inner quarters, men, young and old, and all women everywhere wept. Once again, the city was in a tumult.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे सप्तपञ्चाशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the fifty seventh Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
