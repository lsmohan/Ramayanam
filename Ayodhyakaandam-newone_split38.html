Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 38
In this Sarga, seeing Seetā wear the robes of bark, everyone says 'shame on you!' to Daṡaratha.
Daṡaratha lashes at Kaikēyee, for making Seetā wear robes of bark, which is beyond the promise made by him.
Set to leave to the Vana, Rāma asks the king to take good care of his mother Kousalyā while he is away.
2.38.1
तस्यां चीरं वसानायाम्
नाथवत्यामनाथवत् ।
प्रचुक्रोश जनस्सर्वो
धिक्त्वां दशरथं त्विति ॥
Seeing Seetā wear those robes of bark, like an orphan with no one to care, even though she had her husband right by her, everyone cried aloud, saying 'Dhik!' to Daṡaratha!
'Dhik!' means 'Shame on you!'
What a fair life did the people of those times lead! People were able to say 'Dhik!' to the supreme lord of the kingdom without any fear or apprehension!
Dharma, indeed, seems to have ruled above everything else, in those times.
2.38.2
तेन तत्र प्रणादेन
दुःखितस्स महीपतिः ।
चिच्छेद जीविते श्रद्धाम्
धर्मे यशसि चात्मनः ॥
So grieved was the King on hearing that indignant outburst that he lost all interest in life, in Dharma and in his own good repute.
2.38.3
स निः श्वस्योष्णमैक्ष्वाक
स्तां भार्यामिदमब्रवीत् ।
कैकेयि कुशचीरेण
न सीता गन्तुमर्हति ॥
Heaving hot sighs, the king of Ikshwāku dynasty said to his wife, "O Kaikēyee, Seetā shall not go clad in this attire of Kuṡa grass"!
2.38.4
सुकुमारी च बाला च
सततं च सुखोचिता ।
नेयं वनस्य योग्येति
सत्यमाह गुरुर्मम ॥
What my Guru said is correct. She is young, delicate and used to every comfort. The life of Vana is not for her!
2.38.5
इयं हि कस्यापकरोति किञ्चि-
त्तपस्विनी राजवरस्य कन्या ।
या चीरमासाद्य जनस्य मध्ये
स्थिता विसंज्ञाश्रमणीव काचित् ॥
She is standing here with this robe in the midst of everyone like an ascetic, completely disoriented! She is the daughter of a great king! What harm did this disciplined and pious one cause to anyone?
2.38.6
चीराण्यपास्याज्जनकस्य कन्या
नेयं प्रतिज्ञा मम दत्तपूर्वा ।
यथासुखं गच्छतु राजपुत्री
वनं समग्रा सह सर्वरत्नैः ॥
May the daughter of Janaka drop the robes of bark! This is not part of my promise. Let that princess go in all comfort taking everything that is precious for her!
2.38.7
अजीवनार्हेण मया नृशंसा
कृता प्रतिज्ञा नियमेन तावत् ।
तवया हि बाल्यात् प्रतिपन्नमेतत्
तन्मां दहेद्वेणुमिवात्मपुष्पम् ॥
I do not deserve to hang on to this life any more. I have made such a dreadful promise. Your stupidity has taken full advantage of it! I will be completely burned by this, just as a bamboo is burned by its own flowers.
See wikipedia.org for the details of mass flowering of Bamboo.
2.38.8
रामेण यदि ते पापे
किञ्चित्कृतमशोभनम् ।
अपकारः क इह ते
वैदेह्या दर्शितोऽधमे ॥
O sinful wretch! Whether or not Rāma has done anything unbecoming to you, what offense did the princess of Vidēha commit against you?
2.38.9
मृगीवोत्फुल्लनयना
मृदुशीला तपस्विनी ।
अपकारं कमिह ते
करोति जनकात्मजा ॥
What harm did the daughter of Janaka, the woman of gentle and austere ways, with eyes wide like those of a doe, do to you?
2.38.10
ननु पर्याप्त मेतत्ते
पापे रामविवासनम् ।
किमेभिः कृपणैर्भूयः
पातकैरपि ते कृतैः ॥
O sinful one! It is enough that you send Rāma in exile! Why do you need to commit even more abhorrent sins like these?
2.38.11
प्रतिज्ञातं मया तावत्
त्वयोक्तं देवि शृण्वता ।
रामं यदभिषेकाय
त्वमिहागतमब्रवीः ॥
I have promised you only what you have asked for, when Rāma came here all set for consecration.
2.38.12
तत्त्वेतत्समतिक्रम्य
निरयं गन्तुमिच्छसि ।
मैथिलीमपि या हि त्वम्
ईक्षसे चीरवासिनीम् ॥
By going beyond that and by watching the princess of Mithilā wear robes of bark, you are certainly asking for hell!
2.38.13
इतीव राजा विलपन्महात्मा
शोकस्य नान्तं स ददर्श किञ्चित् ।
भृशातुरत्वाच्च पपात भूमौ
तेनैव पुत्रव्यसनेन मग्नः ॥
Lamenting thus, and finding no end to that grief, the king sank down to the ground, deeply troubled by the fate of his son.
2.38.14
एवं ब्रुवन्तं पितरम्
रामस्सम्प्रस्थितो वनम् ।
अवाक्छिरसमासीनम्
इदं वचनमब्रवीत् ॥
Rāma, who was set for his departure to Vana, then spoke to his father, who said thus and sat down with his head hanging down.
Rāma realizes that nothing will change because of the chiding by Sumantra, Siddhārtha and Daṡaratha. So, as he leaves for the Vana, he asks the king to take good care of his mother, just as he asked her (2.24.24-25) earlier to take good care of his father.
2.38.15
इयं धार्मिक कौसल्या
मम माता यशस्विनी ।
वृद्धा चाक्षुद्रशीला च
न च त्वां देव गर्हते ॥
O Dhārmika! My mother Kousalyā of great repute is aged now. She has never been mean nor did she ever slight you in any way.
2.38.16
मया विहीनां वरद
प्रपन्नां शोकसागरम् ।
अदृष्टपूर्वव्यसनाम्
भूयस्सम्मन्तुमर्हसि ॥
Hence, O benefactor of all, may you take extremely good care of her, who is now sunk into an ocean of grief on account of the separation from me. She has never before experienced hardship like this.
2.38.17
पुत्रशोकं यथा नर्च्छेत्
त्वया पूज्येन पूजिता ।
मां हि सञ्चिन्तयन्ती सा
त्वयि जीवेत्तपस्विनी ॥
I hope she will be so well regarded by you, the respectable one, that she, the troubled one, would never sink into grief and gloom of separation from me!
Vālmeeki never seems to get enough of writing about grief. And there is a good reason for it.
Remember that the sight of the bird that sunk into grief as her husband was shot down by a fowler, is still hanging heavily on Vālmeeki.
That grief echoes itself through his pen, all through Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa and later again starting in Araṇya Kāṇḍa after Seetā is separated from Rāma.
2.38.18
इमां महेंन्द्रोपम! जातगर्धिनीं
तथा विधातुं जननीं ममार्हसि ।
यथा वनस्थे मयि शोककर्शिता
न जीवितं न्यस्य यमक्षयं व्रजेत् ॥
You are comparable to the magnanimous Indra! May you do everything needed to ensure that my mother does not lay down her life and leave to the abode of Yama while I am away in the Vana, pining from grief and yearning to see me!
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे अष्टत्रिंशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the thirty eighth Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
