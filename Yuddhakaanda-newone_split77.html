Yuddha Kaanda - Sarga 77
In this Sarga, Nikumbha is outraged at his brother's death and jumps into the fray with a huge Parigha, at the very sight of which everyone becomes scared. Only Hanumān dares make a stand against Nikumbha, who hurls that Parigha at him, which gets shattered upon hitting his chest. In the duel that ensues between them, Hanumān twists Nikumbha's neck and pulls his head out. All the Vānaras shout in joy at the death of Nikumbha.
6.77.1
निकुम्भो भ्रातरं दृष्ट्वा
सुग्रीवेण निपातितम् ।
प्रदहन्निव कोपेन
वानरेन्द्रमवैक्षत ॥
Seeing his brother struck down by Sugreeva, the king of Vānaras, Nikumbha was outraged, and cast a glance at him, as if it would burn him.
6.77.2
ततः स्रग्दामसन्नद्धम्
दत्तपञ्चाङ्गुलं शुभम् ।
आददे परिघं वीरो
नगेन्द्रशिखरोपमम् ।
हेमपट्टपरिक्षिप्तम्
वज्रविद्रुमभूषितम् ।
यमदण्डोपमं भीमम्
रक्षसां भयनाशनम् ॥
Then that valiant one grabbed a Parigha, which was adorned with flower garlands, stamped with auspicious marks of five fingers, bound with gold bands and decked with diamonds and corals.
Imposing like a mountain peak and terrifying like the staff of Yama, it dispelled the fears of all Rākshasas.
6.77.4
तमाविध्य महातेजाः
शक्रध्वजसमं तदा ।
निननाद विवृत्तास्यो
निकुम्भो भीमविक्रमः ॥
Nikumbha of formidable prowess and extraordinary power held it tight and let off a mighty roar, opening his mouth wide.
6.77.5
उरोगतेन निष्केण
भुजस्थैरङ्गदैरपि ।
कुण्डलाभ्यां च चित्राभ्याम्
मालया च विचित्रया ।
निकुम्भो भूषणैर्भाति
तेन स्म परिघेण च ।
यथेन्द्रधनुषा मेघः
सविद्युत् स्तनयित्नुमान् ॥
With a gold medallion on his chest, armlets on his arms, wearing many ornaments including stylish ear-pendents and a curious-looking garland, Nikumbha, holding that Parigha, looked like a cloud with a rainbow and lightning playing on it.
6.77.7
परिघाग्रेण पुस्फोट
वातग्रन्थिर्महात्मनः ।
प्रजज्वाल सघोषश्च
विधूम इव पावकः ॥
The knot that holds the winds together was shattered by the tip of the booming Parigha of that Mahātma, which blazed like a fire free of smoke.
"The knot that holds the winds together" refers to the story of Maruts, who represent seven different kinds of winds, Āvaha, Pravaha, Vivaha, Paravaha, Udvaha, Saṃvaha, Parivaha (Mareeci). See Sarga 47 of Bāla Kāṇḍa for the story of Maruts.
It is said that Japanese animation stories take inspiration from the Rāmāyaṇa, which could be true, as the description in these three Ṡlōkas, like many others elsewhere, is bound to fire up the imagination of visual artists.
6.77.8
नगर्या विटपावत्या
गन्धर्वभवनोत्तमैः ।
सह चैवामरावत्या
सर्वैश्च भवनैः सह ।
सतारग्रहनक्षत्रम्
सचन्द्रं समहाग्रहम् ।
निकुम्भपरिघाघूर्णम्
भ्रमतीव नभस्स्थलम् ॥
The city of Viṭapāvati (Alakā) and its splendid homes of the Gandharvas, the city of Amarāvati and all its mansions, the stars, the planets, the constellations, the moon and the super-planets, seemed to be thrown into a swirl by the gush of wind from the tip of Nikumbha’s Parigha.
6.77.10
दुरासदश्च सञ्जज्ञे
परिघाभरणप्रभः ।
क्रोधेन्दनो निकुम्भाग्निः
युगान्ताग्निरिवोत्थितः ॥
The dazzle of the ornaments of that Parigha was so blinding that no one could dare set their eyes on it. Fueled by outrage, the fire called Nikumbha blazed like the conflagration at the end of the Yuga.
6.77.11
राक्षसा वानराश्चापि
न शेकुः स्पन्दितुं भयात् ।
हनुमांस्तु विवृत्योरः
तस्थौ प्रमुखतो बली ॥
Vānaras and Rākshasas alike dared not so much as stir in their places. But Hanumān, the strong, stood facing him squarely, with his chest expanded.
6.77.12
परिघोपमबाहुस्तु
परिघं भास्करप्रभम् ।
बली बलवतस्तस्य
पातयामास वक्षसि ॥
That strong one (Nikumbha), whose arms resembled Parighas, threw that Parigha that dazzled like the sun, on the chest of that strong one (Hanumān).
6.77.13
स्थिरे तस्योरसि व्यूढे
परिघः शतधा कृतः ।
विकीर्यमाणः सहसा
उल्काशतमिवाम्बरे ॥
On that vast expanse of his rock-like chest the Parigha instantly shivered into a hundred fragments, and fell scattered like a hundred meteors from the sky.
6.77.14
स तु तेन प्रहारेण
विचचाल महाकपिः ।
परिघेण समाधूतो
यथाभूमिचलेऽचलः ॥
The great Vānara was moved and shaken, like a mountain during an earthquake, from the blow of that Parigha.
6.77.15
स तदाऽभिहतस्तेन
हनुमान् प्लवगोत्तमः ।
मुष्टिं संवर्तयामास
बलेनातिमहाबलः ॥
Then Hanumān, the best of Vānaras, of limitless and immense strength, doubled his fist, putting all his strength into it.
6.77.16
तमुद्यम्य महातेजा
निकुम्भोरसि वीर्यवान् ।
अभिचिक्षेप वेगेन
वेगवान् वायुविक्रमः ॥
That valiant one of great speed and immense power, whose impact equaled that of the wind, lifted his fist and lodged it on the chest of Nikumbha, with all speed.
6.77.17
ततः पुस्फोट वर्मास्य
प्रसुस्राव च शोणितम् ।
मुष्टिना तेन सञ्जज्ञे
मेघे विद्युदिवोत्थिता ॥
The armor was cracked and a profusion of blood gushed out, from that blow of the fist.
And there arose flames like lightning from a cloud.
6.77.18
स तु तेन प्रहारेण
निकुम्भो विचचाल ह ।
स्वस्थश्चापि निजग्राह
हनुमन्तं महाबलम् ॥
Nikumbha was terribly shaken by that blow. Then, after steadying himself, he caught hold of Hanumān of extraordinary strength.
6.77.19
चुक्रुशुश्च तदा सङ्ख्ये
भीमं लङ्कानिवासिनः ।
निकुम्भेनोद्यतं दृष्ट्वा
हनुमन्तं महाबलम् ॥
The residents of Laṅkā let off loud shouts in that battle seeing Nikumbha lift up Hanumān of immense strength.
6.77.20
स तदा ह्रियमाणोऽपि
कुम्भकर्णात्मजेन ह ।
आजघानानिलसुतो
वज्रकल्पेन मुष्टिना ॥
But even as he was being carried off by Kumbhakarṇa’s son, the son of Vāyu struck him with his fist that could strike as hard as Vajra.
6.77.21
आत्मानं मोचयित्वाऽथ
क्षितावभ्यवपद्यत ।
हनुमानुन्ममाथाशु
निकुम्भं मारुतात्मजः ॥
Freeing himself (from the grip of Nikumbha), and finding a strong foothold on the ground, Hanumān, the son of Vāyu, pummeled Nikumbha.
6.77.22
निक्षिप्य परमायत्तो
निकुम्भं निष्पिपेष ह ।
उत्पत्य चास्य वेगेन
पपातोरसि वीर्यवान् ॥
He threw Nikumbha down on the ground with all his force and pounded him. Then he sprang up (into the air) and threw himself down on Nikumbha's chest.
6.77.23
परिगृह्य च बाहुभ्याम्
परिवृत्य शिरोधराम् ।
उत्पाटयामास शिरो
भैरवं नदतो महत् ॥
Seizing with both hands, he twisted his neck as he screamed horribly, and pulled out his huge head.
6.77.24
अथ नदति निपातिते निकुम्भे
पवनसुतेन तदा रणस्य मध्ये ।
दशरथसुतवाहिनी समग्रा
परममागत्सकला तदा प्रहर्षम् ॥
With Nikumbha thus struck down by Hanumān in the thick of that fight, the entire army of the son of Daṡaratha was filled with an unbridled and extreme joy.
These two Ṡlōkas are considered unauthentic.
6.77.25
व्यपेते तु जीवे निकुम्भस्य हृष्टा
विनेदुः प्लवङ्गा दिशः सस्वनुश्च ।
चचालेव चोर्वी पपातेव सा द्यौः
भयं राक्षसानां बलं चाविवेश ॥
With the life of Nikumbha squeezed out, the cardinals reverberated with the joyous shouts of the Vānaras, the earth rocked, the sky seemed to fall and fear entered the hearts of the Rākshasa forces.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
युद्धकाण्डे सप्तसप्ततितमः सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the seventy seventh Sarga in Yuddha Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
