Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 112
In this Sarga, the Ṛshis, eager to see the end of Rāvaṇa, urge Bharata to follow the advice of Rāma. Rāma is happy to get the support of Ṛshis in his adherence to Dharma.
However, Bharata trembles as it becomes clear that his plea is overturned. He falls at the feet of Rāma and beseeches him again to take the responsibility of the kingdom.
Rāma takes Bharata onto his lap and tells him that he should take care of the kingdom with the help of the sagacious ministers, officers and others.
Bharata then asks Rāma to put on a pair of gold covered sandals and give it to him. Bharata says that those sandals will take care of the kingdom and he will live outside the city wearing robes of bark for fourteen years till Rāma comes back.
Rāma then bids farewell to all and enters his cottage, weeping.
2.112.1
तमप्रतिमतेजोभ्यां
भ्रातृभ्यां रोमहर्षणम् ।
विस्मितास्सङ्गमं प्रेक्ष्य
समवेता महर्षयः ॥
The Maharshis assembled there were astonished witnessing that exchange between those two brothers of incomparable splendor, which made one's hair stand on end.
2.112.2
अन्तर्हिता मुनिगणाः
सिद्धाश्च परमर्षयः ।
तौ भ्रातरौ महात्मानौ
काकुत्स्थौ प्रशशंसिरे ॥
The invisible hosts of Munis, Siddhas and foremost Ṛshis praised the Mahātmas, the brothers of the Kākutstha lineage.
2.112.3
स धन्यो यस्य पुत्रौ द्वौ
धर्मज्ञौ धर्मविक्रमौ ।
श्रुत्वा वयं हि सम्भाषाम्
उभयोस्स्पृहयामहे ॥
He was indeed fortunate, to have two such sons who are so conversant with Dharma, and so steadfast in their adherence to it. The more we hear them, the more we are captivated by them.
2.112.4
ततस्त्वृषिगणाः क्षिप्रं
दशग्रीववधैषिणः ।
भरतं राजशार्दूलम्
इत्यूचु स्सङ्गता वचः ॥
Then, the host of Ṛshis, who were eager to see the death of the ten-headed one (Rāvaṇa), got together and said to Bharata, the tiger among men:
2.112.5
कुले जात महाप्राज्ञ
महावृत्त महायशः ।
ग्राह्यं रामस्य वाक्यं ते
पितरं यद्यवेक्षसे ॥
O you of great lineage, great discernment, great character and great renown! If you are considerate to your father, you should take the advice of Rāma!
2.112.6
सदाऽनृणमिमं रामं
वयमिच्छामहे पितुः ।
आनृणत्वाच्च कैकेय्याः
स्वर्गं दशरथो गतः ॥
We would like to see Rāma become free of debt to his father. It was by paying his debt to Kaikēyee that Daṡaratha went to Heaven.
2.112.7
एतावदुक्त्वा वचनं
गन्धर्वास्समहर्षयः ।
राजर्षयश्चैव तदा
सर्वे स्वां स्वां गतिं गताः ॥
Having said this, the Gandharvas, the Maharshis and the Rājarshis went their ways.
2.112.8
ह्लादितस्तेन वाक्येन
शुभेन शुभदर्शनः ।
रामस्संहृष्टवदनः
तानृषीनभ्यपूजयत् ॥
Delighted by those auspicious words, Rāma, of auspicious bearing, paid obeisance to those Ṛshis, with relief on his face.
2.112.9
त्रस्तगात्रस्तु भरतः
स वाचा सज्जमानया ।
कृताञ्जलिरिदं वाक्यं
राघवं पुनरब्रवीत् ॥
But Bharata, with a shiver running down his limbs again spoke to Rāma with folded hands, in faltering voice:
2.112.10
राजधर्ममनुप्रेक्ष्य
कुलधर्मानुसन्ततिम् ।
कर्तुमर्हसि काकुत्स्थ
मम मातुश्च याचनाम् ॥
O Kākutstha! Considering the (gravity of) kingly affairs and the continuation of convention of the family, you should accede to my prayer and my mother's.
2.112.11
रक्षितुं सुमहद्राज्यम्
अहमेकस्तु नोत्सहे ।
पौरजानपदांश्चापि
रक्तान्रञ्जयितुं तथा ॥
I do not want to venture to protect this extremely vast kingdom and satisfy the loving city people and country folk, all by myself.
2.112.12
ज्ञातयश्च हि योधाश्च
मित्राणि सुहृदश्च नः ।
त्वामेव प्रतिवीक्षन्ते
पर्जन्यमिव कर्षकाः ॥
Our kin, friends, well-wishers and warriors are waiting for you, like tillers for rain.
2.112.13
इदं राज्यं महाप्राज्ञ
स्थापय प्रतिपद्य हि ।
शक्तिमानसि काकुत्स्थ
लोकस्य परिपालने ॥
O Kākutstha, O supremely sagacious one! Accept the kingdom and establish it on a firm footing. You have the prowess to govern this world.
2.112.14
इत्युक्त्वा न्यपतद्भ्रातुः
पादयोर्भरतस्तदा ।
भृशं सम्प्रार्थयामास
राममेव प्रियंवदः ॥
Having spoken thus, Bharata fell at his brother's feet. The sweet-spoken one earnestly pleaded thus with Rāma.
2.112.15
तमङ्के भरतं कृत्वा
रामो वचनमब्रवीत् ।
श्यामं नलिनपत्राक्षं
मत्तहंसस्वरं स्वयम् ॥
The dark-hued, lotus-petal eyed Rāma, whose voice resembled that of the amorous swan took Bharata on his lap and said:
2.112.16
आगता त्वामियं बुद्धिः
स्वजा वैनयिकी च या ।
भृशमुत्सहसे तात
रक्षितुं पृथिवीमपि ॥
My child! By this very innate and nurtured virtue of yours, you are very much capable of protecting the earth!
2.112.17
अमात्यैश्च सुहृद्भिश्च
बुद्धिमद्भिश्च मन्त्रिभिः ।
सर्वकार्याणि सम्मन्त्र्य
सुमहन्त्यपि कारय ॥
Even though it is a monumental task, you shall get it done with the counsel of the officials, well-wishers and the wise ministers.
2.112.18
लक्ष्मीश्चन्द्रादपेयाद्वा
हिमवान्वा हिमं त्यजेत् ।
अतीयात्सागरो वेलां
न प्रतिज्ञामहं पितुः ॥
Moonlight may leave the moon. The Himalayas may abandon snow. The ocean may disregard its boundary. But I will not abjure the word given by my father.
2.112.19
कामाद्वा तात लोभाद्वा
मात्रातुभ्यमिदं कृतम् ।
न तन्मनसि कर्तव्यं
वर्तितव्यं च मातृवत् ॥
Your mother might have done this for you out of fancy or greed. You should not hold it against her. You still have to regard her as a mother.
2.112.20
एवं ब्रुवाणं भरतः
कौसल्यासुतमब्रवीत् ।
तेजसाऽऽदित्यसङ्काशं
प्रतिपच्चन्द्रदर्शनम् ॥
Having been told thus, Bharata responded to the son of Kousalyā, whose brilliance was like that of the sun and whose presence was like that of the moon on the day after new moon day.
2.112.21
आधिरोहाऽर्य पादाभ्यां
पादुके हेमभूषिते ।
एतेहि सर्वलोकस्य
योगक्षेमं विधास्यतः ॥
O noble one! Please place your feet in these gold covered sandals. And they will take care of the Yōga and Kshēma of the entire world.
Yōga in this context means the gain of something new. Kshēma means the management of what is already gained.
2.112.22
सोऽधिरुह्य नरव्याघ्रः
पादुके ह्यवरुह्य च ।
प्रायच्छत्सुमहातेजा
भरताय महात्मने ॥
And that tiger among men, that man of infinite splendor, put on the sandals, took them off, and gave them to Mahātma Bharata.
2.112.23
स पादुके सम्प्रणम्य
रामं वचनमब्रवीत् ॥
Bharata, then prostrated to the sandals and said to Rāma:
2.112.23
चतुर्दश हि वर्षाणि
जटाचीरधरो ह्याहम् ।
फलमूलाशनो वीर
भवेयं रघुनन्दन ।
तवाऽगमनमाकाङ्क्षान्
वसन्वै नगराद्बहिः ।
तव पादुकयोर्न्यस्त
राज्यतन्त्रः परन्तप ॥
O Veera, O slayer of enemies, O pleaser of Raghu lineage! I shall reside outside the city for fourteen years wearing matted locks and robes of bark, living on fruits and roots, placing the responsibility of ruling this kingdom on your sandals and looking forward to your arrival.
2.112.25
चतुर्दशे तु संपूर्णे
वर्षेऽहनि रघूत्तम ।
न द्रक्ष्यामि यदि त्वां तु
प्रवेक्ष्यामि हुताशनम् ॥
O foremost of Raghus, if I do not see you on the day after the completion of fourteen years, I shall throw myself into the fire.
2.112.26
तथेति च प्रतिज्ञाय
तं परिष्वज्य सादरम् ।
शत्रुघ्नं च परिष्वज्य
भरतं चेदमब्रवीत् ॥
Promising the same by saying 'be it so', and taking him into his embrace affectionately and also taking Ṡatrughna into his embrace, he said these words to Bharata:
2.112.27
मातरं रक्ष कैकेयीं
मा रोषं कुरु तां प्रति ।
मया च सीतया चैव
शप्तोऽसि रघुसत्तम ॥
O best of Raghus, swear on me and Seetā that you will take good care of your mother Kaikēyee, and will not be bitter with her.
2.112.28
इत्युक्त्वाऽश्रुपरीताक्षो
भ्रातरं विससर्ज ह ॥
Saying this and his eyes filled with tears, he bade farewell to his brother.
2.112.29
स पादुके ते भरतः प्रतापवान्
स्वलङ्कृते सम्परिपूज्य धर्मवित् ।
प्रदक्षिणं चैव चकार राघवम्
चकार ते चोत्तमनागमूर्धनि ॥
The valiant Bharata, who knew Dharma, received the decorated sandals with all deference. He circumambulated Rāghava reverently and then placed the sandals on the head of a premier elephant.
2.112.30
अथाऽनुपूर्व्यात्प्रतिनन्द्य तं जनं
गुरूंश्च मन्त्रिप्रकृतीस्तथाऽनुजौ ।
व्यसर्जयद्राघववंशवर्धनः
स्थिरः स्वधर्मे हिमवानिवाचलः ॥
Then the illustrious scion of the race of Raghu, unshakable like Himalaya, in his adherence to his own Dharma, bade farewell to all those people, the gurus, the Ministers, the representatives of the realm, and his brothers in proper order, with due expression of his affection and esteem.
2.112.31
तं मातरो बाष्पगृहीतकण्ठ्यो
दुःखेन नामन्त्रयितुं हि शेकुः ।
स त्वेव मात्रऽरभिवाद्य सर्वा-
रुदन्कुटीं स्वां प्रविवेश राघवः ॥
But his mothers, their throats choked with tears of grief, found it hard to take leave of him. He then prostrated to all of them, and entered his cottage, weeping.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे द्वादशोत्तरशततमस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the one hundred and twelfth Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
