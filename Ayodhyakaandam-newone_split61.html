Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 61
In the previous Sarga, Sumantra tries to console Kousalyā. But it proves to be of no use. In this short Sarga, Kousalyā laments for the state of her mighty son and her daughter-in-law. She thrashes Daṡaratha for hurting everyone including her, her son, daughter-in-law, the ministers, himself, the citizens, and the kingdom and the country. Hearing those terrible words of hers, Daṡaratha plunges into grief.
2.61.1
वनं गते धर्मपरे
रामे रमयतां वरे ।
कौसल्या रुदती स्वार्ता
भर्तारमिदमब्रवीत् ॥
With Rāma, the supreme adherent of Dharma and the best among those who make everyone cheerful, gone to the Vana, Kousalyā sobbing extremely pitifully, said these words to her husband:
2.61.2
यद्यपि त्रिषु लोकेषु
प्रथितं ते महद्यशः ।
सानुक्रोशो वदान्यश्च
प्रियवादी च राघवः ॥
All the three worlds may be hailing your great fame: "Rāghava (Daṡaratha) is compassionate, generous and sweet-spoken".
2.61.3
कथं नरवरश्रेष्ठ
पुत्रौ तौ सह सीतया ।
दुःखितौ सुखसंवृद्धौ
वने दुःखं सहिष्यतः ॥
But, how, O best of the best among men, your two sons, along with Seetā, who were raised in comfort, will suffer and endure the hardship of Vana?
2.61.4
सा नूनं तरुणी श्यामा
सुकुमारी सुखोचिता ।
कथमुष्णं च शीतं च
मैथिली प्रसहिष्यते ॥
How could the princess of Mithilā, in the prime of her youth, so delicate and worthy of all comfort, endure the heat and cold (of Vana)?
2.61.5
भुक्त्वाऽशनं विशालाक्षी
सूपदं शान्वितं शुभम् ।
वन्यं नैवारमाहारम्
कथं सीतोपभोक्ष्यते ॥
How could Seetā of wide eyes, used to eating well sauced and seasoned food all her life, subsist on the wild rice of the Vana!
2.61.6
गीतवादित्रनिर्घोषम्
श्रुत्वा शुभमनिन्दिता ।
कथं क्रव्यादसिंहानाम्
शब्दं श्रोष्यत्यशोभनम् ॥
How could the blameless one, accustomed to the delightful sounds of music, sung and played, bear the harsh din of lions and other savage beasts?
2.61.7
महेन्द्रध्वजसङ्काशः
क्व नु शेते महाभुजः ।
भुजं परिघसङ्काशम्
उपधाय महाबलः ॥
Where could he of mighty shoulders and great strength, one (who stands tall) like the flagstaff of Mahēndra be sleeping, using his iron-club-like (hard) arm as his pillow!
2.61.8
पद्मवर्णं सुकेशान्तम्
पद्मनिश्श्वासमुत्तमम् ।
कदा द्रक्ष्यामि रामस्य
वदनं पुष्करेक्षणम् ॥
Ah! When would I see Rāma's face, radiant like a lotus, with its lotus eyes, its crown of lovely tresses, and breath sweet as the scent of lotus?
This Ṡlōka beautifully captures what any mother would feel about her son!
2.61.9
वज्रसारमयं नूनम्
हृदयं मे न संशयः ।
अपश्यन्त्या न तं यद्वै
फलतीदं सहस्रधा ॥
My heart must be made of the core of a diamond, without doubt. For it is not breaking apart into thousands of pieces, even a Rāma is not to be seen.
2.61.10
यत्त्वयाऽकरुणं कर्म
व्यपोह्य मम बान्धवाः ।
निरस्ताः परिधावन्ति
सुखार्हाः कृपणा वने ॥
Instead of enjoying your grace, all my people who deserve to enjoy comforts, are now distanced, and are roaming in the Vana in a pathetic manner.
2.61.11
यदि पञ्चदशे वर्षे
राघवः पुनरेष्यति ।
जह्याद्राज्यं च कोशं च
भरतो नोपलक्ष्यते ॥
There is no knowing whether Bharata would give up the throne and the treasury, when Rāma returns in the fifteenth year.
2.61.12
भोजयन्ति किल श्राद्धे
केचित्स्वानेव बान्धवान् ।
ततः पश्चात्समीक्षन्ते
कृतकार्या द्विजर्षभान् ॥
Some people, on the occasion of annual ceremonial offerings to the deceased, feed their own relations first and only after that worry about the esteemed Brāhmaṇas.
2.61.13
तत्र ये गुणवन्तश्च
विद्वांसश्च द्विजातयः ।
न पश्चात्तेऽभिमन्यन्ते
सुधामपि सुरोपमाः ॥
But learned Brāhmaṇas of illustrious qualities who are comparable to Dēvas, would not accept even Amṛta, if it was served to others before them.
2.61.14
ब्राह्मणेष्वपि तृप्तेषु
पश्चाद्भोक्तुं द्विजर्षभाः ।
नाभ्युपैतुमलं प्राज्ञाः
शृङ्गच्छेदमिवर्षभाः ॥
Even if those that were served ahead of them were Brāhmaṇas, the best of the Brāhmaṇas, would not agree to eat later, any more than bulls will tolerate attempts to cut their horns off.
2.61.15
एवं कनीयसा भ्रात्रा
भुक्तं राज्यं विशाम्पते ।
भ्राता ज्येष्ठो वरिष्ठश्च
किमर्थं नावमंस्यते ॥
O lord of the world, why would not an elder and most distinguished brother, likewise look with disdain upon a kingdom that has been enjoyed by a younger brother?
2.61.16
न परेणाऽहृतं भक्ष्यम्
व्याघ्रः खादितुमिच्छति ।
एवमेतन्नरव्याघ्रः
परलीढं न मन्यते ॥
A tiger would never eat the food left over by other animals. Similarly, a tiger among men will never accept anything that has already been enjoyed by others.
2.61.17
हविराज्यं पुरोडाशाः
कुशा यूपाश्च खादिराः ।
नैतानि यातयामानि
कुर्वन्ति पुनरध्वरे ॥
Oblations of cooked food, clarified butter, ground flour, Kuṡa grass and stakes made of ebony, which have been once used, are not used again in Yajñas.
2.61.18
तथा ह्यात्तमिदं राज्यम्
हृतसारां सुरामिव ।
नाभिमन्तुमलं रामो
नष्टसोममिवाध्वरम् ॥
Likewise, Rāma will not desire this kingdom which has been enjoyed by another, any more than one would like to drink wine that has lost its savor or partake in a Yajña after the Sōma has already been served.
2.61.19
नैवं विधमसत्कारम्
राघवो मर्षयिष्यति ।
बलवानिव शार्दूलो
वालधेरभिमर्शनम् ॥
Rāghava will not tolerate this indignity just as a mighty tiger cannot tolerate somebody touching its tail.
2.61.20
नैतस्य सहिता लोका
भयं कुर्युर्महामृथे ।
अधर्मंत्विह धर्मात्मा
लोकं धर्मेण योजयेत् ॥
Not even all the worlds arrayed against him in a fierce battle can cause fear in him. He is a Dharmātma, capable of establishing Dharma in this world, if it deviates from it.
2.61.21
नन्वसौ काञ्चनैर्बाणैः
महावीर्यो महाभुजः ।
युगान्त इव भूतानि
सागरानपि निर्दहेत् ॥
He, of the mighty arms and immense strength, Can, with his gold-tipped arrows, burn up, like the Fire of the Dissolution, all the beings and the ocean itself.
2.61.22
स तादृशस्सिंहबलो
वृषभाक्षो नरर्षभः ।
स्वयमेव हतः पित्रा
जलजेनात्मजो यथा ॥
Like a fish killing its own offspring, you have undone such a bull among men, whose strength rivals that of a lion and whose eyes are (fierce) like that of a bull.
2.61.23
द्विजातिचरितो धर्मः
शास्त्रदृष्टस्सनातनः ।
यदि ते धर्मनिरते
त्वया पुत्रे विवासिते ॥
If the time-honored Dharma that is enunciated in the scriptures and adhered to by the twice-born was respected by you, you should not have exiled your son who is so devoted to Dharma.
The word twice-born is normally used to refer to Brāhmaṇas. But it also applies to Kshatriyas and Vaiṡyas.
'Twice-born' obviously indicates second birth. The second birth here refers to the taking of an oath, asserting that one would lead his life in accordance with the appropriate Dharma that is right for the assumed role, as opposed to leading one's life based simply on instincts.
Unlike 'roles' in the modern times, which start at 8am and end at 5pm, roles in the conventional Indian society are for 24 hours. A Kshatriya, for example, is a Kshatriya even while sleeping.
The word Dharma refers to the explicit rules and regulations as well as implicit customs and conventions related to a role. The famous Ṡlōka स्वधर्मे निधनं श्रेयः परधर्मो भयावहः (Bhagavadgeeta 3.5) refers to this Dharma.
2.61.24
गतिरेका पतिर्नार्या
द्वितीया गतिरात्मजः ।
तृतीया ज्ञातयो राजन्
चतुर्थी नेह विद्यते ॥
O King! For a woman, the first refuge is her husband, the second is her son and the third, her relations. No fourth one is known.
2.61.25
तत्र त्वं चैव मे नास्ति
रामश्च वनमाश्रितः ।
न वनं गन्तुमिच्छामि
सर्वथा निहता त्वया ॥
Of these, you are lost to me, and Rāma has gone to the Vana. As I do not wish to go to the Vana, I am hurt by you in every manner (of having to put up with the third option).
2.61.26
हतं त्वया राज्यमिदं सराष्ट्रं
हतस्तथाऽत्मा सह मन्त्रिभिश्च ।
हता सपुत्राऽस्मि हताश्च पौराः
सुतश्च भार्या च तव प्रहृष्टौ ॥
This kingdom including all the country, is hurt by you. All the ministers and yourself are hurt. I am hurt, along with my son. The citizens are hurt. Only your wife (Kaikēyee) and son (Bharata) are happy.
2.61.27
इमां गिरं दारुणशब्द संश्रितां
निशम्य राजाऽपि मुमोह दुःखितः ।
तत स्स शोकं प्रविवेश पार्थिव-
स्स्वदुष्कृतं चापि पुनस्तदा स्मरन् ॥
On hearing those terrible words, the King's senses were numbed by sadness. Then, thinking of the damage he himself had caused, that ruler of the earth plunged into grief.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे एकषष्टितमस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the sixty first Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
