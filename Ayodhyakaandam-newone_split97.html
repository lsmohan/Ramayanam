Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 97
In this Sarga, Rāma pacifies Lakshmaṇa and tells him that there is no reason for him to suspect Bharata. He further says, having sworn to uphold the word given by his father, how does it make sense for him to kill Bharata and take the kingdom back?
Rāma further asserts that he lives, and would want a kingdom only for the betterment of his brothers. Even then, he wouldn't want a kingdom that is gained by unrighteous means.
He asserts that Bharata must have come to mend ways for what had happened and to give the kingdom back. He also says that anything Lakshmaṇa says against Bharata, would be considered being said against himself.
Lakshmaṇa feels ashamed and says that it could be Daṡaratha who is coming. Rāma picks on that positive note and says that it must be true that Daṡaratha might have come to take them, or at least Seetā, back.
Bharata meanwhile, orders the army to disperse and scatter instead of crowding at one place.
2.97.1
सुसंरब्धं तु सौमित्रिं
लक्ष्मणं क्रोधमूर्छितम् ।
रामस्तु परिसान्त्व्याथ
वचनं चेदमब्रवीत् ॥
When Lakshmaṇa burst out thus overpowered by rage, Rāma calmed him down and said these words:
2.97.2
किमत्र धनुषा कार्यम्
असिना वा सचर्मणा ।
महेष्वासे महाप्राज्ञे
भरते स्वयमागते ॥
When Bharata, the great bowman of immense wisdom, comes in person, what use is bow or sword and the shield?
2.97.3
पितुस्सत्यं प्रतिश्रुत्य
हत्वा भरतमागतम् ।
किं करिष्यामि राज्येन
सापवादेन लक्ष्मण ॥
O Lakshmaṇa, having sworn to make father's word truthful, and then having killed Bharata who comes here, what would I do with the kingdom that is earned slanderously!
2.97.4
यद्ध्रव्यं बान्धवानां वा
मित्राणां वा क्षये भवेत् ।
नाहं तत्प्रतिगृह्णीयां
भक्षान्विषकृतानिव ॥
I will never take riches that result from the adversity of relations and friends, any more than I would take poisoned food.
2.97.5
धर्ममर्थं च कामं च
पृथिवीं चापि लक्ष्मण ।
इच्छामि भवतामर्थे
एतत् प्रतिशृणोमि ते ॥
I seek either Dharma, or Artha or Kāma, or even this earth, O Lakshmaṇa, only for the sake of all of you, I swear.
2.97.6
भ्रात्रूणां संग्रहार्थं च
सुखार्थं चापि लक्ष्मण ।
राज्यमप्यहमिच्छामि
सत्येनाऽयुधमालभे ॥
Even a kingdom, O Lakshmaṇa, I seek only for the happiness of my brothers and to be with them, I swear, touching my weapons.
Rāma tries to clarify his priorities and intentions, like any other leader would, at times of crisis.
In Ṡlōka 6, he makes it clear that he would want a kingdom only for the sake of his brothers.
In Ṡlōka 7 he says, but again, even for the sake of brothers, he would not seek a kingdom by unjust means.
2.97.7
नेयं मम मही सौम्य
दुर्लभा सागराम्बरा ।
न हीच्छेयमधर्मेण
शक्रत्वमपि लक्ष्मण ॥
O gentle one! This sea-clad earth is not at all unattainable for me. But I would not like even the position of Ṡakra at the cost of Dharma.
2.97.8
यद्विना भरतं त्वां च
शत्रुघ्नं चापि मानद ।
भवेन्मम सुखं किञ्चित्
भस्म तत्कुरुतां शिखी ॥
O you, the protector of my dignity! Let fire reduce any happiness that I find without you, Bharata and Ṡatrughna, to ashes.
2.97.9
मन्येऽहमागतोऽयोध्यां
भरतो भ्रातृवत्सलः ।
मम प्राणात्प्रियतरः
कुलधर्ममनुस्मरन् ।
श्रुत्वा प्रव्राजितं मां हि
जटावल्कलधारिणम् ।
जानक्यासहितं वीर
त्वया च पुरुषर्षभ ।
स्नेहेनाऽक्रान्तहृदयः
शोकेनाकुलितेन्द्रियः ।
द्रष्टुमभ्यागतो ह्येष
भरतो नान्यथाऽगतः ॥
I think that Bharata, O Veera, who loves his brothers and who is dearer to me than my own life, must have come to Ayōdhyā and hearing that I had gone into exile with matted locks, clad in tree bark, along with daughter of Janaka and you, has come to visit us here, mindful of the family traditions, his heart flooded by love and his mind disoriented with grief. I am sure that he has come for no other purpose.
2.97.12
अम्बां च कैकयीं रुष्य
परुषं चाप्रियं वदन् ।
प्रसाद्य पितरं श्रीमान्
राज्यं मे दातुमागतः ॥
Having in anger spoken harsh and unkind words to his mother Kaikēyee, and having pleaded with father, he, of rich bearing, must have come to give me the kingdom.
Rāma, obviously, was not aware and was not even suspecting the demise of Daṡaratha.
2.97.13
प्राप्तकालं यदेषोऽस्मान्
भरतो द्रष्टुमिच्छति ।
अस्मासु मनसाऽप्येष
नाप्रियं किञ्चिदाचरेत् ॥
This is about the right time for Bharata to wish to see me. Not even a thought of doing anything unpleasant to us would cross his mind.
2.97.14
विप्रियं कृतपूर्वं ते
भरतेन कदा नु किम् ।
ईदृशं वा भयं तेऽद्य
भरतं योऽत्र शङ्कसे ॥
Did Bharata ever do anything unpleasant to you, that you should suspect him to be a source of fear now?
2.97.15
न हि ते निष्ठुरं वाच्यो
भरतो नाप्रियं वचः ।
अहं ह्यप्रियमुक्तस्स्यां
भरतस्याप्रिये कृते ॥
You shall not say aught in malice or in unkindness to Bharata; any unkindness to Bharata would be unkindness to me.
2.97.16
कथं नु पुत्राः पितरं
हन्युः कस्यां चिदापदि ।
भ्राता वा भ्रातरं हन्यात्
सौमित्रे प्राणमात्मनः ॥
How could sons bring themselves to kill their father, whatever be the danger that threatens, or brother to kill a brother, who is his own other life, O son of Sumitrā?
2.97.17
यदि राज्यस्य हेतोस्त्वम्
इमां वाचं प्रभाषसे ।
वक्ष्यामि भरतं दृष्ट्वा
राज्यमस्मै प्रदीयताम् ॥
If it is for the sake of kingdom that you have said all this, I shall, on seeing Bharata, tell him, 'you shall give the kingdom to him.'
2.97.18
उच्यमानोऽपि भरतो
मया लक्ष्मण तद्वचः ।
राज्यमस्मै प्रयच्छेति
बाढमित्येव वक्ष्यति ॥
The moment I tell Bharata, O Lakshmaṇa, those words of 'Give him the kingdom', he will only say 'Yes'!
2.97.19
तथोक्तो धर्मशीलेन
भ्रात्रा तस्य हिते रतः ।
लक्ष्मणः प्रविवेशेव
स्वानि गात्राणि लज्जया ॥
Told thus by his brother, the essence of whose character was Dharma, Lakshmaṇa, who was devoted to him, felt as if every limb of his body was filled with shame.
2.97.20
तद्वाक्यं लक्ष्मणश्श्रुत्वा
व्रीलितः प्रत्युवाच ह ।
त्वां मन्ये द्रष्टुमायातः
पिता दशरथस्स्वयम् ॥
Hearing those words, he replied, feeling ashamed: 'I consider, it is father Daṡaratha who has come in person to see you'.
2.97.21
व्रीलितं लक्ष्मणं दृष्ट्वा
राघवः प्रत्युवाच ह ।
एष मन्ये महाबाहुः
इहास्मान्द्रष्टुमागतः ॥
Seeing that Lakshmaṇa felt ashamed, Rāghava responded saying: 'Yes, I too think that he of mighty arm, has himself came to see us'.
2.97.22
अथवा नौ ध्रुवं मन्ये
मन्यमानस्सुखोचितौ ।
वनवासमनुध्याय
गृहाय प्रतिनेष्यति ॥
Or certainly, I think, to take us back home, considering the comforts we are used to and (the lack of them in) this stay in the Vana.
2.97.23
इमां वाप्येष वैदेहीम्
अत्यन्तसुखसेविनीम् ।
पिता मे राघवश्श्रीमान्
वनादादाय यास्यति ॥
Or, may be, my gracious father, the scion of the Raghus, will take back from this Vana, the princess of Vidēha who was raised in great comfort and luxury.
2.97.24
एतौ तौ सम्प्रकाशेते
गोत्रवन्तौ मनोरमौ ।
वायुवेगसमौ वीर
जवनौ तुरगोत्तमौ ॥
O Veera! These two beautiful pedigree horses of distinction, that vie with the wind in speed, stand out.
2.97.25
स एष सुमहाकायः
कम्पते वाहिनीमुखे ।
नागश्शत्रुञ्जयो नाम
वृद्धस्तातस्य धीमतः ॥
And there comes, swaying at the head of the army, the giant old tusker Ṡatruñjaya, of our sagacious father.
2.97.26
न तु पश्यामि तच्छत्र
पाण्डुरं लोकसत्कृतम् ।
पितुर्दिव्यं महाबाहो
संशयो भवतीह मे ॥
But I do not see the highly regarded white splendid parasol of father, O you of mighty arm, which raises suspicion in me.
2.97.27
वृक्षाग्रादवरोह त्वं
कुरु लक्ष्मण मद्वचः ।
इतीव रामो धर्मात्मा
सौमित्रिं तमुवाच ह ॥
'Please come down from the tree, O Lakshmaṇa, and do as I tell you.' said Rāma, the Dharmātma, to the son of Sumitrā.
2.97.28
अवतीर्य तु सालाग्रात्
तस्मात्ससमितिञ्जयः ।
लक्ष्मणः प्राञ्जलिर्भूत्वा
तस्थौ रामस्य पार्श्वतः ॥
Climbing down from the Sāla tree, Lakshmaṇa, the conqueror of armies waited at the side of Rāma with palms joined.
2.97.29
भरतेनापि सन्दिष्टा
सम्मर्दो न भवेदिति ।
समन्तात्तस्य शैलस्य
सेना वासमकल्पयत् ॥
Ordered by Bharata not to gather in a crowd, the army camped, scattered around the mountain.
2.97.30
अध्यर्धमिक्ष्वाकुचमूः
योजनं पर्वतस्य सा ।
पार्श्वेन्यविशदावृत्य
गजवाजिरथाकुला ॥
That army of the Ikshwākus, packed with elephants, horses and chariots, settled down on the flanks of the mountain, spreading out to one and half Yōjanas around it.
2.97.31
सा चित्रकूटे भरतेन सेना
धर्मं पुरस्कृत्य विधूय दर्पम् ।
प्रसादनार्थं रघुनन्दनस्य
विराजते नीतिमता प्रणीता ॥
The army brought by Bharata, knower of protocol and strategy, in accordance with Dharma, to earn the grace of Rāma, made a splendid presence, without any ostentation, at that Citrakūṭa.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे सप्तनवतितमस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the ninety seventh Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
