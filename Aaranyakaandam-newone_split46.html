Aranya Kaanda - Sarga 46
In this Sarga, Lakshmaṇa leaves to find Rāma. Immediately, without losing any time, Rāvaṇa approaches Seetā in the form of a Parivrājaka reciting Vedas. He praises Seetā and her beauty. He says that he has never seen a beautiful woman like her among humans, Gandharvas, Dēvas or Kinnaras.
He asks why she is alone in the forest, which is frequented by Rākshasas and full of wild and terrifying beasts. He says that she deserves to live in a palace in a city, enjoying good food and garlands, with a good husband.
Taking him to be none other than a Brāhmaṇa, Seetā honors him in a customary manner and serves him food. Then she looks around the greenery of the Vana, but does not find Rāma or Lakshmaṇa coming back.
3.46.1
तया परुषमुक्तस्तु
कुपितो राघवानुजः ।
स विकाङ्क्षन्भृशं रामम्
प्रतस्थे नचिरादिव ॥
Rāghava’s younger brother, angered by her harsh words, started instantly in earnest (to meet Rāma).
3.46.2
तदासाद्य दशग्रीवः
क्षिप्रमन्तरमास्थितः ।
अभिचक्राम वैदेहीम्
परिव्राजकरूपधृत् ॥
Daṡagreeva, quickly seizing the opportunity for which he had been waiting, went to Vaidēhee, disguised as a Parivrājaka.
Parivrājaka is the same as Samnyāsi, one who has renounced the world.
3.46.3
श्लक्ष्णकाषायसंवीतः
शिखी छत्री उपानही ।
वामे चांसेऽवसज्याथ
शुभे यष्टिकमण्डलू ।
परिव्राजकरूपेण
वैदेहीमन्वपद्यत ॥
Wrapped in soft saffron robes, wearing a top-knot, with sandals on his feet, carrying an umbrella and holding Daṇḍa and Kamaṇḍala in his left hand he approached Vaidēhee in the guise of a Parivrājaka.
3.46.4
तामाससादातिबलो
भ्रातृभ्यां रहितां वने ।
रहितां चन्द्रसूर्याभ्याम्
सन्ध्यामिव महत्तमः ॥
He of immense strength approached her in the Vana in the absence of the brothers like the great darkness descending at dusk when there is no sun or moon.
3.46.5
तामपश्यत्ततो बालाम्
रामपत्नीं यशस्विनीम् ।
रोहिणीं शशिना हीनाम्
ग्रहवद्भृशदारुणः ॥
He, the terribly cruel one, looked at the young and renowned wife of Rāma, like a baleful planet looking at Rōhiṇee when the moon is not present.
3.46.6
तमुग्रतेजःकर्माणम्
जनस्थानरूहा द्रुमाः ।
समीक्ष्य सम्प्रकम्पन्ते
न प्रवाति च मारुतः ॥
Seeing him, of terrible strength and deeds, the trees of Janasthāna shivered and the wind froze.
3.46.7
शीघ्रस्रोताश्च तं दृष्ट्वा
वीक्षन्तं रक्तलोचनम् ।
स्तिमितं गन्तुमारेभे
भयाद्गोदावरी नदी ॥
Upon seeing him cast a glance with his blood-red eyes, the swift-flowing Gōdāvaree slowed down with fear.
3.46.8
रामस्य त्वन्तरप्रेप्सुः
दशग्रीवस्तदन्तरे ।
उपतस्थे च वैदेहीम्
भिक्षुरूपेण रावणः ॥
Rāvaṇa of ten necks, who had been waiting for the absence of Rāma, took that opportunity and approached Vaidēhee, disguised as a mendicant.
3.46.9
अभव्यो भव्यरूपेण
भर्तारमनुशोचतीम् ।
अभ्यवर्तत वैदेहीम्
चित्रामिव शनैश्चरः ॥
The sinner garbed as a saint approached Vaidēhee who was worried about her husband, like planet Saturn approaching star Citra.
The repetition in Ṡlōkas 9 through 12 is poetic in nature. As we have noted several times earlier, Rāmāyaṇa should be sung and heard, rather than read. Repetition enhances the beauty of the song, as we well know.
3.46.10
स पापो भव्यरूपेण
तृणैः कूप इवावृतः ।
अतिष्ठत्प्रेक्ष्य वैदेहीम्
रामपत्नीं यशस्विनीम् ॥
That sinner in the form of a saint, like a well covered with grass stood there looking at Vaidēhee, the wife of Rāma, of great repute.
3.46.11
शुभां रुचिरदन्तोष्ठीम्
पूर्णचन्द्रनिभाननाम् ।
आसीनां पर्णशालायाम्
बाष्पशोकाभिपीडिताम् ।
स तां पद्मपलाशाक्षीम्
पीतकौशेयवासिनीम् ।
अभ्यगच्छत वैदेहीम्
दुष्टचेता निशाचरः ॥
The Night-rover with evil intentions approached the good lady Vaidēhee, of lovely teeth and lips, eyes resembling lotus petals, face like the full moon, clad in a yellow silk garment, who sat by the leafy cottage in tears, crushed by grief.
3.46.13
स मन्मथशराविष्टो
ब्रह्मघोषमुदीरयन् ।
अब्रवीत्प्रश्रितं वाक्यम्
रहिते राक्षसाधिपः ॥
The lord of Rākshasas who was struck by the darts of Manmatha, said these words in a pleasing manner, while chanting Vedas aloud, with no one else present:
3.46.14
तामुत्तमां स्त्रियं लोके
पद्महीनामिव श्रियम् ।
विभ्राजमानां वपुषा
रावणः प्रशशंस ह ॥
Rāvaṇa then praised her, who was the paragon of womankind, whose body was dazzling and who looked like goddess Lakshmee except for the lotus.
3.46.15
का त्वं काञ्चनवर्णाभे
पीतकौशेयवासिनि ।
कमलानां शुभां मालाम्
पद्मिनीव हि बिभ्रती ॥
Who are you, of golden complexion, clad in yellow silks and wearing the beautiful garland of lotus flowers, looking like the lotus pool itself?
3.46.16
ह्रीः कीर्तिः श्रीश्शुभा लक्ष्मीः
अप्सरा वा शुभानने ।
भूतिर्वा त्वं वरारोहे
रतिर्वा स्वैरचारिणी ॥
O you of beautiful countenance and excellent Kaṭee! Are you by any chance the goddess of modesty or the goddess of fame or the blessing goddess of prosperity or the goddess of wealth, or a nymph of Heaven, or Ratee on her carefree strolls!
3.46.17
समाश्शिखरिणस्स्निग्धाः
पाण्डुरा दशनास्तव ।
विशाले विमले नेत्रे
रक्तान्ते कृष्णतारके ॥
Your teeth are even, rounded, shiny and white. your eyes are crystal clear and wide, with black pupils and corners showing a red tinge.
3.46.18
विशालं जघनं पीनम्
ऊरू करिकरोपमौ ।
एतावुपचितौ वृत्तौ
संहतौ सम्प्रवल्गितौ ।
पीनोन्नतमुखौ कान्तौ
स्निग्धौ तालफलोपमौ ।
मणिप्रवेकाभरणौ
रुचिरौ ते पयोधरौ ॥
Your hips are wide and plump. Your thighs resemble the trunk of the elephant. Your breasts, well developed, high and round, snuggled up to each other and swaying, with the nipples firm and erect, looking like the fruit of the date palm and covered with ornaments set with rare gems, are beautiful, shining and attractive.
From this Ṡlōka, it does seem that women in those days used to dress topless, except for ornaments.
Some historians say that tailoring is not native to India and that is why there is no tailor caste in India. Even if one exists in a few places here and there, it is of recent origin.
Rather, India excelled in the art of wrapping long pieces of unstitched cloth and hence developed the rich variety of ways of wearing Sarees and Dhotees.
Sarees stitched with border or the ‘half-and-half Sarees’ which are made by stitching together two different pieces of cloth as one Saree, are not worn, even today, at the time of sacred ceremonies, especially in southern India.
3.46.20
चारुस्मिते चारुदति
चारुनेत्रे विलासिनि ।
मनो हरसि मे कान्ते
नदी कूलमिवाम्भसा ।
करान्तमितमध्यासि
सुकेशी संहतस्तनी ॥
O gorgeous one! You have a lovely smile, lovely teeth and lovely eyes. O winsome one, you have stolen my heart like a river ravishes its banks with its waters. O you of lovely hair! Your breasts snuggle up to each other and your waist can be measured with a fist!
3.46.22
नैव देवी न गन्धर्वी
न यक्षी न च किन्नरी ।
नैवंरूपा मया नारी
दृष्टपूर्वा महीतले ॥
I have seen neither a woman, nor a goddess, nor a Gandharvee, nor a Yakshee, nor a Kinnaree, who has figure like yours, on the face of the earth.
3.46.23
रूपमग्र्यं च लोकेषु
सौकुमार्यं वयश्चते ।
इह वासश्च कान्तारे
चित्तमुन्मादयन्ति मे ॥
With your beauty, the rarest in the world, your delicacy and your youthfulness, it worries me that you should live in a forest (like this).
3.46.24
सा प्रतिक्राम भद्रं ते
नैवं वस्तुमिहार्हसि ।
राक्षसानामयं वासो
घोराणां कामरूपिणाम् ॥
Given all of that, you should not live like this here, where the dreaded Rākshasas who can take any form at will, live. You should get away and may all bode well for you.
3.46.25
प्रासादाग्राणि रम्याणि
नगरोपवनानि च ।
सम्पन्नानि सुगन्धीनि
युक्तान्याचरितुं त्वया ॥
The places that are right for you are the top stories of tall mansions and the beautiful, opulent and fragrant parks of the cities.
3.46.26
वरं माल्यं वरं भोज्यम्
वरं वस्त्रं च शोभने ।
भर्तारं च वरं मन्ये
त्वद्युक्तमसितेक्षणे ॥
You deserve the best of garlands, best of foods and best of clothes, and the best of husbands too, O gorgeous one, with coal-black eyes!
3.46.27
का त्वं भवसि रुद्राणाम्
मरुतां वा वरानने ।
वसूनां वा वरारोहे
देवता प्रतिभासि मे ॥
O you of best countenance and best Kaṭee! Who are you? Whom do you belong to - Maruts, Vasus, or Rudras? You look like a Dēvata to me!
3.46.28
नेह गच्छन्ति गन्धर्वा
न देवा न च किन्नराः ।
राक्षसानामयं वासः
कथं नु त्वमिहागता ॥
But the Gandharvas do not come here, neither do the Dēvas, nor the Kinnaras. This is the abode of the Rākshasas; how then did you come to be here?
3.46.29
इह शाखामृगास्सिंहा
द्वीपिव्याघ्रमृगास्तथा ।
ऋक्षास्तरक्षवः कङ्काः
कथं तेभ्यो न बिभ्यसि ॥
Monkeys, lions, leopards, panthers, bears, tigers and vultures abound here; how is it that you are not afraid of them?
3.46.30
मदान्वितानां घोराणाम्
कुञ्जराणां तरस्विनाम् ।
कथमेका महारण्ये
न बिभेषि वरानने ॥
O you of best countenance! How come you are not afraid, being alone in this great forest, of the swift, terrible and giant elephants that are in rut!
3.46.31
कासि कस्य कुतश्चित्त्वम्
किं निमित्तं च दण्डकान् ।
एका चरसि कल्याणि
घोरान्राक्षससेवितान् ॥
Who are you, to whom do you belong, and where do you come from? O auspicious one! Why are you here alone, in the dreaded Daṇḍaka that is frequented by the Rākshasas?
3.46.32
इति प्रशस्ता वैदेही
रावणेन दुरात्मना ।
द्विजातिवेषेण हि तम्
दृष्ट्वा रावणमागतम् ।
सर्वैरतिथिसत्कारैः
पूजयामास मैथिली ॥
Thus praised by the evil-minded Rāvaṇa, Vaidēhee, the princess of Mithilā, seeing only the Brāhmaṇa form in which he had came, attended on him with all honors due to a guest.
3.46.33
उपनीयासनं पूर्वम्
पाद्येनाभिनिमन्त्र्य च ।
अब्रवीत्सिद्धमित्येव
तदा तं सौम्यदर्शनम् ॥
After offering him a seat, and sprinkling water on his feet, she said to him, who looked so gentle, that it (food) was ready.
3.46.34
द्विजातिवेषेण समीक्ष्य मैथिली
समागतं पात्रकुसुम्भधारिणम् ।
अशक्यमुद्वेष्टुमपायदर्शनम्
न्यमन्त्रयद्ब्राह्मणवत्तदाङ्गना ॥
Maithili, seeing him come in the form of a Brāhmaṇa complete with a (begging) bowl and robes of ochre, finding no reason to turn him down, offered hospitality to the one with evil intentions.
3.46.35
इयं बृसी ब्राह्मण काममास्यताम्
इदं च पाद्यं प्रतिगृह्यतामिति ।
इदं च सिद्धं वनजातमुत्तमं
त्वदर्थमव्यग्रमिहोपभुज्यताम् ॥
O Brāhmaṇa! Here is your seat and may you be seated comfortably. Here is water for you to wash your feet and may you accept it. Here is the best food made from the products of Vana. May you enjoy it in a relaxed manner!
3.46.36
निमन्त्र्यमाणः प्रतिपूर्णभाषिणीं
नरेन्द्रपत्नीं प्रसमीक्ष्य मैथिलीम् ।
प्रसह्य तस्याहरणे धृतं मनः
समर्पयत्स्वात्मवधाय रावणः ॥
Taking a good look at Maithili, the wife of the lord of the people, who was talking with all her heart while serving, Rāvaṇa, sealing his own doom, set his mind on abducting her forcibly.
3.46.37
ततः सुवेषं मृगयागतं पतिं
प्रतीक्षमाणा सहलक्ष्मणं तदा ।
विवीक्षमाणा हरितं ददर्श तत्
महद्वनं नैव तु रामलक्ष्णौ ॥
Then, waiting for her husband of charming form who went hunting, to return along with Lakshmaṇa, she scanned the greenery of that great Vana, but did not see Rāma or Lakshmaṇa.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अरण्यकाण्डे षट्चत्वारिंशस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the forty sixth Sarga in Araṇya Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
