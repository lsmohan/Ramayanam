Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 119
In this final Sarga of Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa, Anasūyā asks Seetā to put on the clothes, ornaments and garlands she gave. Seetā obliges her and offers Praṇāma to her. Rāma and Lakshmaṇa are happy seeing her thus honored by Anasūyā.
Rāma stays for the night there and then proceeds onwards, along with Seetā and Lakshmaṇa, after receiving blessings and words of caution from the Ṛshis.
2.119.1
अनसूया तु धर्मज्ञा
श्रुत्वा तां महतीं कथाम् ।
पर्यष्वजत बाहुभ्यां
शिरस्याघ्राय मैथिलीम् ॥
Having heard the great tale, Anasūyā, the Dharmajña, embraced the princess of Mithilā with her arms and smelled her head.
2.119.2
व्यक्ताक्षरपदं चित्रं
भाषितं मधुरं त्वया ।
यथा स्वयंवरं वृत्तं
तत्सर्वं हि श्रुतं मया ।
रमेऽहं कथया ते तु
दृढं मधुरभाषिणि ॥
I am extremely pleased hearing this wonderful story in all detail, including the Swayamvara, narrated by you in sweet and animated voice with every word and syllable as clear as it could be.
2.119.3
रविरस्तङ्गतश्श्रीमान्
उपोह्य रजनीं शिवाम् ।
दिवसं प्रतिकीर्णानाम्
आहारार्थं पतत्रिणाम् ।
सन्ध्याकाले निलीनानां
निद्रार्थं श्रूयते ध्वनिः ॥
The glorious sun has set now, having ushered in the auspicious night. The chattering of birds which have roamed far and wide all day long seeking their food, is heard as they subside into slumber.
2.119.5
एते चाप्यभिषेकार्द्रा
मुनयः कलशोद्यताः ।
सहिता उपवर्तन्ते
सलिलाप्लुतवल्कलाः ॥
The Munis too are returning, in groups, after taking bath (in the river) with wet bodies, holding their Kalaṡas up, their robes of bark dripping.
2.119.6
ऋषीणामग्निहोत्रेषु
हुतेषु विधिपूर्वकम् ।
कपोताङ्गारुणो धूमो
दृश्यते पवनोद्धतः ॥
Smoke, in the color of pigeon feathers, wafted by the wind can be seen, rising from the sacred fires as Ṛshis offer oblations in it.
2.119.7
अल्पपर्णा हि तरवो
घनीभूतास्समन्ततः ।
विप्रकृष्टेन्द्रिये देशे
न प्रकाशन्ति वै दिशः ॥
Even trees with scanty leaves feel dense all around fooling the senses, as the four quarters have lost their brightness (with the sun setting).
2.119.8
रजनीचरसत्त्वानि
प्रचरन्ति समन्ततः ।
तपोवनमृगा ह्येते
वेदितीर्थेषु शेरते ॥
Nocturnal animals are on the prowl everywhere. The animals of the Tapōvana are resting by the Vēdikas and waterfronts.
2.119.9
सम्प्रवृत्तानिशा सीते
नक्षत्रसमलङ्कृता ।
ज्योत्स्नाप्रावरणश्चन्द्रो
दृश्यतेऽभ्युदितोऽम्बरे ॥
O Seetā, the night has descended now adorned with the constellations of stars. Moon, with his mantle of moonlight, is seen to rise in the sky.
2.119.10
गम्यतामनुजानामि
रामस्यानुचरी भव ।
कथायन्त्या हि मधुरं
त्वयाऽहं परितोषिता ॥
I shall let you go now. Please join Rāma. I am very happy listening to the sweet stories you have recounted.
2.119.11
अलङ्कुरु च तावत्त्वं
प्रत्यक्षं मम मैथिलि ।
प्रीतिं जनय मे वत्से
दिव्यालङ्कारशोभिता ॥
O princess of Mithilā, would you please adorn yourself with all these here! My dear child, give me the pleasure of seeing you radiate with the splendid make up.
2.119.12
सा तथा समलङ्कृत्य
सीता सुरसुतोपमा ।
प्रणम्य शिरसा तस्यै
रामं त्वभिमुखी ययौ ॥
Then, she, Seetā, adorning herself well and looking like an offspring of Dēvas, bowed her head and offered Pramānas to her (Anasūyā). Then she went towards Rāma.
2.119.13
तथा तु भूषितां सीतां
ददर्श वदतां वरः ।
राघवः प्रीतिदानेन
तपस्विन्या जहर्ष च ॥
Rāghava, the best among men of eloquence, saw Seetā who was thus adorned and enjoyed the gifts given out of love by the Tapasvini.
2.119.14
न्यवेदयत्ततस्सर्वं
सीता रामाय मैथिली ।
प्रीतिदानं तपस्विन्या
वसनाभरणस्रजम् ॥
Seetā, the princess of Mithilā, then showed Rāma all the dresses, ornaments and garlands given by the Tapasvini in all affection.
2.119.15
प्रहृष्टस्त्वभवद्रामो
लक्ष्मणश्च महारथः ।
मैथिल्यास्सत्क्रियां दृष्ट्वा
मानुषेषु सुदुर्लभाम् ॥
Rāma and also Lakshmaṇa, the great charioter, felt very happy seeing the honor received by the princess of Mithilā, a very difficult achievement for any ordinary human being.
2.119.16
ततस्तां शर्वरीं प्रीतः
पुण्यां शशिनिभाननः ।
अर्चितस्तापसैस्सिद्धैः
उवास रघुनन्दनः ॥
The delight of Raghus, his face glowing like the moon, stayed back there for the night receiving hospitality from the Tapasvis and Siddhas.
2.119.17
तस्यां रात्र्यां व्यतीतायाम्
अभिषिच्य हुताग्निकान् ।
आपृच्छेतां नरव्याघ्रौ
तापसान्वनगोचरान् ॥
After the night passed, those tigers among men took bath and took leave of the Vana-dwelling Tapasvis, who had just completed their Hōmas.
2.119.18
तावूचुस्ते वनचराः
तापसा धर्मचारिणः ।
वनस्य तस्य सञ्चारं
राक्षसैस्समभिप्लुतम् ॥
The Vana-dwelling Tapasvis, who followed Dharma, warned both of them that movement in the Vana could be full of dangers from Rākshasas.
2.119.19
रक्षांसि पुरुषादानि
नानारूपाणि राघव ।
वसन्त्यस्मिन्महारण्ये
व्यालाश्च रुधिराशनाः ॥
O Rāghava! Rākshasas of many forms who eat humans and many wild beasts that suck blood live in this dense forest.
2.119.20
उच्छिष्टं वा प्रमत्तं वा
तापसं धर्मचारिणम् ।
अदन्त्यस्मिन्महारण्ये
तान्निवारय राघव ॥
They eat the Tapasvis who follow Dharma, whenever they are not vigilant or whenever they do not clean themselves after taking food. May you be aware!
2.119.21
एष पन्था महर्षीणां
फलान्याहरतां वने ।
अनेन तु वनं दुर्गं
गन्तुं राघव ते क्षमम् ॥
This route, used by Maharshis to gather fruit, would be the best for you, in this dense Vana.
2.119.22
इतीव तैः प्राञ्जलिभिस्तपस्विभि-
र्द्विजैः कृतस्वस्त्ययनः परन्तपः ।
वनं सभार्यः प्रविवेश राघव-
स्सलक्ष्मणस्सूर्य इवाभ्रमण्डलम् ॥
Thus, with the Brāhmaṇas, the Tapasvis blessing, with palms joined in reverence, Rāghava, the scorcher of foes, entered the Vana like the sun over a cloudy sky, along with his wife and Lakshmaṇa.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये ।
चतुर्विंशत्सहस्रिकायां संहितयां
अयोध्याकाण्डे एकोनविंशत्युत्तरशततमस्सर्गः ॥
Thus concludes the one hundred and nineteenth Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, consisting of twenty four thousand Ṡlōkas, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
