Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 76
In this short Sarga, Vasishṭha asks Bharata to regain his composure and perform the funeral rites for Daṡaratha as per the custom. Daṡaratha's body is taken to the funeral ground in a palanquin and consigned to the fire. The king's women also reach the funeral ground in various carriages.
After offering water libations at river Sarayu, everyone, including Bharata, ministers, Purōhitas and the king's women, return to Ayōdhyā and mourn for ten days, sleeping on the bare ground.
2.76.1
तमेवं शोकसन्तप्तम्
भरतं कैकयी सुतम् ।
उवाच वदतां श्रेष्ठो
वसिष्ठश्श्रेष्ठवागृषिः ॥
Vasishṭha, the Ṛshi, of speech par excellence, and the best among conversationalists, then said to Bharata, the son of Kaikēyee, who was scorched by grief:
2.76.2
अलं शोकेन भद्रं ते
राजपुत्र महायशः ।
प्राप्तकालं नरपतेः
कुरु संयानमुत्तमम् ॥
O prince, may all bode well for you. You must keep aside this grief and perform the last rites for the King that are now due and that will lead him to the worlds of the blessed!
2.76.3
वसिष्ठस्य वचः श्रुत्वा
भरतो धारणां गतः ।
प्रेतकार्याणि सर्वाणि
कारयामास धर्मवित् ॥
Bharata, who knew Dharma, heard the words of Vasishṭha, gained his composure and had all the funeral rites performed.
2.76.4
उद्धृतं तैलसंरोधात्
सतु भूमौ निवेशितम् ।
आपीतवर्णवदनम्
प्रसुप्तमिव भूमिपम् ।
संवेश्य शयने चाग्य्रे
नानारत्नपरिष्कृते ।
ततो दशरथं पुत्रो
विललाप सुदुःखितः ॥
The body of the king was taken out of the oil tub and was laid on the ground. His face looked pale yellow and he seemed as if he was sleeping. His body was then placed on a magnificent couch adorned with every kind of gem. The son then mourned the death of Daṡaratha with extreme grief.
2.76.6
किं ते व्यवसितं राजन्
प्रोषिते मय्यनागते ।
विवास्य रामं धर्मज्ञम्
लक्ष्मणं च महाबलम् ॥
Why did you decide, O King, to exile Rāma who knows Dharma and Lakshmaṇa who is mighty strong, while I was far away from home?
2.76.7
क्व यास्यसि महाराज
हित्वेमं दुःखितं जनम् ।
हीनं पुरुषसिंहेन
रामेणाक्लिष्टकर्मणा ॥
Where have you gone, O great King, abandoning these grieving people, who are already bereft of the irreproachable Rāma, that lion among men ?
2.76.8
योगक्षेमं तु ते राजन्
कोऽस्मिन्कल्पयिता पुरे ।
त्वयि प्रयाते स्वस्तात
रामे च वनमाश्रिते ॥
With you gone to Heaven, and Rāma in the forest, who will care for the welfare and safety of this city?
2.76.9
विधवा पृथिवी राजन्
स्त्वया हीना न राजते ।
हीनचन्द्रेव रजनी
नगरी प्रतिभाति मा ॥
The widowed earth, bereaved of you, O King, is lusterless; the city looks to me like the sky bereft of the moon.
2.76.10
एवं विलपमानं तम्
भरतं दीनमानसम् ।
अब्रवीद्वचनं भूयो
वसिष्ठस्तु महामुनिः ॥
As he, Bharata, thus lamented, Vasishṭha, the Mahā Muni, again told that dejected one:
2.76.11
प्रेतकार्याणि यान्यस्य
कर्तव्यानि विशां पतेः ।
तान्यव्यग्रं महाबाहो
क्रियान्तामविचारितम् ॥
O you of mighty arm! the funeral rites that are due for the King should be performed by you without giving in to any other thought and with all diligence.
2.76.12
तथेति भरतो वाक्यम्
वसिष्ठस्याभिपूज्य तत् ।
ऋत्विक्पुरोहिताचार्यान्
स्त्वरयामास सर्वशः ॥
Giving due respect to his words, Bharata said, 'It shall be done,' and he hurried the Ṛtviks, the Purōhitas and the gurus.
2.76.13
ये त्वग्नयो नरेन्द्रेस्य
चाग्न्यगाराद्बहिष्कृताः ।
ऋत्विग्भिर्याजकैश्चैव
तेऽह्रियन्त यथाविधि ॥
The sacred fires tended by the great King, which had been brought out of the fire-chamber, were received by Ṛtviks and their assistants in a ceremonial manner.
2.76.14
शिबिकायामथारोप्य
राजानं गतचेतसम् ।
बाष्पकण्ठा विमनसः
तमूहुः परिचारकाः ॥
Thereafter the attendants, disconsolate and throats choked with tears, raised the body of the deceased king onto a palanquin, and bore it away.
2.76.15
हिरण्यं च सुवर्णं च
वासांसि विविधानि च ।
प्रकिरन्तो जना मार्गम्
नृपतेरग्रतो ययुः ॥
People went ahead of the King along the way scattering gold, silver and different kinds of garments.
2.76.16
चन्दनागरुनिर्यासान्
सरलं पद्मकं तथा ।
देवदारूणि चाहृत्य
क्षेपयन्ति तथापरे ।
गन्धानुच्चावचांश्चान्याम्
स्तत्र गत्वाथ भूमिपम् ।
तत्र संवेशयामासुः
चितामध्ये तमृत्विजः ॥
Others brought sandal, Agaru and the resin of Balsa tree and woods of Sarala, Padmaka and Dēvadāru and built a pyre and strewed many kinds of fragrant substances on it. Thereafter, the priests laid the body of the king at the centre of the pyre.
2.76.18
तदा हुताशनं हुत्वा
जेपुस्तस्य तदृत्विजः ।
जगुश्च ते यथाशास्त्र
तत्र सामानि सामगाः ॥
Then having lit the fire, the priests chanted mantras. Singers of Sāma Vēda sang Sāmas as per the Ṡastras.
2.76.19
शिबिकाभिश्च यानैश्च
यथार्हं तस्य योषितः ।
नगरान्निर्ययुस्तत्र
वृद्धैः परिवृतास्तदा ॥
By palanquins and other suitable carriages, his women came out of the city escorted by elders.
2.76.20
प्रसव्यं चापि तं चकुः
ऋत्विजोऽग्निचितं नृपम् ।
स्त्रियश्च शोकसन्तप्ताः
कौसल्याप्रमुखास्तदा ॥
Then the priests circumambulated anti-clockwise, the king's pyre that had been lit. So did the grief-stricken Kousalyā and other women.
2.76.21
क्रौञ्चीनामिव नारीणाम्
निनादस्तत्र शुश्रुवे ।
आर्तानां करुणं काले
क्रोशन्तीनां सहस्रशः ॥
Then rose the piteous wailing of thousands of women harrowed by grief, like the eerie sounds of the female Krounca birds.
2.76.22
ततो रुदन्त्यो विवशा
विलप्य च पुनः पुनः ।
यानेभ्यस्सरयूतीरम्
अवतेरुर्वराङ्गनाः ॥
Thereafter, the women weeping and lamenting endlessly and uncontrollably, reached the banks of river Sarayu and alighted from their carriages.
2.76.23
कृत्वोदकं ते भरतेन सार्धं
नृपाङ्गना मन्त्रिपुरोहिता श्च ।
पुरंप्रविश्याश्रुपरीतनेत्राः
भूमौ दशाहं व्यनयन्त दुःखम् ॥
The King's women, having offered water libations, along with Bharata, ministers and Purōhitas, entered the city with their eyes overflowing with tears. They mourned for ten days, sleeping on bare ground.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे षटसप्ततितमस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the seventy sixth Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
