Yuddha Kaanda - Sarga 16
In this Sarga, Rāvaṇa rebukes Vibheeshaṇa, saying that it is only natural for siblings and cousins to feel jealous about one who has amassed wealth, gained prominence and is receiving acclaim from everyone in the world. He accuses Vibheeshaṇa of not deserving the affection that he bestowed on him thus far.
Vibheeshaṇa then leaves, along with four other Rākshasas, telling his brother that it is impossible to impress upon one who is caught in the noose of destiny and whose end is fast approaching.
6.16.1
सुनिविष्टं हितं वाक्यम्
उक्तवन्तं विभीषणम् ।
अब्रवीत् परुषं वाक्यम्
रावणः कालचोदितः ॥
Rāvaṇa, goaded by destiny, responded harshly to Vibheeshaṇa who held his ground firmly and spoke what was in the best interest (of Rāvaṇa).
6.16.2
वसेत् सह सपत्नेन
क्रुद्धेनाशीविषेण वा ।
न तु मित्रप्रवादेन
संवसेच्छत्रुणा सह ॥
One might live with a declared enemy or with an angry venomous serpent, but not with an enemy who professes friendship.
6.16.3
जानामि शीलं ज्ञातीनाम्
सर्वलोकेषु राक्षस ।
हृष्यन्ति व्यसनेष्वेते
ज्ञातीनां ज्ञातयः सदा ॥
I sure know, O Rākshasa, how siblings and cousins fare in this world! They, the siblings and cousins, always rejoice when one suffers!
6.16.4
प्रधानं साधकं वैद्यम्
धर्मशीलं च राक्षस ।
ज्ञातयो ह्यवमन्यन्ते
शूरं परिभवन्ति च ॥
O Rākshasa! Siblings and cousins degrade and insult (out of jealousy) anyone who is capable, learned, valiant, adheres to Dharma and gains prominence.
6.16.5
नित्यमन्योन्यसंहृष्टा
व्यसनेष्वाततायिनः ।
प्रच्छन्नहृदया घोरा
ज्ञातयस्तु भयावहाः ॥
Siblings and cousins, who throw hurdles in the path of someone, and who rejoice among themselves when someone is caught in adversity, all the while hiding their true intentions, are terrible and to be dreaded.
6.16.6
श्रूयन्ते हस्तिभिर्गीताः
श्लोकाः पद्मवने क्वचित् ।
पाशहस्तान्नरान् दृष्ट्वा
शृणु तान् गदतो मम ॥
It is said that the elephants in a lotus Vana uttered these verses, on seeing men armed with the noose; listen, I will recite them to you.
6.16.7
नाग्निर्नान्यानि शस्त्राणि
न नः पाशा भयावहाः ।
घोराः स्वार्थप्रयुक्तास्तु
ज्ञातयो नो भयावहाः ॥
We have no fear from fire or weapons or noose; the only ones whom we dread are our terrible and selfish siblings and cousins.
6.16.8
उपायमेते वक्ष्यन्ति
ग्रहणे नात्र संशयः ।
कृत्स्नाद्भयाज्ज्ञातिभयम्
सुकष्टं विदितं च नः ॥
For it is these siblings and cousins that give the tips on how we can be taken captive. Danger from siblings and cousins for us is the worst and is the most-known of all dangers.
6.16.9
विद्यते गोषु सम्पन्नम्
विद्यते ब्रह्मणे दमः ।
विद्यते स्त्रीषु चापल्यम्
विद्यते ज्ञातितो भयम् ॥
Fear from siblings and cousins is as natural as wealth is in cows, self-control in Brāhmaṇas and fickleness in women.
6.16.10
ततो नेष्टमिदं सौम्य
यदहं लोकसत्कृतः ।
ऐश्वर्यमभिजातश्च
ज्ञातीनां मूर्ध्न्यवस्थितः ॥
You do not like me, my dear, because I receive great acclaim in this world, have amassed a lot of wealth and tower among all siblings and cousins.
6.16.11
यथा पुष्करपर्णेषु
पतितास्तोयबिन्दवः ।
न श्लोषमुपगच्छन्ति
तथाऽनार्येषु सौहृदम् ॥
Affection does not stick to an ignoble one, any more than water drops that fall on the lotus leaves do.
6.16.12
यथा शरदि मेघानाम्
सिञ्चतामपि गर्जताम् ।
न भवत्यम्बुसङ्क्लेदः
तथाऽनार्येषु सौहृदम् ॥
An ignoble one cannot be soaked in affection, any more than the earth can in the showers of the thundering post-monsoon clouds.
6.16.13
यथा मधुकरस्तर्षात्
रसं विन्द्रन्न तिष्ठति ।
तथा त्वमपि तत्रैव
तथाऽनार्येषु सौहृदम् ॥
You are like a bee that does not stay (with the flower any longer) after enjoying the nectar that it loves. Such is the fate of affection bestowed on the ignoble!
6.16.14
यथा पूर्वं गजः स्नात्वा
गृह्य हस्तेन वै रजः ।
दूषयत्यात्मनो देहम्
तथाऽनार्येषु सौहृदम् ॥
Affection bestowed on the ignoble goes in vain, like the bath taken by an elephant, for it immediately grabs dirt with its trunk and fouls its body again.
6.16.15
अन्यस्त्वेवंविधं ब्रूयात्
वाक्यमेतन्निशाचर ।
अस्मिन् मुहूर्ते न भवेत्
त्वां तु धिक् कुलपांसनम् ॥
O Night-rover! If anyone else had said the kind of words you did, he would have not been alive in this hour! Dhik on you, a threat to the family!
6.16.16
इत्युक्तः परुषं वाक्यम्
न्यायवादी विभीषणः ।
उत्पपात गदापाणिः
चतुर्भिः सह राक्षसैः ॥
On hearing those harsh words, Vibheeshaṇa, who spoke for what was just, sprang up (into the air) with club in hand, along with four other Rākshasas.
6.16.17
अब्रवीच्च तदा वाक्यम्
जातक्रोधो विभीषणः ।
अन्तरिक्षगतः श्रीमान्
भ्रातरं राक्षसाधिपम् ॥
The blessed Vibheeshaṇa rose into the air, and spoke these words to his brother, the king of Rākshasas, in exasperation.
6.16.18
स त्वं भ्राताऽसि मे राजन्
ब्रूहि मां यद्यदिच्छसि ।
ज्येष्ठो मान्यः पितृसमो
न च धर्मपथे स्थितः ।
इदं तु परुषं वाक्यम्
न क्षमाम्यनृतं तव ॥
O King! You are my brother, you may say to me whatever you like. An elder brother, even the one who strays from the path of Dharma, deserves the respect that a father does. But I cannot tolerate these harsh words from you, my elder bother.
6.16.19
सुनीतं हितकामेन
वाक्यमुक्तं दशानन ।
न गृह्णन्त्यकृतात्मानः
कालस्य वशमागताः ॥
Swayed by destiny, O Ten-faced, those who lack clarity in their minds do not heed the well-considered advice given by their well-wishers.
6.16.20
सुलभाः पुरुषा राजन्
सततं प्रियवादिनः ।
अप्रियस्य तु पथ्यस्य
वक्ता श्रोता च दुर्लभः ॥
It is easy to find, O king, people who always talk sweetly! But when it comes to bitter truths that are not so pleasing to the ears, it is hard to find one who would venture to say it and one who would be willing to listen.
Back in Araṇya Kāṇḍa (Ṡlōka 3.37.2), Māreeca says exactly the same words to Rāvaṇa.
6.16.21
बद्धं कालस्य पाशेन
सर्वभूतापहारिणः ।
न नश्यन्तमुपेक्षे त्वाम्
प्रदीप्तं शरणं यथा ॥
I cannot stay indifferent when you are getting caught in the noose of destiny, which can put an end to every creature, any more than one can when a house is on fire.
6.16.22
दीप्तपावकसङ्काशैः
शितैः काञ्चनभूषणैः ।
न त्वामिच्छाम्यहं द्रष्टुम्
रामेण निहतं शरैः ॥
I do not like to see you slain by Rāma’s arrows that are sharp, decked with gold and blaze like tongues of flame.
6.16.23
शूराश्च बलवन्तश्च
कृतास्त्राश्च नरा रणे ।
कालाभिपन्नाः सीदन्ति
यथा वालुकसेतवः ॥
Even men of valor, men of strength and men who are masters of weapons crumble like dykes of sand when destiny casts its evil eye on them.
6.16.24
तन्मर्षयतु यच्चोक्तम्
गुरुत्वाद्धितमिच्छता ।
आत्मानं सर्वथा रक्ष
पुरीं चेमां सराक्षसाम् ।
स्वस्ति तेऽस्तु गमिष्यामि
सुखी भव मया विना ॥
Please pardon me for saying what I said in the interest of the welfare of one who is older to me.
Please protect yourself and this city and its Rākshasas by every possible means.
I am leaving, and may all bode well for you. Be happy, I will not be here anymore (to bother you).
6.16.26
निवार्यमाणस्य मया हितैषिणा
न रोचते ते वचनं निशाचर ।
परीतकाले हि गतायुषो नरा
हितं न गृह्णन्ति सुहृद्भिरीरितम् ॥
O Night-rover, I am your well-wisher trying to dissuade you. But you do not like my advice. In times that are as eerie as these, men, whose end is fast approaching, would obviously not heed the advice given by friends.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
युद्धकाण्डे षोडशः सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the sixteenth Sarga in Yuddha Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
