Ayodhya Kaanda - Sarga 111
In this Sarga, Vasishṭha continues to persuade Rāma, saying that Rāma would not go wrong by fulfilling the wishes of everyone including himself, his brothers, his mothers, his ministers, his guilds, the assembly and the people of the city and the country. Rāma responds by saying there is no way to repay the love shown by one's father except by honoring his word.
Bharata, feeling terrible hearing those words, sits in protest on Darbha grass, and tells that he will not budge till Rāma agrees to return, and asks everyone to implore Rāma on his behalf. But everyone finds it hard to press upon Rāma, as he stands by what is right.
Bharata then says that neither did he ask his father for the kingdom, nor did he urge his mother to do so, nor did he approve of Rāma going to Vana. He further says that if it is necessary for one to stay in the Vana, he would stay in the Vana instead of Rāma.
Rāma then says to Bharata that finding a substitute to stay in the Vana in his place would be despicable. And that both he and Bharata have the responsibility to fulfill his father's word.
2.111.1
वसिष्ठस्तु तदा रामम्
उक्त्वा राजपुरोहितः ।
अब्रवीद्धर्मसंयुक्तं
पुनरेवापरं वचः ॥
Having said this to Rāma, Vasishṭha, the royal Purōhita, said again these words informed by Dharma:
2.111.2
पुरुषस्येह जातस्य
भवन्ति गुरवस्त्रयः ।
आचार्यश्चैव काकुत्स्थ
पिता माता च राघव ॥
There are three gurus for any man born in this world, mother, father and teacher.
2.111.3
पिता ह्येवं जनयति
पुरुषं पुरुषर्षभ ।
प्रज्ञां ददाति चाचार्यः
तस्मात्स गुरुरुच्यते ॥
O bull among men! Parents, of course, give birth to a man. Whereas teacher gives him wisdom, and hence is called a Guru.
2.111.4
सोऽहं ते पितुराचार्यः
तव चैव परन्तप ।
मम त्वं वचनं कुर्वन्
नातिवर्तेस्सतां गतिम् ॥
I am the teacher, O scorcher of foes, both for him and for you. By following my advice, you would, in no way, be falling away from the ways of the good.
2.111.5
इमा हि ते परिषदः
श्रेणयश्च द्विजास्तथा ।
एषु तात चरन्धर्मं
नातिवर्ते स्सतां गतिम् ॥
My child! The assemblies, the guilds and the Brāhmaṇas are yours! By doing the right thing for them, you would, in no way, be falling away from the ways of the good.
2.111.6
वृद्धाया धर्मशीलाया
मातुर्नार्हस्यवर्तितुम् ।
अस्यास्तु वचनं कुर्वन्
नातिवर्तेस्सतां गतिम् ॥
You must not default in your duty to attend on your virtuous and aged mother. By obeying her word, you would, in no way, be falling away from the ways of the good.
2.111.7
भरतस्य वचः कुर्वन्
याचमानस्य राघव ।
आत्मानं नातिवर्तेस्त्वं
सत्यधर्मपराक्रम ॥
Rāma, you are a man of truthfulness, Dharma and ultimate prowess! By following the word of the beseeching Bharata, you would, in no way, violate yourself.
2.111.8
एवं मधुरमुक्तस्सन्
गुरुणा राघवस्स्वयम् ।
प्रत्युवाच समासीनं
वसिष्ठं पुरुषर्षभः ॥
Rāma, the bull among men, exhorted thus directly by the guru in pleasing words, responded to Vasishṭha who was sitting next to him:
2.111.9
यन्मातापितरौ वृत्तं
तनये कुरुतस्सदा ।
न सुप्रतिकरं तत्तु
मात्रा पित्रा च यत्कृतम् ।
यथाशक्ति प्रदानेन
स्वापनोच्छादनेन च ।
नित्यं च प्रियवादेन
तथा संवर्धनेन च ॥
Whatever the mother and father do to a son, in giving him everything they can, in putting him to bed (every night) in doting on him and in nurturing him, can never be fully repaid.
2.111.11
स हि राजा जनयिता
पिता दशरथो मम ।
आज्ञातं यन्मया तस्य
न तन्मिथ्या भविष्यति ॥
To my father, the King Daṡaratha, I owe my existence. The word I gave him cannot simply go void.
2.111.12
एवमुक्तस्तु रामेण
भरतः प्रत्यनन्तरम् ।
उवाच परमोदारः
सूतं परमदुर्मनाः ॥
When Rāma said this, the magnanimous Bharata, feeling terribly dejected, said to the charioteer who was by his side:
2.111.13
इह मे स्थण्डिले शीघ्रं
कुशानास्तर सारथे ।
आर्यं प्रत्युपवेक्ष्यामि
यावन्मे न प्रसीदति ॥
Spread Darbha grass quick here, on the ground, O charioteer! I shall sit here in protest, till the noble one takes pity on me.
2.111.14
अनाहारो निरालोको
धनहीनो यथा द्विजः ।
शेष्ये पुरस्ताच्छालाया
यावन्न प्रतियास्यति ॥
I will, like a Brāhmaṇa who has no money and no food, lie down at the entrance of this cottage, with my face covered, until he returns.
2.111.15
स तु राममवेक्षन्तं
सुमन्त्रं पेक्ष्य दुर्मनाः ।
कुशोत्तरमुपस्थाप्य
भूमावेवाऽस्तरत्स्वयम् ॥
Seeing Sumantra (hesitating and) looking at Rāma, he, the extremely dejected one, himself brought a stack of Darbha grass and spread it on the ground.
2.111.16
तमुवाच महातेजा
रामो राजर्षिसत्तमः ।
किं मां भरत कुर्वाणं
तात प्रत्युपवेक्ष्यसि ॥
Then the supremely radiant Rāma, the foremost among the Rājarshis said to him: 'My dear Bharata! What have I done, for you to protest like this?
2.111.17
ब्राह्मणो ह्येकपार्श्वेन
नरान्रोद्धुमिहार्हति ।
न तु मूर्धाभिषिक्तानां
विधिः प्रत्युपवेशने ॥
A Brāhmaṇa may lie down on his side in order to stop someone, but a consecrated one (Kshatriya) is not expected to lie down like this to coerce.
2.111.18
उत्तिष्ठ नरशार्दूल
हित्वैतद्दारुणं व्रतम् ।
पुरवर्यामितः क्षिप्रम्
अयोध्यां याहि राघव ॥
O tiger among men! Get up and give up this terrible vow! O Rāghava! Go quick to Ayōdhyā, the best of the cities!
2.111.19
आसीनस्त्वेव भरतः
पौरजानपदं जनम् ।
उवाच सर्वतः प्रेक्ष्य
किमार्यं नानुशासथ ॥
Bharata, still sitting on the ground, looked around and said to the urban and rural folk, 'Why do you not exhort the noble one (to do as I wish)?'
2.111.20
ते तमूचुर्महात्मानं
पौरजानपदा जनाः ।
काकुत्स्थमभिजानीमः
सम्यग्वदति राघवः ॥
But they, the citizens and the country folk, responded to that Mahātma saying: 'We know Kākutstha quite well. He, the scion of Raghus, speaks only what is right.'
2.111.21
एषोऽपि हि महाभागः
पितुर्वचसि तिष्ठति ।
अत एव न शक्ताः स्मो
व्यावर्तयितुमञ्जसा ॥
He, the blessed one, stands firmly by his father's word. Hence, we cannot get him to go back, in a rush.
2.111.22
तेषामाज्ञाय वचनं
रामो वचनमब्रवीत् ।
एवं निबोध वचनं
सुहृदां धर्मचक्षुषाम् ॥
Hearing what they said, Rāma said: 'Listen to what our friends, who see what is right, say.'
2.111.23
एतच्चैवोभयं श्रुत्वा
सम्यक्सम्पश्य राघव ।
उत्तिष्ठ त्वं महाबाहो
मां च स्पृश तथोदकम् ॥
Consider carefully what you heard from both of us (me and the friends). Get up, O you of the mighty arm, touch me and then water!
Touching water is a symbol of giving up fasting.
2.111.24
अथोत्थाय जलंस्पृष्ट्वा
भरतो वाक्यमब्रवीत् ।
श्रुण्वन्तु मे परिषदो
मन्त्रिण श्श्रेणयस्तथा ॥
Then Bharata got up, touched the water and said: 'May the members of the assembly, the guildsmen and the ministers hear me!'
2.111.25
न याचे पितरं राज्यं
नानुशासामि मातरम् ।
आर्यं परमधर्मज्ञं
नानुजानामि राघवम् ॥
Neither did I ask my father for the kingdom, nor did I urge my mother, nor did I give my assent to Rāghava, the noble one, the knower of Dharma (to go to the forest).
2.111.26
यदित्ववश्यं वस्तव्यं
कर्तव्यं च पितुर्वचः ।
अहमेव निवत्स्यामि
चतुर्दश समा वने ॥
If it is absolutely essential to stay (in the Vana) according to the word given by my father, I shall myself stay (in the Vana) for the period of fourteen years.
2.111.27
धर्मात्मा तस्य तथ्येन
भ्रातुर्वाक्येन विस्मितः ।
उवाच रामस्सम्प्रेक्ष्य
पौरजानपदं जनम् ॥
Astonished at those heartfelt words of his brother, Rāma, the Dharmātma, looked at the city people and country folk and said:
2.111.28
विक्रीतमाहितं क्रीतं
यत्पित्रा जीवता मम ।
न तल्लोपयितुं शक्यं
मया वा भरतेन वा ॥
Neither I nor Bharata can undermine whatever my father did while he lived, by way of sale, pledge or purchase.
What is hinted in Ṡlōka 2.107.19 is made explicit in this Ṡlōka.
In the closely knit communities of India, people promise one another many things and enter into many transactions with each other. Keeping those promises and fulfilling those transactions is the bedrock of the social existence.
But, it also happens that people die before they can fulfill the promises they made. Then the question is whether their children, who inherit the wealth, would also fulfill the promises made by the deceased or not. If they do, they bring name to their family and lineage. And such families are held in high regard.
The lineage of Ikshwāku, Kakutstha and Raghu is one such. And hence Rāma says what he says here. It is not an option for them to keep the word of his father. The well earned reputation of their family made it an obligation.
The truth of the adage that 'Rāmāyaṇa is the life blood of Indian society' lies in Ṡlōkas like this.
This is the pivotal Ṡlōka that helped an entire civilization take pride in its sense of obligation and provided the needed support when tough times put that sense to test.
A man who keeps his word and a family which honors its word earn their due reputation.
In this modern day and times, the knee jerk reaction would be 'how about women?' And the answer to that question is:
Women are understood to be emotionally fragile compared to men. Hence their character was held to a milder standard. What was asked of them was loyalty, to the father or husband or any other guardian and their families.
Not only women, even men and entire families who could not function as independent reliable social entities by themselves used to live under the shade and support of capable persons and families. They were the dependants, staff, attendants, etc., that were oft-mentioned in Rāmāyaṇa.
Sociologists who confuse them to be the equivalent of the subservient and indentured slaves of the last couple of centuries are missing the point.
2.111.29
उपधिर्न मया कार्यो
वनवासे जुगुप्सितः ।
युक्तमुक्तं च कैकेय्या
पित्रा मे सुकृतं कृतम् ॥
I shall not take the cheap route of delegating the life in Vana to a substitute. Whatever Kaikēyee asked was appropriate (for her), and whatever my father did was right (for him).
2.111.30
जानामि भरतं क्षान्तं
गुरुसत्कारकारिणम् ।
सर्वमेवात्र कल्याणं
सत्यसन्धे महात्मनि ॥
I know that Bharata is a man of forbearance and is zealous in his regard to elders. Everything will turn out well for him, for this Mahātma who is true to his word.
2.111.31
अनेन धर्मशीलेन
वनात्प्रत्यागतः पुनः ।
भ्रात्रा सह भविष्यामि
पृथिव्याः पतिरुत्तमः ॥
Returning from the forest, I shall, along with this brother of mine, who is devoted to Dharma, become a worthy ruler of the earth.
2.111.32
वृतो हि राजा कैकेय्या
मया तद्वचनं कृतम् ।
अनृतान्मोचयानेन
पितरं तं महीपतिम् ॥
The king was requested by Kaikēyee and I fulfilled that word. Relieve the king, our father, from the ignominy of not keeping his word.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अयोध्याकाण्डे एकादशोत्तरशततमस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the one hundred and eleventh Sarga in Ayōdhyā Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
