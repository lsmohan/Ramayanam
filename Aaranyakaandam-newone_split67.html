Aranya Kaanda - Sarga 67
In this Sarga, Rāma, following the inspiring words and advice of Lakshmaṇa, goes all around the Vana along with him, searching for Seetā. They then see Jaṭāyu with his body covered in blood. Rāma mistakes him to be a Rākshasa who came in the form of an eagle and who had devoured Seetā. As he is about to shoot Jaṭāyu with his arrows, Jaṭāyu announces himself and tells that he has seen Rāvaṇa carrying off Seetā. He then explains how he fought with him, breaking his chariot and killing the charioteer and how Rāvaṇa subsequently cut his wings off when he was tired. Rāma embraces Jaṭāyu and cries, showing filial love to him.
3.67.1
पूर्वजोऽप्युक्तमात्रस्तु
लक्ष्मणेन सुभाषितम् ।
सारग्राही महासारम्
प्रतिजग्राह राघवः ॥
Even though he was the elder of the two, Rāghava who could grasp the essence with ease, accepted the wholesome and well spoken words of Lakshmaṇa right away.
3.67.2
सन्निगृह्य महाबाहुः
प्रवृत्तं कोपमात्मनः ।
अवष्टभ्य धनुश्चित्रम्
रामो लक्ष्मणमब्रवीत् ॥
Restraining his swelling anger, Rāma of the mighty arm said to Lakshmaṇa who stood leaning on his splendid bow:
3.67.3
किं करिष्यावहे वत्स
क्व वा गच्छाव लक्ष्मण ।
केनोपायेन पश्येयम्
सीतामिति विचिन्तय ॥
O Lakshmaṇa, my dear, what shall we do, where shall we go, how shall we find Seetā? please think it over!
3.67.4
तं तथा परितापार्तम्
लक्ष्मणो राममब्रवीत् ।
इदमेव जनस्थानम्
त्वमन्वेषितुमर्हसि ।
राक्षसैर्बहुभिः कीर्णम्
नानाद्रुमलतायुतम् ॥
Lakshmaṇa then said to Rāma who was pitifully in distress: ‘You should search right here in Janasthāna, which is full of Rākshasas and trees and creepers of every kind.’
3.67.5
सन्तीह गिरिदुर्गाणि
निर्दराः कन्दराणि च ।
गुहाश्च विविधा घोरा
नानामृगगणाकुलाः ।
आवासाः किन्नराणां च
गन्धर्वभवनानि च ॥
There are many mountain peaks, valleys, dreadful caves and crevices in which dwell all kinds of beasts. There are also the dwellings of Kinnaras and mansions of Gandharvas.
3.67.7
तानि युक्तो मया सार्धम्
त्वमन्वेषितुमर्हसि ।
त्वद्विधा बुद्धिसम्पन्ना
महात्मानो नरर्षभ ।
आपत्सु न प्रकम्पन्ते
वायुवेगैरिवाचलाः ॥
You, aided by me, should make a thorough search. Mahātmas and men of excellent intellect like you are no more shaken by calamities, however great, O bull among men, than mountains by high winds.
3.67.8
इत्युक्तस्तद्वनं सर्वम्
विचचार सलक्ष्मणः ।
क्रुद्धो रामश्शरं घोरम्
सन्धाय धनुषि क्षुरम् ॥
Thus advised, Rāma, in his anger, mounted a dreaded crescent-headed arrow on the bow and went around the entire Vana along with Lakshmaṇa.
3.67.9
ततः पर्वतकूटाभम्
महाभागं द्विजोत्तमम् ।
ददर्श पतितं भूमौ
क्षतजार्द्रं जटायुषम् ॥
Then he saw the blessed Jaṭāyu, the eminent bird, who was huge like a mountain crest fallen on the ground soaked in blood.
3.67.10
तं दृष्ट्वा गिरिशृङ्गाभम्
रामो लक्ष्मणमब्रवीत् ।
अनेन सीता वैदेही
भक्षिता नात्र संशयः ॥
Seeing him, who was like a mountain crest, Rāma said to Lakshmaṇa: ‘Without a doubt he is the one who devoured Vaidēhee.’
3.67.11
गृध्ररूपमिदं रक्षो
व्यक्तं भवति कानने ।
भक्षयित्वा विशालाक्षीम्
आस्ते सीतां यथासुखम् ।
एनं वधिष्ये दीप्तास्यैः
घोरैर्बाणैरजिह्मगैः ॥
This, clearly, is a Rākshasa in the guise of an eagle that roams the forest. Having devoured Seetā of wide eyes, he is lying here happily. I shall kill him with the terrible straight-flying arrows with blazing tips!
3.67.13
इत्युक्त्वाभ्यपतद्गृध्रम्
सन्धाय धनुषि क्षुरम् ।
क्रुद्धो रामस्समुद्रान्ताम्
कम्पयन्निव मेदिनीम् ॥
Saying this, the angry Rāma dashed to the eagle, mounting a crescent-headed arrow on the bow, as if he would shake the earth that extends to the ocean’s edge.
3.67.14
तं दीनं दीनया वाचा
सफेनं रुधिरं वमन् ।
अभ्यभाषत पक्षी तु
रामं दशरथात्मजम् ॥
But the bird addressed Rāma, son of Daṡaratha who was feeling miserable, in a feeble voice, vomiting frothy blood as he did so:
3.67.15
यामोषधिमिवायुष्मन्
अन्वेषसि महावने ।
सा देवी मम च प्राणा
रावणेनोभयं हृतम् ॥
O you of long life! Both the lady for whom you are searching, like for a rare herb, in this great Vana and my life were snatched away by Rāvaṇa.
3.67.16
त्वया विरहिता देवी
लक्ष्मणेन च राघव ।
ह्रियमाणा मया दृष्टा
रावणेन बलीयसा ॥
I saw the lady being carried off by the powerful Rāvaṇa when you and Lakshmaṇa were away, O Rāghava!
3.67.17
सीतामभ्यवपन्नोऽहम्
रावणश्च रणे मया ।
विध्वंसितरथश्चात्र
पातितो धरणीतले ॥
Then I went to her and fought with Rāvaṇa, shattering his chariot and hurling him down on the ground.
3.67.18
एतदस्य धनुर्भग्नम्
एतदस्य शरावरम् ।
अयमस्य रथो राम
भग्नसाङ्ग्रामिको मया ॥
This is the broken bow and this, his armor. This, O Rāma, is the war-chariot that was shivered by me.
3.67.19
अयं तु सारथिस्तस्य
मत्पक्षनिहतो युधि ।
परिश्रान्तस्य मे पक्षौ
छित्त्वा खड्गेन रावणः ।
सीतामादाय वैदेहीम्
उत्पपात विहायसम् ।
रक्षसा निहतं पूर्वम्
न मां हन्तुं त्वमर्हसि ॥
This is his charioteer, who was killed by a blow from my wing. Cutting down my wings with his sword when I was tired and exhausted, Rāvaṇa rose into the sky, carrying Vaidēhee with him.
The Rākshasa has already killed me; don’t you hit me too.
3.67.21
रामस्तस्य तु विज्ञाय
बाष्पपूर्णमुखस्तदा ।
द्विगुणीकृततापार्तः
सीतासक्तां प्रियां कथाम् ॥
Hearing from him the news of Seetā that he was longing to hear, Rāma felt doubly distressed, his face filled with tears.
3.67.22
गृध्रराजं परिष्वज्य
परित्यज्य महद्धनुः ।
निपपातावशो भूमौ
रुरोद सहलक्ष्मणः ॥
Throwing away the great bow and embracing the king of eagles, he fell on the ground unable to hold himself and cried bitterly along with Lakshmaṇa.
3.67.23
एकमेकायने कृच्छ्रे
निश्श्वसन्तं कथञ्चन ।
समीक्ष्य दुःखिततरो
रामस्सौमित्रिमब्रवीत् ॥
On seeing the bird that lay alone managing to gasp somehow as the breath passed only in one direction, Rāma, his grief aggravated, said to Sowmitri:
‘Breath passed in only one direction’ means ‘able to exhale but not inhale’.
3.67.24
राज्यं भ्रष्टं वने वासः
सीता नष्टा हतो द्विजः ।
ईदृशीयं ममालक्ष्मीः
निर्दहेदपि पावकम् ॥
Kingdom gone, Vana for residence, Seetā lost and the bird killed; a misfortune like this could burn out even the fire.
3.67.25
सम्पूर्णमपि चेदद्य
प्रविशेयं महोदधिम् ।
सोऽपि नूनं ममालक्ष्म्या
विशुष्येत्सरितांपतिः ॥
Even the great ocean, the recourse of all rivers, which is full (with water) will dry up without a doubt, if I enter it, because I carry ill-luck (with me).
3.67.26
नास्त्यभाग्यतरो लोके
मत्तोऽस्मिन्सचराचरे ।
येनेयं महती प्राप्ता
मया व्यसनवागुरा ॥
There is none in this world, among the mobile and the stationary who is more unfortunate than myself, caught in this huge net of sorrows.
Mobile refers to the animal kingdom, planets, etc. that move and stationary refers to the plant kingdom, mountains, etc., which do not move.
3.67.27
अयं पितृवयस्यो मे
गृध्रराजो जरान्वितः ।
शेते विनिहतो भूमौ
मम भाग्यविपर्ययात् ॥
It is due to my luck turning upside down that this king of eagles, a good friend of my father, ripe in age lies on the ground, struck down.
3.67.28
इत्येवमुक्त्वा बहुशो
राघवस्सहलक्ष्मणः ।
जटायुषं च पस्पर्श
पितृस्नेहं विदर्शयन् ॥
Saying many a word like this, Rāghava along with Lakshmaṇa stroked Jaṭāyu with his hands, showing filial love.
3.67.29
निकृत्तपक्षं रुधिरावसिक्तं
स गृध्रराजं परिरभ्य रामः ।
क्व मैथिली प्राणसमा ममेति
विमुच्य वाचं निपपात भूमौ ॥
Embracing the King of eagles, who was covered with blood and whose wings had been cut off, Rāghava fell on the ground, while saying ‘Where is Maithili, who is like my own life!’
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
अरण्यकाण्डे सप्तषष्टितमस्सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the sixty seventh Sarga in Araṇya Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
