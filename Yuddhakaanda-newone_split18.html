Yuddha Kaanda - Sarga 18
In this Sarga, Rāma says, regarding Vibheeshaṇa, that it is not right to reject someone who has come seeking friendship. Sugreeva cautions that someone who deserted his own brother could desert anyone. Rāma, though he agrees with Sugreeva on that observation, sees some subtlety in the situation, because of which he should be given refuge. Rāma points out that fraternal rivalry is not uncommon in this world, and Vibheeshaṇa could obviously be seeking the kingdom for himself. More than anything, he says, that he has taken a vow to free anyone, who sought refuge in him, from fear. Sugreeva concedes and concurs with Rāma.
6.18.1
अथ रामः प्रसन्नात्मा
श्रुत्वा वायुसुतस्य ह ।
प्रत्यभाषत दुर्धर्षः
श्रुतवानात्मनि स्थितम् ॥
Having heard the views of the son of Vāyu the invincible Rāma of pleasant disposition, having deep knowledge of Ṡastras, spoke out what was on his mind.
6.18.2
ममापि तु विवक्षाऽस्ति
काचित्प्रति विभीषणम् ।
श्रोतुमिच्छामि तत्सर्वम्
भवद्भिः श्रेयसि स्थितैः ॥
I too have something to say regarding the matter of Vibheeshaṇa. I would like you, who are firmly interested in my welfare, to hear it all.
6.18.3
मित्रभावेन सम्प्राप्तम्
न त्यजेयं कथञ्चन ।
दोषो यद्यपि तस्य स्यात्
सतामेतदगर्हितम् ॥
This man, who arrived professing friendship, can by no means be rejected, even if there is something with him to be concerned about. This stance will not be condemned by good men.
6.18.4
सुग्रीवस्त्वथ तद्वाक्यम्
आभाष्य च विमृश्य च ।
ततः शुभतरं वाक्यम्
उवाच हरिपुङ्गवः ॥
Thereupon, Sugreeva, a bull among Vānaras, pondered over those words and interpreting them well, raised an even more important point:
6.18.5
सुदुष्टो वाप्यदुष्टो वा
किमेष रजनीचरः ।
ईदृशं व्यसनं प्राप्तम्
भ्रातरं यः परित्यजेत् ।
को नाम स भवेत्तस्य
यमेष न परित्यजेत् ॥
How does it matter whether this Night-rover is extremely bad or not bad at all? When he could desert like this a brother who found himself in trouble, would there be anyone whom he would not desert?
6.18.6
वानराधिपतेर्वाक्यम्
श्रुत्वा सर्वानुदीक्ष्य च ।
ईषदुत्स्मयमानस्तु
लक्ष्मणं पुण्यलक्षणम् ।
इति होवाच काकुत्स्थो
वाक्यं सत्यपराक्रमः ॥
On hearing those words of the Lord of Vānaras, Kākutstha of true prowess looked around at everyone, and then turning to Lakshmaṇa of auspicious marks, said these words, smiling:
6.18.8
अनधीत्य च शास्त्राणि
वृद्धाननुपसेव्य च ।
न शक्यमीदृशं वक्तुम्
यदुवाच हरीश्वरः ॥
It is not possible for anyone who had not studied Ṡastras and who had not waited upon the elders, to speak like the Lord of Vānaras did.
6.18.9
अस्ति सूक्ष्मतरं किञ्चित्
यदत्र प्रतिभाति मे ।
प्रत्यक्षं लौकिकं वापि
विद्यते सर्वराजसु ॥
I see something very subtle in here, which is very much evident in general in this world among all kings.
6.18.10
अमित्रास्तत्कुलीनाश्च
प्रातिदेश्याश्च कीर्तिताः ।
व्यसनेषु प्रहर्तारः
तस्मादयमिहागतः ॥
Kinsmen and neighboring (kings) are said to become enemies and strike when someone is beset by troubles. That explains why he is here.
6.18.11
अपापास्तत्कुलीनाश्च
मानयन्ति स्वकान् हितान् ।
एष प्रायो नरेन्द्राणाम्
शङ्कनीयस्तु शोभनः ॥
However, kinsmen who are not evil do hold their near and dear in great regard. But anyone, even a good person, is suspected by kings in general.
6.18.12
यस्तु दोषस्त्वया प्रोक्तो
ह्यादानेऽरिबलस्य च ।
तत्र ते कीर्तयिष्यामि
यथाशास्त्रमिदं शृणु ॥
Let me tell you what Ṡastras say regarding the issues you raised in accepting someone from the enemy forces; please listen:
6.18.13
न वयं तत्कुलीनाश्च
राज्यकाङ्क्षी च राक्षसः ।
पण्डिता हि भविष्यन्ति
तस्माद्ग्राह्यो विभीषणः ॥
We are not of his clan (and hence there is no fraternal rivalry with us). And the Rākshasa surely wants the kingdom. There are shrewd men (like him everywhere). So we should accept Vibheeshaṇa.
6.18.14
अव्यग्राश्च प्रहृष्टाश्च
न भविष्यन्ति सङ्गताः ।
प्रवादश्च महानेष
ततोऽस्य भयमागतम् ।
इति भेदं गमिष्यन्ति
तस्माद्ग्राह्यो विभीषणः ॥
(Kinsmen) are not free of fear from each other, nor happy with each other, nor mix freely with each other. He who is speaking out so loud and clear, is certainly gripped with fear. And there is a clear fall out (between the kinsmen) and hence Vibheeshaṇa should be accepted.
6.18.15
न सर्वे भ्रातरस्तात
भवन्ति भरतोपमाः ।
मद्विधा वा पितुः पुत्राः
सुहृदो वा भवद्विधाः ॥
My dear! Not all brothers are like Bharata, nor all sons like I am, towards their father, nor all friends like you!
6.18.16
एवमुक्तस्तु रामेण
सुग्रीवः सहलक्ष्मणः ।
उत्थायेदं महाप्राज्ञः
प्रणतो वाक्यमब्रवीत् ॥
Told thus by Rāma, the extremely sagacious Sugreeva stood up along with Lakshmaṇa, and said these words, saluting him:
6.18.17
रावणेन प्रणिहितम्
तमवेहि विभीषणम् ।
तस्याहं निग्रहं मन्ये
क्षमं क्षमवतां वर ॥
Please note that Vibheeshaṇa was sent by Rāvaṇa. And hence, O foremost of the forgivers, I think he should be restrained.
6.18.18
राक्षसो जिह्मया बुद्ध्या
सन्दिष्टोऽयमिहागतः ।
प्रहर्तुं त्वयि विश्वस्ते
प्रच्छन्नो मयि वाऽनघ ।
लक्ष्मणे वा महाबाहो
स वध्यः सचिवैः सह ।
रावणस्य नृशंसस्य
भ्राता ह्येष विभीषणः ॥
This Rākshasa who has come here was sent with nefarious motives, to strike you in secret after gaining your confidence, or me or Lakshmaṇa of mighty arm.
He should be killed along with his cohorts. After all, this Vibheeshaṇa is none other than the brother of cruel Rāvaṇa.
6.18.20
एवमुक्त्वा रघुश्रेष्ठम्
सुग्रीवो वाहिनीपतिः ।
वाक्यज्ञो वाक्यकुशलम्
ततो मौनमुपागमत् ॥
Sugreeva, the Commander-in-Chief of the army, who was good at his choice of words said this to that foremost of the Raghus, who was also skilled at words, and stayed silent.
6.18.21
सुग्रीवस्य तु तद्वाक्यम्
रामः श्रुत्वा विमृश्य च ।
ततः शुभतरं वाक्यम्
उवाच हरिपुङ्गवम् ॥
Rāma listened to the words of Sugreeva and after pondering over them, offered an even better counterpoint to that bull among Vānaras.
6.18.22
सुदुष्टो वाप्यदुष्टो वा
किमेष रजनीचरः ।
सूक्ष्ममप्यहितं कर्तुम्
ममाशक्तः कथञ्चन ॥
How does it matter whether this Night-rover is utterly bad or not bad at all? He cannot do even an iota of damage to me, by any means.
6.18.23
पिशाचान् दानवान् यक्षान्
पृथिव्यां चैव राक्षसान् ।
अङ्गुल्यग्रेण तान् हन्याम्
इच्छन् हरिगणेश्वर ॥
If I want to, I could destroy, with the flip of my little finger, all Piṡācas, Dānavas, Yakshas and Rākshasas in the world, O Lord of Vānara hosts!
6.18.24
श्रूयते हि कपोतेन
शत्रुः शरणमागतः ।
अर्चितश्च यथान्यायम्
स्वैश्च मांसैर्निमन्त्रितः ॥
Is it not heard that a dove received its foe with all due honors and offered its own flesh for a meal, when the foe sought refuge in him?
6.18.25
स हि तं प्रतिजग्राह
भार्याहन्तारमागतम् ।
कपोतो वानरश्रेष्ठ
किं पुनर्मद्विधो जनः ॥
That dove accepted him even though its wife was killed by him. O best of Vānaras, why would someone like me do anything less!
6.18.26
ऋषेः कण्वस्य पुत्रेण
कण्डुना परमर्षिणा ।
शृणु गाथां पुरा गीताम्
धर्मिष्ठां सत्यवादिना ॥
Please listen to what had been said once upon a time, redolent of Dharma, by the great Ṛshi Kaṇḍu, the son of Ṛshi Kaṇva, who always stood for upholding the Truth.
6.18.27
बद्धाञ्जलिपुटं दीनम्
याचन्तं शरणागतम् ।
न हन्यादानृशंस्यार्थम्
अपि शत्रुं परन्तप ॥
O Scorcher of foe, one should be kind, and not kill even an enemy if he is in misery and seeking refuge, beseeching with his palms joined in reverence.
6.18.28
आर्तो वा यदि वा दृप्तः
परेषां शरणागतः ।
अरिः प्राणान् परित्यज्य
रक्षितव्यः कृतात्मना ॥
Even a foe, miserable or prideful, who seeks refuge in someone must be protected by any worthy person, even if it means giving up one’s own life.
6.18.29
स चेद्भयाद्वा मोहाद्वा
कामाद्वापि न रक्षति ।
स्वया शक्त्या यथान्यायम्
तत्पापं लोकगर्हितम् ॥
The sin of not protecting thus, according to one’s own power and as is appropriate, out of fear or out of poor judgment or willfully, is condemned in the world.
6.18.30
विनष्टः पश्यतस्तस्य
रक्षिणः शरणं गतः ।
आदाय सुकृतं तस्य
सर्वं गच्छेदरक्षितः ॥
One who seeks refuge, but goes unprotected and eventually perishes, will carry away all the Puṇya of the one who fails to protect him.
6.18.31
एवं दोषो महानत्र
प्रपन्नानामरक्षणे ।
अस्वर्ग्यं चायशस्यं च
बलवीर्यविनाशनम् ॥
Such would be the sin of not protecting one who seeks refuge. It would bring one infamy, debar him from Heaven and dissipate one’s strength and valor.
6.18.32
करिष्यामि यथार्थं तु
काण्डोर्वचनमुत्तमम् ।
धर्मिष्ठं च यशस्यं च
स्वर्ग्यं स्यात्तु फलोदये ॥
I will follow Kaṇḍu’s noble precept in the true spirit, since it keeps me on the path of Dharma, earns me approval of the world, and only adds to the prospect of Heaven in due course.
6.18.33
सकृदेव प्रपन्नाय
तवास्मीति च याचते ।
अभयं सर्वभूतेभ्यो
ददाम्येतद् व्रतं मम ॥
If one seeks refuge saying, ‘I am yours, once and for all’, I free him from the fear of every force and creature. And that is my vow.
6.18.34
आनयैनं हरिश्रेष्ठ
दत्तमस्याभयं मया ।
विभीषणो वा सुग्रीव
यदि वा रावणः स्वयम् ॥
Bring him here, O eminent Vānara; he has my protection, O Sugreeva, be he Vibheeshaṇa or Rāvaṇa himself.’
6.18.35
रामस्य तु वचः श्रुत्वा
सुग्रीवः प्लवगेश्वरः ।
प्रत्यभाषत काकुत्स्थम्
सौहार्देननभिपूरितः ॥
Hearing those words of Rāma, Sugreeva, the Lord of Vānaras, responded to Kākutstha with words that betrayed his deep affection:
6.18.36
किमत्र चित्रं धर्मज्ञ
लोकनाथ शिखामणे ।
यत्त्वमार्यं प्रभाषेथाः
सत्त्ववान् सत्पथे स्थितः ॥
There is no surprise, O Dharmajña, O pinnacle among all lords of men, that you, who is so powerful and so firmly established in the path of righteousness, speak so nobly!
6.18.37
मम चाप्यन्तरात्माऽयम्
शुद्धं वेत्ति विभीषणम् ।
अनुमानाच्च भावाच्च
सर्वतः सुपरीक्षितः ॥
In my heart of hearts, I too feel that this Vibheeshaṇa is a man of unsullied character, from all that is evident and can be guessed and examined.
6.18.38
तस्मात् क्षिप्रं सहास्माभिः
तुल्यो भवतु राघव ।
विभीषणो महाप्राज्ञः
सखित्वं चाभ्युपैतु नः ॥
Therefore, O Rāghava, let the sagacious Vibheeshaṇa be at once treated just like us, and let him become our friend!
6.18.39
ततस्तु सुग्रीववचो निशम्य तत्
हरीश्वरेणाभिहितं नरेश्वरः ।
विभीषणेनाशु जगाम सङ्गमं
पतत्रिराजेन यथा पुरन्दरः ॥
On hearing those words spoken by that Sugreeva, the Lord of Vānaras, (Rāma) the lord of men, met with Vibheeshaṇa, like Purandara met with (Garuḍa) the king of birds.
इत्यार्षे वाल्मीकीये
श्रीमद्रामायणे आदिकाव्ये
युद्धकाण्डे अष्टादशः सर्गः॥
Thus concludes the eighteenth Sarga in Yuddha Kāṇḍa of the glorious Rāmāyaṇa, the first ever poem of humankind, composed by Maharshi Vālmeeki.
